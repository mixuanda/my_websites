## What a sequence really is

A sequence is not merely a row of numbers written with commas. Formally, it is
a function whose input is a positive integer and whose output is a real number.
The input tells us the position of the term. The output is the value sitting at
that position.

<Definition title="Sequence of real numbers">
A sequence of real numbers is a function

$$
a:\mathbb Z^+\to \mathbb R.
$$

Instead of writing `a(n)`, we usually write `a_n`, and we denote the whole
sequence by `{a_n}`.
</Definition>

This definition is worth taking seriously. It explains why the same values in
a different order form a different sequence, and why a formula for `a_n` must
say what happens for every positive integer `n`.

<WorkedExample title="Reading a general term">
If `a_n=(-1)^n`, then

$$
a_1=-1,\quad a_2=1,\quad a_3=-1,\quad a_4=1.
$$

If `b_n=2^{n-1}`, then

$$
b_1=1,\quad b_2=2,\quad b_3=4,\quad b_4=8.
$$

Both are sequences because each positive integer `n` determines exactly one
real number.
</WorkedExample>

## Finding general terms from patterns

A finite list suggests a pattern, but the general term must describe the
`n`-th term, not just the first few terms.

<WorkedExample title="Alternating zeros and ones">
The sequence

$$
1,0,1,0,\ldots
$$

is `1` when `n` is odd and `0` when `n` is even. A compact formula is

$$
a_n=\frac{1+(-1)^{n-1}}2.
$$

When `n` is odd, `n-1` is even and the numerator is `2`. When `n` is even,
`n-1` is odd and the numerator is `0`.
</WorkedExample>

<WorkedExample title="Products of even and odd factors">
The product

$$
2\cdot4\cdot6\cdots(2n)
$$

has the `k`-th factor `2k`. Therefore

$$
2\cdot4\cdots(2n)
=\prod_{k=1}^n 2k
=2^n n!.
$$

Similarly,

$$
1\cdot3\cdot5\cdots(2n-1)
=\frac{(2n)!}{2^n n!},
$$

because `(2n)!` is the product of all factors from `1` to `2n`, and dividing
by the even part `2\cdot4\cdots(2n)=2^n n!` leaves only the odd part.
</WorkedExample>

<WorkedExample title="A sequence made from repeated differentiation">
The chapter also uses `f(x)=\sin x` to show that sequences do not have to come
from ordinary lists first. Define `a_n=f^{(n)}(0)`, where `f^{(n)}` means the
`n`-th derivative. The derivatives cycle:

$$
\sin x,\quad \cos x,\quad -\sin x,\quad -\cos x,\quad \sin x,\ldots
$$

So

$$
a_1=1,\quad a_2=0,\quad a_3=-1,\quad a_4=0,
$$

and the same four-term pattern repeats. One compact general term is

$$
a_n=\cos\left(\frac{(n-1)\pi}{2}\right).
$$

This example is important because it separates the idea of a sequence from the
idea of a simple algebraic pattern. The sequence is still a function of the
index `n`; its values are produced by a calculus operation before evaluating at
`0`.
</WorkedExample>

<CommonMistake title="A pattern is not a definition until the indexing is fixed">
Writing `2,4,8,...` is not as precise as writing `a_n=2^{n-1}`. The first term
matters: `2^n` would give `2,4,8,...` only if the indexing started at `n=1`
with first term `2`, while the chapter example `1,2,4,8,...` is
`2^{n-1}`.
</CommonMistake>

## Recursive definitions

Some sequences are easier to define by saying how to get the next term from
earlier terms.

<Definition title="Recursive sequence">
A recursive sequence is a sequence whose terms are defined using one or more
preceding terms, together with enough initial values to start the process.
</Definition>

For example,

$$
a_1=4,\qquad a_{n+1}=2a_n+1
$$

gives

$$
a_2=9,\quad a_3=19,\quad a_4=39.
$$

The recurrence determines the next term only after the earlier term is known.

For this particular recurrence, we can also find a closed formula. Add `1` to
both sides and set `b_n=a_n+1`. Then

$$
b_{n+1}=a_{n+1}+1=2a_n+2=2(a_n+1)=2b_n.
$$

Since `b_1=a_1+1=5`, the transformed sequence is geometric:

$$
b_n=5\cdot2^{n-1}.
$$

Therefore

$$
a_n=b_n-1=5\cdot2^{n-1}-1.
$$

The lesson is not that every recurrence becomes geometric. The useful habit is
to look for a change of variables that removes the constant term when the
recurrence is affine.

<TheoremCard title="Recursion theorem">
Let `X` be a set, let `b\in X`, and let `f:X\to X` be a function. Then there
is a unique function `a:\mathbb Z^+\to X` such that

$$
a(1)=b,\qquad a(n+1)=f(a(n))
$$

for every `n\in\mathbb Z^+`.
</TheoremCard>

The theorem gives the formal reason why a first term plus a rule such as
`a_{n+1}=f(a_n)` really defines a sequence. The rule never leaves `X`, because
`f` maps `X` back into `X`, and uniqueness says that there is no second
sequence satisfying the same starting value and same next-term rule.

<WorkedExample title="Turning a recurrence into theorem data">
For

$$
a_1=1,\qquad a_{n+1}=a_n^2+1,
$$

take `X=\mathbb R`, `b=1`, and `f(x)=x^2+1`. The recursion theorem says that
there is a unique real sequence satisfying this rule. The first terms are

$$
1,\quad 2,\quad 5,\quad 26,\ldots
$$

There may be no simple closed formula, but the sequence is still completely
defined.
</WorkedExample>

## Fibonacci as a first-order recursion on pairs

The Fibonacci sequence is defined by two starting values and a rule using the
previous two terms:

$$
F_1=F_2=1,\qquad F_{n+2}=F_{n+1}+F_n.
$$

To fit the recursion theorem, package two consecutive values as one ordered
pair:

$$
a(n)=(F_n,F_{n+1}).
$$

Then the next pair is obtained from the function

$$
f(x,y)=(y,x+y).
$$

So a two-term recurrence can be viewed as a one-step recurrence on the set
`\mathbb R^2`.

<TheoremCard title="Binet form of Fibonacci numbers">
Let

$$
\phi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2},
\qquad
\psi=\frac{1-\sqrt5}{2}.
$$

Then for every positive integer `n`,

$$
F_n=\frac{\phi^n-\psi^n}{\phi-\psi}.
$$
</TheoremCard>

This formula is usually proved by checking that the right-hand side has the
same first two values and satisfies the same recurrence. The essential
identities are `\phi^2=\phi+1` and `\psi^2=\psi+1`.

Here is the verification in a form that is useful for similar recurrence
questions. Define

$$
B_n=\frac{\phi^n-\psi^n}{\phi-\psi}.
$$

First,

$$
B_1=1,\qquad
B_2=\frac{\phi^2-\psi^2}{\phi-\psi}=\phi+\psi=1.
$$

Next, because each of `\phi` and `\psi` satisfies `t^2=t+1`, we have

$$
\phi^{n+2}=\phi^{n+1}+\phi^n,
\qquad
\psi^{n+2}=\psi^{n+1}+\psi^n.
$$

Subtracting the second identity from the first and dividing by
`\phi-\psi` gives

$$
B_{n+2}=B_{n+1}+B_n.
$$

Thus `{B_n}` has the same first two values and the same recursive rule as the
Fibonacci sequence. By uniqueness of the recursively defined sequence, `B_n`
must equal `F_n` for every positive integer `n`.

## Arithmetic sequences and sums

<Definition title="Arithmetic sequence">
A sequence `{a_n}` is arithmetic if there is a constant `d` such that

$$
a_{n+1}-a_n=d
$$

for every `n\in\mathbb Z^+`. The number `d` is the common difference.
</Definition>

If `a=a_1`, then the general term is

$$
a_n=a+(n-1)d.
$$

The sum of the first `n` terms is

$$
s_n=\sum_{k=1}^n a_k
=\frac n2\left[2a+(n-1)d\right]
=\frac n2(a_1+a_n).
$$

The last equality comes from pairing the first term with the last, the second
with the second-last, and so on. Every pair has the same sum `a_1+a_n`.

<WorkedExample title="Recover an arithmetic sequence from a sum">
Suppose the common difference is `d=3/2` and the sum of the first `15` terms is
`240`. Then

$$
240=\frac{15}{2}\left[2a+14\cdot\frac32\right].
$$

Solving gives `a=5.5`.

If the sum of the first `k` terms is `361`, then

$$
\frac{k}{2}\left[2(5.5)+(k-1)\frac32\right]=361.
$$

This simplifies to

$$
3k^2+19k-1444=0.
$$

The positive integer solution is `k=19`; the other root is rejected because a
number of terms cannot be negative.
</WorkedExample>

## Geometric sequences and sums

<Definition title="Geometric sequence">
A sequence `{b_n}` of nonzero numbers is geometric if there is a nonzero
constant `r` such that

$$
\frac{b_{n+1}}{b_n}=r
$$

for every `n\in\mathbb Z^+`. The number `r` is the common ratio.
</Definition>

If `b=b_1`, then

$$
b_n=br^{n-1}.
$$

For `r\ne 1`, the finite geometric sum is

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n br^{k-1}
=b\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}r^k
=b\frac{1-r^n}{1-r}
=b\frac{r^n-1}{r-1}.
$$

The proof is the subtraction trick:

$$
(1-r)(1+r+\cdots+r^{n-1})=1-r^n.
$$

If `r=1`, the sum is simply `bn`.

## Arithmetic-geometric sums

The final exercise in the source chapter mixes an arithmetic factor with a
geometric factor. Let

$$
x_k=(a+kd)br^k,\qquad k\in\mathbb N,
$$

where `r\ne0` and `r\ne1`, and define

$$
S_n=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}x_k
=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(a+kd)br^k.
$$

This is not a new kind of magic formula. It is the same subtraction method,
but now the coefficient changes by `d` when the index moves forward. Compare
`S_n` with `rS_n`:

$$
S_n-rS_n
=ab+db(r+r^2+\cdots+r^{n-1})-(a+(n-1)d)br^n.
$$

The middle part is again a geometric sum. Dividing by `1-r` and simplifying
gives the source formula

$$
S_n=
\frac{ab-(a+nd)br^n}{1-r}
+\frac{dbr(1-r^n)}{(1-r)^2}.
$$

<CommonMistake title="Watch the shifted boundary term">
The boundary term is the subtle point. The last term in `rS_n` is
`(a+(n-1)d)br^n`, but after combining with the finite geometric sum the
equivalent final formula can be written with `(a+nd)br^n`.
</CommonMistake>

## An applied recurrence: the mortgage formula

The chapter's mortgage example is a useful applied recurrence. Let:

- `P` be the initial principal;
- `R` be the annual fixed interest rate;
- `N` be the duration in months;
- `x` be the monthly payment;
- `L_n` be the loan amount after the `n`-th monthly payment.

Then

$$
L_0=P,\qquad
L_n=L_{n-1}\left(1+\frac{R}{12}\right)-x.
$$

Writing `q=1+R/12`, repeated substitution gives

$$
L_n=Pq^n-x(q^{n-1}+q^{n-2}+\cdots+q+1).
$$

Using the geometric sum,

$$
L_n=Pq^n-x\frac{q^n-1}{q-1}.
$$

To finish the loan after `N` months, set `L_N=0`, so

$$
x=P\frac{(R/12)(1+R/12)^N}{(1+R/12)^N-1}.
$$

The formula is not a separate trick. It is the geometric series formula applied
to a recurrence where interest multiplies the balance and the payment subtracts
from it.

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="A sequence is formally a function from which set to which set in this chapter?">
Use the chapter definition.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It is a function `a:\mathbb Z^+\to\mathbb R`, so each positive integer index
returns one real-number term.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="For `a_1=4` and `a_{n+1}=2a_n+1`, what is `a_3`?">
Compute one term at a time.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`a_2=2(4)+1=9`, so `a_3=2(9)+1=19`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What is the sum of the first `n` terms of an arithmetic sequence with first term `a` and common difference `d`?">
Use first-last pairing.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">

$$
s_n=\frac n2\left[2a+(n-1)d\right].
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the geometric-sum formula need a separate case when `r=1`?">
Look at the denominator.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The formula `b(1-r^n)/(1-r)` divides by `1-r`, which is zero when `r=1`.
In that case every term is `b`, so the sum is `bn`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="In the arithmetic-geometric sum, why is the method `S_n-rS_n` useful?">
Track what happens to the coefficient `a+kd` after shifting by one power of
`r`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Most terms line up with the same power of `r`, and their coefficients differ by
exactly `d`. That leaves two boundary terms and a geometric sum
`db(r+\cdots+r^{n-1})`, which can be simplified by the ordinary geometric-sum
formula.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

1. Find a formula for `1,0,1,0,\ldots`.
2. Prove that `1\cdot3\cdot5\cdots(2n-1)=(2n)!/(2^n n!)`.
3. Let `a_1=3` and `a_{n+1}=a_n+4`. Find `a_n` and `s_n`.
4. Let `b_1=5` and `b_{n+1}=3b_n`. Find `b_n` and the sum of the first `n`
   terms.
5. Let `B_n=(\phi^n-\psi^n)/(\phi-\psi)`, where
   `\phi=(1+\sqrt5)/2` and `\psi=(1-\sqrt5)/2`. Verify that
   `B_1=B_2=1` and `B_{n+2}=B_{n+1}+B_n`.
6. Let `L_0=2000` and `L_n=1.02L_{n-1}-150`. Write `L_n` using a finite
   geometric sum.
7. Let `x_k=(2+3k)5(1/2)^k` and `S_n=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}x_k`. Write `S_n`
   using the arithmetic-geometric formula.

## Guided solutions

1. The term is `1` on odd indices and `0` on even indices, so
   `a_n=(1+(-1)^{n-1})/2`.
2. Divide `(2n)!` into its even and odd factors. The even product is
   `2\cdot4\cdots(2n)=2^n n!`, so the odd product is
   `(2n)!/(2^n n!)`.
3. This is arithmetic with first term `3` and common difference `4`, hence
   `a_n=3+4(n-1)=4n-1` and
   `s_n=n[2\cdot3+(n-1)4]/2=n(2n+1)`.
4. This is geometric with first term `5` and ratio `3`, so
   `b_n=5\cdot3^{n-1}` and
   `s_n=5(3^n-1)/(3-1)=5(3^n-1)/2`.
5. Since `\phi^2=\phi+1` and `\psi^2=\psi+1`, multiplying by
   `\phi^n` or `\psi^n` gives
   `\phi^{n+2}=\phi^{n+1}+\phi^n` and
   `\psi^{n+2}=\psi^{n+1}+\psi^n`. Subtract the two identities and divide by
   `\phi-\psi` to obtain `B_{n+2}=B_{n+1}+B_n`. Also
   `B_1=1` and `B_2=(\phi^2-\psi^2)/(\phi-\psi)=\phi+\psi=1`. Therefore
   `{B_n}` satisfies the same initial values and recurrence as Fibonacci.
6. Repeated substitution gives
   `L_n=2000(1.02)^n-150[(1.02)^{n-1}+\cdots+1]`, so
   `L_n=2000(1.02)^n-150((1.02)^n-1)/0.02`.
7. Here `a=2`, `d=3`, `b=5`, and `r=1/2`. Therefore

   $$
   S_n=
   \frac{10-(2+3n)5(1/2)^n}{1-1/2}
   +\frac{15(1/2)(1-(1/2)^n)}{(1-1/2)^2}.
   $$

   This can be simplified further, but the important point is the substitution
   into the general finite-sum formula.
