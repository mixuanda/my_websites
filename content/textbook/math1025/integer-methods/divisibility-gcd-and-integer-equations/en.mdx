## Why integer arithmetic needs new language

Integer arithmetic looks familiar, but proofs about integers need vocabulary
that is more precise than ordinary division. When we write `a/b` in real
algebra, the quotient usually exists as a real number. In integer arithmetic,
the important question is different: can the quotient be chosen as another
integer?

This question controls many later methods. It lets us define prime numbers,
measure common factors, compute greatest common divisors efficiently, and
decide when an equation such as

$$
ax+by=c
$$

has integer solutions.

## Divisibility

<Definition title="Divisibility">
Let `a,b\in\mathbb Z`. We say that `a` divides `b`, written `a\mid b`, if
there exists an integer `d` such that

$$
b=ad.
$$

In that case, `a` is called a divisor or factor of `b`.
</Definition>

The phrase "there exists an integer" is the essential part of the definition.
For example, `3\mid 15` because `15=3\cdot5`, while `4\nmid 15` because
there is no integer `d` with `15=4d`.

Several edge cases are worth fixing immediately:

- every integer divides `0`, because `0=a\cdot0`;
- `0` divides only `0`, because `x=0\cdot d` forces `x=0`;
- `\pm1` divides every integer;
- if `a\ne0`, then both `a` and `-a` divide `a`.

<TheoremCard title="Basic divisibility rules">
Let `a,b,c\in\mathbb Z`.

1. If `b\ne0` and `a\mid b`, then `|a|\le |b|`.
2. If `a\mid b`, then `(\pm a)\mid(\pm b)`.
3. If `a\mid b` and `b\mid c`, then `a\mid c`.
4. If `a\mid b` and `a\mid c`, then
   `a\mid (bx+cy)` for all `x,y\in\mathbb Z`.
</TheoremCard>

The fourth rule is the one that quietly powers much of the chapter. If a number
divides two integers, then it divides every integer linear combination of them.
For instance, if `6\mid 18` and `6\mid 30`, then

$$
6\mid 18x+30y
$$

for every pair of integers `x,y`.

## Primes, composites, and well-ordering

<Definition title="Prime and composite">
Let `n\in\mathbb Z^+` with `n>1`.

- `n` is prime if its only positive divisors are `1` and `n`;
- `n` is composite if it is not prime.

The integer `1` is neither prime nor composite.
</Definition>

Equivalently, `n>1` is composite exactly when

$$
n=n_1n_2
$$

for integers `n_1,n_2` satisfying `1<n_1,n_2<n`.

The proofs about prime numbers rely on a simple but powerful ordering fact.

<TheoremCard title="Well-ordering principle">
Every nonempty subset of `\mathbb N=\{0,1,2,\ldots\}` has a least element.
</TheoremCard>

This principle is a way to run a least-counterexample argument. If a bad set of
positive integers is nonempty, choose its least bad element and show that this
least element creates an even smaller bad element. That contradiction proves
that the bad set was empty.

<TheoremCard title="Every integer greater than 1 has a prime factor">
Every positive integer `n>1` is divisible by at least one prime.
</TheoremCard>

Here is the proof pattern. Suppose the claim fails, and let `m` be the least
integer greater than `1` with no prime factor. Then `m` cannot itself be prime.
So `m=n_1n_2` with `1<n_1,n_2<m`. By minimality of `m`, the smaller number
`n_1` has a prime factor `p`. Since `p\mid n_1` and `n_1\mid m`, transitivity
gives `p\mid m`, contradicting the choice of `m`.

<TheoremCard title="Infinitely many primes">
There are infinitely many prime numbers.
</TheoremCard>

If there were only finitely many primes `p_1,\ldots,p_N`, consider

$$
n=p_1p_2\cdots p_N+1.
$$

The previous theorem gives a prime divisor of `n`. That prime must be one of
the listed primes, say `p_i`. But `p_i` also divides the product
`p_1p_2\cdots p_N`, so it divides the difference

$$
n-p_1p_2\cdots p_N=1,
$$

which is impossible for a prime.

## Greatest common divisors

<Definition title="Greatest common divisor">
Let `a,b\in\mathbb Z`. If at least one of `a,b` is nonzero, the greatest common
divisor is

$$
\gcd(a,b)=\max\{d\in\mathbb Z:d\mid a\text{ and }d\mid b\}.
$$

If `a=b=0`, define `\gcd(0,0)=0`.
</Definition>

For nonzero input, the maximum exists because the possible common divisors are
bounded in absolute value. Also, `\gcd(a,b)` is always positive unless both
inputs are zero.

<Definition title="Relatively prime">
Two nonzero integers `a` and `b` are relatively prime if their only positive
common divisor is `1`. Equivalently,

$$
\gcd(a,b)=1.
$$
</Definition>

The gcd removes the common part of two integers. If

$$
d=\gcd(a,b),
$$

then

$$
\gcd\left(\frac ad,\frac bd\right)=1.
$$

For example, `\gcd(126,140)=14`, so

$$
\frac{126}{140}=\frac{9}{10},
$$

and `9` and `10` are relatively prime.

## The division algorithm

The next task is computational: how do we find the gcd without already knowing
all common divisors? The key is integer division with a controlled remainder.

<TheoremCard title="Division algorithm">
Let `a,b\in\mathbb Z` with `b\ne0`. Then there exist unique integers `q` and
`r` such that

$$
a=bq+r,\qquad 0\le r<|b|.
$$

The integer `q` is the quotient and `r` is the remainder.
</TheoremCard>

The condition `0\le r<|b|` is not decoration. It is what makes the remainder
unique. For example,

$$
17=5\cdot3+2
$$

has an allowed remainder `2`, while

$$
17=5\cdot2+7
$$

does not count as the division algorithm because `7` is not smaller than `5`.

<CommonMistake title="Do not ignore the remainder range">
The equation `a=bq+r` alone is not enough. There are infinitely many such
representations if `r` is unrestricted, because changing `q` changes `r`.
The inequality `0\le r<|b|` is what selects the canonical quotient and
remainder.
</CommonMistake>

## Gcd invariance and the Euclidean algorithm

The division algorithm is useful because replacing `(a,b)` by `(b,r)` preserves
the gcd.

<TheoremCard title="Gcd step">
If `a=bq+r`, then

$$
\gcd(a,b)=\gcd(b,r).
$$
</TheoremCard>

Indeed, a common divisor of `a` and `b` divides `r=a-bq`, and a common divisor
of `b` and `r` divides `a=bq+r`. Therefore the two pairs have exactly the same
positive common divisors.

<WorkedExample title="Euclidean algorithm for `\gcd(7224,1290)`">
Apply the division algorithm repeatedly:

$$
\begin{aligned}
7224&=1290\cdot5+774,\\
1290&=774\cdot1+516,\\
774&=516\cdot1+258,\\
516&=258\cdot2+0.
\end{aligned}
$$

The last nonzero remainder is `258`, so

$$
\gcd(7224,1290)=258.
$$
</WorkedExample>

The algorithm must terminate because the remainders form a strictly decreasing
sequence of nonnegative integers:

$$
r_1>r_2>r_3>\cdots\ge0.
$$

A strictly decreasing sequence of nonnegative integers cannot continue forever.

## Extended Euclidean algorithm and Bézout's identity

The same computation can be run backward to express the gcd as an integer
linear combination of the original two numbers.

<WorkedExample title="Back-substitution">
From the previous computation,

$$
258=774-516.
$$

Substitute `516=1290-774`:

$$
258=774-(1290-774)=2\cdot774-1290.
$$

Substitute `774=7224-5\cdot1290`:

$$
258=2(7224-5\cdot1290)-1290
=2\cdot7224-11\cdot1290.
$$

Thus

$$
258=7224\cdot2+1290\cdot(-11).
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Watch the Euclidean algorithm become Bézout's identity

The downward Euclidean computation and the upward back-substitution are not two
separate tricks. They are the same division equations read in opposite
directions: first to locate the last nonzero remainder, then to rewrite that
remainder using the original two integers.

<VideoExplanation id="math1025-euclidean-bezout-integer-equation-story" />

<TheoremCard title="Bézout's identity">
For any integers `a,b`, there exist integers `x,y` such that

$$
\gcd(a,b)=ax+by.
$$
</TheoremCard>

This result says that the gcd is not merely the largest common divisor; it is
also the smallest positive integer that can be built as an integer linear
combination of `a` and `b`.

<TheoremCard title="Relative primality criterion">
Let `a,b` be nonzero integers. Then `a` and `b` are relatively prime if and only
if there exist integers `x,y` such that

$$
ax+by=1.
$$
</TheoremCard>

One direction is Bézout's identity when `\gcd(a,b)=1`. For the converse, if
`ax+by=1`, then every common divisor of `a` and `b` must divide `1`; hence the
greatest positive common divisor is `1`.

<WorkedExample title="Extended Euclidean table">
For `12345` and `11111`, the Euclidean algorithm gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
12345&=11111\cdot1+1234,\\
11111&=1234\cdot9+5,\\
1234&=5\cdot246+4,\\
5&=4\cdot1+1,\\
4&=1\cdot4+0.
\end{aligned}
$$

So the gcd is `1`. Back-substitution, or the coefficient table form of the
extended Euclidean algorithm, gives

$$
1=12345(-2224)+11111(2471).
$$

This proves in particular that `12345` and `11111` are relatively prime.
</WorkedExample>

## Common divisors and Euclid's lemma

Bézout's identity also gives a cleaner characterization of the gcd.

<TheoremCard title="Common divisors divide the gcd">
For integers `a,b,n`,

$$
n\mid a\text{ and }n\mid b
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
n\mid \gcd(a,b).
$$
</TheoremCard>

The forward direction is the useful one. If `n` divides both `a` and `b`, then
it divides every linear combination `ax+by`; in particular, it divides the
Bézout combination equal to `\gcd(a,b)`.

<TheoremCard title="Euclid's lemma">
Let `p` be prime. If `p\mid ab`, then `p\mid a` or `p\mid b`.
</TheoremCard>

If `p\nmid a`, then `\gcd(a,p)=1`, so there exist integers `m,n` with

$$
am+pn=1.
$$

Multiplying by `b` gives

$$
abm+pbn=b.
$$

Since `p\mid ab`, both terms on the left are divisible by `p`, so `p\mid b`.

## Unique prime factorization

<TheoremCard title="Fundamental theorem of arithmetic">
Every positive integer greater than `1` can be written as a product of primes,
and this product is unique up to the order of the factors.
</TheoremCard>

Existence follows from the same least-counterexample strategy used earlier.
If there were a least integer greater than `1` that could not be written as a
product of primes, it would not be prime, so it would split into smaller
factors, and those smaller factors would already have prime factorizations.

Uniqueness uses Euclid's lemma. If

$$
p_1p_2\cdots p_r=q_1q_2\cdots q_s,
$$

then `p_1` divides the right-hand product, so `p_1` must divide one of the
prime factors `q_j`. Hence `p_1=q_j`. Cancel and repeat.

The theorem lets us compute gcds by comparing exponents. If

$$
a=p_1^{m_1}\cdots p_r^{m_r},
\qquad
b=p_1^{n_1}\cdots p_r^{n_r},
$$

where missing primes have exponent `0`, then

$$
\gcd(a,b)=p_1^{\min(m_1,n_1)}\cdots p_r^{\min(m_r,n_r)}.
$$

<WorkedExample title="Gcd from prime powers">
Since

$$
144=2^4\cdot3^2,\qquad 60=2^2\cdot3\cdot5,
$$

we have

$$
\gcd(144,60)=2^2\cdot3^1\cdot5^0=12.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Linear Diophantine equations

An equation

$$
ax+by=c
$$

where the unknowns `x,y` must be integers is called a linear Diophantine
equation. Bézout's identity tells us exactly when it is solvable.

<TheoremCard title="Solvability criterion">
Let `a,b,c\in\mathbb Z` and let `d=\gcd(a,b)`. The equation

$$
ax+by=c
$$

has an integer solution `(x,y)` if and only if `d\mid c`.
</TheoremCard>

If a solution exists, then `d` divides `a` and `b`, so `d` divides
`ax+by=c`. Conversely, if `c=dr`, Bézout's identity gives `d=am+bn`, and then

$$
c=dr=a(mr)+b(nr).
$$

Thus `(mr,nr)` is a solution.

Once one solution is known, all solutions can be described. Suppose
`d=\gcd(a,b)\ne0`, put

$$
u=\frac ad,\qquad v=\frac bd,
$$

and let `(x_0,y_0)` be one solution. Then every solution is

$$
x=x_0+kv,\qquad y=y_0-ku,\qquad k\in\mathbb Z.
$$

<WorkedExample title="A measuring problem">
Can a `180` mL cup and a `105` mL glass measure exactly `30` mL? Algebraically,
we ask whether

$$
180x+105y=30
$$

has integer solutions. Since

$$
\gcd(180,105)=15
$$

and `15\mid 30`, the equation is solvable.

Using the extended Euclidean algorithm,

$$
15=180\cdot3-105\cdot5.
$$

Multiplying by `2` gives

$$
30=180\cdot6-105\cdot10.
$$

So one solution is `(x,y)=(6,-10)`. Since `a/d=12` and `b/d=7`, all solutions
are

$$
(x,y)=(6+7k,-10-12k),\qquad k\in\mathbb Z.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="What does `a\mid b` mean in integer language?">
Use the quantifier hidden in the definition.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It means that there exists an integer `d` such that `b=ad`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does every common divisor of `a` and `b` divide every expression `ax+by` with `x,y\in\mathbb Z`?">
Use the definition of divisibility twice.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
If `n\mid a` and `n\mid b`, then `a=nr` and `b=ns` for some integers `r,s`.
Thus `ax+by=n(rx+sy)`, and `rx+sy` is an integer.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="In the Euclidean algorithm for `7224` and `1290`, why is the gcd the last nonzero remainder?">
Use the identity `\gcd(a,b)=\gcd(b,r)` after each division step.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Each division step preserves the gcd, so the original gcd equals the gcd of
the last nonzero remainder `258` and `0`. That gcd is `258`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What condition decides whether `ax+by=c` has integer solutions?">
State it using `\gcd(a,b)`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The equation has integer solutions if and only if `\gcd(a,b)\mid c`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

1. List all positive divisors of `84`, and compute `\gcd(84,60)`.
2. Use the division algorithm to write `-37=5q+r` with `0\le r<5`.
3. Use the Euclidean algorithm to compute `\gcd(252,198)`.
4. Express `\gcd(252,198)` as `252x+198y`.
5. Prove that if `n\mid a` and `n\mid b`, then `n\mid\gcd(a,b)`.
6. Determine whether `35x+21y=14` has integer solutions. If it does, find one.
7. Determine whether `35x+21y=10` has integer solutions.
8. Use prime factorizations to compute `\gcd(2^4\cdot3^2\cdot5,\,
   2^3\cdot3^5\cdot7)`.

<RevealSolution title="Guided solutions">

1. The positive divisors of `84` are `1,2,3,4,6,7,12,14,21,28,42,84`.
   Since `60=2^2\cdot3\cdot5` and `84=2^2\cdot3\cdot7`,
   `\gcd(84,60)=2^2\cdot3=12`.
2. We need a remainder between `0` and `4`. Since
   `-37=5(-8)+3`, the quotient is `q=-8` and the remainder is `r=3`.
3. `252=198\cdot1+54`, `198=54\cdot3+36`, `54=36\cdot1+18`,
   and `36=18\cdot2+0`. Hence `\gcd(252,198)=18`.
4. Back-substitute:
   `18=54-36=54-(198-3\cdot54)=4\cdot54-198`.
   Since `54=252-198`, we get
   `18=4(252-198)-198=4\cdot252-5\cdot198`.
5. By Bézout's identity, `\gcd(a,b)=ax+by` for some integers `x,y`.
   If `n` divides both `a` and `b`, then `n` divides the right-hand side.
6. `\gcd(35,21)=7`, and `7\mid14`, so solutions exist. Since
   `7=35-21`, multiplying by `2` gives `14=35\cdot2+21(-2)`, so
   `(x,y)=(2,-2)` is one solution.
7. `\gcd(35,21)=7`, but `7\nmid10`, so there are no integer solutions.
8. Compare the smaller exponent of each prime:
   `2^{\min(4,3)}3^{\min(2,5)}5^{\min(1,0)}7^{\min(0,1)}
   =2^3\cdot3^2=72`.
</RevealSolution>
