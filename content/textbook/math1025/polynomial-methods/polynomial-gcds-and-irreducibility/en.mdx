## From integer gcds to polynomial gcds

Chapter 7 showed that integer divisibility is controlled by gcds, the
Euclidean algorithm, Bezout identities, and prime factorization. Chapter 8
repeats the same architecture for polynomials. The analogy is powerful, but
there is one new subtlety: multiplying a polynomial by a nonzero constant does
not change its divisibility behavior.

For example, `x-1` and `5x-5` divide exactly the same polynomials up to a
constant factor. To make a gcd unique, we choose the monic representative.

## Polynomial divisibility and associates

<Definition title="Polynomial divisibility">
For `f(x),g(x)\in R[x]`, we say that `g(x)` divides `f(x)`, written
`g(x)\mid f(x)`, if there exists `q(x)\in R[x]` such that

$$
f(x)=g(x)q(x).
$$
</Definition>

Two nonzero polynomials divide each other exactly when they differ by a nonzero
constant factor.

<TheoremCard title="Mutual divisibility">
For nonzero `f(x),g(x)\in R[x]`,

$$
g\mid f\text{ and }f\mid g
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
f(x)=kg(x)
$$

for some nonzero constant `k\in R`.
</TheoremCard>

The proof uses degrees. If `f=d_1g` and `g=d_2f`, then
`f=d_1d_2f`. Since `f\ne0`, the product `d_1d_2` must be the constant
polynomial `1`, so both `d_1` and `d_2` are constant.

## Greatest common divisors in R[x]

<Definition title="Greatest common divisor of polynomials">
Let `f(x),g(x)\in R[x]` not both be zero. A polynomial `d(x)` is a greatest
common divisor of `f` and `g` if

1. `d(x)\mid f(x)` and `d(x)\mid g(x)`;
2. every common divisor of `f` and `g` divides `d(x)`.

The notation `\gcd(f,g)` means the unique monic greatest common divisor.
</Definition>

The second condition is often more useful than the word "greatest." There is
no natural ordering by size for polynomials, so the gcd is characterized by
divisibility: it is the common divisor that absorbs every other common divisor.

<CommonMistake title="A gcd is monic by convention">
If a Euclidean computation ends with `-2x+2`, the gcd is not usually recorded
as `-2x+2`. Since `-2x+2=-2(x-1)`, the monic gcd is `x-1`.
</CommonMistake>

## The Euclidean algorithm for polynomials

The division algorithm immediately gives the polynomial Euclidean algorithm.
If

$$
f(x)=g(x)q(x)+r(x),\qquad \deg r\lt\deg g,
$$

then

$$
\gcd(f,g)=\gcd(g,r).
$$

Indeed, every common divisor of `f` and `g` divides `r=f-gq`, and every common
divisor of `g` and `r` divides `f=gq+r`.

Repeating this step produces remainders of strictly decreasing degree, so the
algorithm must stop. The last nonzero remainder, made monic, is the gcd.

<WorkedExample title="A polynomial Euclidean algorithm">
Let

$$
f(x)=4x^4-2x^3-16x^2+5x+9,\qquad
g(x)=2x^3-x^2-5x+4.
$$

The slide computation gives

$$
f(x)=(2x)g(x)+(-6x^2-3x+9),
$$

$$
g(x)=\left(-\frac13x+\frac13\right)(-6x^2-3x+9)+(-x+1),
$$

and

$$
-6x^2-3x+9=(6x+9)(-x+1)+0.
$$

The last nonzero remainder is `-x+1`, so the monic gcd is

$$
\gcd(f,g)=x-1.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Bezout identities

The extended Euclidean algorithm also survives in `R[x]`.

<TheoremCard title="Polynomial Bezout identity">
If `f(x),g(x)\in R[x]` are nonzero, then there exist `a(x),b(x)\in R[x]`
such that

$$
\gcd(f,g)=a(x)f(x)+b(x)g(x).
$$
</TheoremCard>

For the worked example above, back-substitution gives

$$
x-1=
\left(-\frac13x+\frac13\right)f(x)
\left(\frac23x^2-\frac23x-1\right)g(x).
$$

This identity is more than a computational trick. It proves that if
`\gcd(f,g)=1`, then polynomial combinations of `f` and `g` can produce the
constant polynomial `1`. That is the engine behind many divisibility theorems.

<Definition title="Relatively prime polynomials">
Nonzero polynomials `f(x)` and `g(x)` are relatively prime if

$$
\gcd(f,g)=1.
$$

Equivalently, there exist `a(x),b(x)\in R[x]` such that

$$
a(x)f(x)+b(x)g(x)=1.
$$
</Definition>

## Irreducible polynomials

<Definition title="Irreducible over a field">
Let `F` be a field. A nonconstant polynomial `p(x)\in F[x]` is irreducible
over `F` if it cannot be written as

$$
p(x)=g(x)h(x)
$$

with `g(x),h(x)\in F[x]` and

$$
0\lt\deg g,\deg h\lt\deg p.
$$
</Definition>

Irreducibility depends on the coefficient field.

<WorkedExample title="Changing the field can change irreducibility">
The polynomial `x^2-2` is irreducible over `Q`, but reducible over `R`:

$$
x^2-2=(x-\sqrt2)(x+\sqrt2).
$$

The polynomial `x^2+1` is irreducible over `R`, but reducible over `C`:

$$
x^2+1=(x-i)(x+i).
$$
</WorkedExample>

Over `C`, every irreducible polynomial is linear, because the fundamental
theorem of algebra gives a root for every nonconstant polynomial. Over `R`, the
irreducible polynomials are exactly:

- linear polynomials;
- quadratic polynomials `ax^2+bx+c` with discriminant `b^2-4ac\lt0`.

The quadratic case reflects complex conjugate roots. If `\alpha\notin R`, then

$$
(x-\alpha)(x-\bar\alpha)=x^2-2\operatorname{Re}(\alpha)x+|\alpha|^2
$$

has real coefficients and no real linear factor.

## Divisibility principles for irreducibles

Irreducible polynomials behave like primes.

<TheoremCard title="Irreducible-divisibility principles">
Let `p(x)` be irreducible over `R`.

1. If `p\nmid a`, then `\gcd(a,p)=1`.
2. If `p\mid ab`, then `p\mid a` or `p\mid b`.
3. The equation `a(x)u(x)+b(x)v(x)=c(x)` has polynomial solutions if and
   only if `\gcd(a,b)\mid c`.
4. Every nonconstant polynomial over a field factors into irreducibles, uniquely
   up to order and nonzero constant factors.
</TheoremCard>

The proofs mirror the integer proofs from chapter 7. The Bezout identity gives
the key step. For example, if `p` is irreducible and `p\nmid a`, then
`\gcd(a,p)=1`, so

$$
u(x)a(x)+v(x)p(x)=1.
$$

Multiplying by `b(x)` shows that if `p\mid a(x)b(x)`, then `p\mid b(x)`.

## Proof sketch or proof idea

The safest way to reason about polynomial gcds is to avoid thinking of a gcd as
"the biggest expression." Polynomials do not come with a natural size order
that behaves like the order on positive integers. Instead, the gcd is the
common divisor that is divisible by every other common divisor. This is why the
second clause in the definition is the real content.

The Euclidean step

$$
\gcd(f,g)=\gcd(g,r)
$$

is just a divisibility equivalence. If a polynomial divides both `f` and `g`,
it divides `r=f-gq`. Conversely, if it divides both `g` and `r`, it divides
`f=gq+r`. Thus the two pairs have exactly the same common divisors, and hence
the same monic gcd. The only algorithmic ingredient is that `\deg r\lt\deg g`,
so the sequence of degrees strictly decreases.

Bezout's identity is obtained by keeping the quotient equations instead of
throwing them away. Each remainder is a polynomial combination of the two
previous remainders. Since the first two remainders are `f` and `g`, every later
remainder is a polynomial combination of `f` and `g`. The final nonzero
remainder is a constant multiple of the gcd, and after making it monic we get

$$
\gcd(f,g)=a(x)f(x)+b(x)g(x).
$$

This proof also explains why Bezout identities are not unique. Once one pair
`a,b` works, other pairs may be obtained by adding and subtracting suitable
multiples of `g/\gcd(f,g)` and `f/\gcd(f,g)`. In checkpoint problems, a prompt
must either ask for a specific Euclidean back-substitution pair or check the
identity itself.

<WorkedExample title="Use gcd to decide a polynomial equation">
Determine whether there exist `u(x),v(x)\in R[x]` such that

$$
(x^2-1)u(x)+(x-1)v(x)=x+1.
$$

The left side is a polynomial combination of `x^2-1` and `x-1`. Since

$$
x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1),
$$

we have

$$
\gcd(x^2-1,x-1)=x-1.
$$

By the polynomial Bezout-solvability criterion, the equation has a solution
only if `x-1` divides `x+1`. It does not, because substituting `x=1` gives
`2\ne0`. Therefore no such polynomials `u` and `v` exist.
</WorkedExample>

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="Treating scalar multiples as different gcd answers">
In `R[x]`, `x-1`, `2x-2`, and `-7x+7` have the same divisibility content.
Only `x-1` is the monic representative, so it is the standard value of the
gcd.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Saying irreducible without naming the field">
The phrase "`x^2-2` is irreducible" is incomplete. It is irreducible over
`Q`, but reducible over `R`. Always state the coefficient field when
irreducibility is the issue.
</CommonMistake>

## Summary

Polynomial gcd theory copies the structure of integer gcd theory, with degree
replacing size and monic normalization replacing positivity. The Euclidean
algorithm computes a gcd by preserving common divisors while lowering degree.
The extended algorithm gives Bezout identities. Irreducible polynomials play
the role of primes, but irreducibility depends on the field: over `C` only
linear polynomials are irreducible, while over `R` irreducibles are linear
polynomials and quadratics with negative discriminant.

## Study guide for the exercises

When a problem asks for a polynomial gcd, decide first whether factoring is
obvious. If both polynomials factor cleanly, the common monic factors can be
read directly. If not, use the Euclidean algorithm exactly as with integers:
divide, replace the pair by divisor and remainder, and repeat until the
remainder is zero. The last nonzero remainder may need to be multiplied by a
nonzero constant to make it monic.

When a problem asks for a Bezout identity, keep a record of each division
equation. The gcd is found by running the Euclidean algorithm downward, but the
Bezout expression is found by running the equations backward. Students often
make errors by changing a remainder without updating the earlier equation it
came from. A useful check is to expand the final `a(x)f(x)+b(x)g(x)` and verify
that every higher-degree term cancels.

For irreducibility questions, the coefficient field is part of the problem.
A quadratic with no rational root may still factor over `R`; a quadratic with
no real root will factor over `C`. Over `R`, the discriminant test is enough
for quadratics. Over `Q`, rational-root and number-system information matters.
For example, `x^2-5` is irreducible over `Q` because `\sqrt5` is not rational,
but it is reducible over `R`.

For proof questions, reuse the integer chapter deliberately. The statement
"irreducible divides a product implies it divides a factor" is not magic; it is
Bézout plus multiplication. If `p\nmid a`, then `\gcd(a,p)=1`, so
`ua+vp=1`. Multiplying by `b` turns this identity into a divisibility proof
for `b` whenever `p` divides `ab`.

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Why do we choose the monic gcd in `R[x]`?">
Think about multiplying a common divisor by a nonzero constant.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Greatest common divisors are unique only up to a nonzero constant factor, so
choosing the monic representative makes the notation unique.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What is the monic gcd of `-x+1` and `0`?">
Normalize the nonzero polynomial.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The gcd is `x-1`, because `-x+1=-(x-1)` and the monic representative is
`x-1`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Is `x^2+1` irreducible over `R`? Is it irreducible over `C`?">
Use the available roots in each field.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It is irreducible over `R` because it has no real root, but it is reducible
over `C` since `x^2+1=(x-i)(x+i)`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

1. Use the Euclidean algorithm to compute
   `\gcd(x^3-1,x^2-1)` in `R[x]`.
2. In the worked example, verify the Bezout identity for `x-1` by expanding the
   right-hand side.
3. Decide whether `x^2-5` is irreducible over `Q`, `R`, and `C`.
4. Prove: if `p(x)` is irreducible and `p\nmid a(x)`, then
   `\gcd(a,p)=1`.
5. Prove: if `p(x)` is irreducible and `p\mid a(x)b(x)`, then
   `p\mid a(x)` or `p\mid b(x)`.
6. Determine whether there exist `u(x),v(x)\in R[x]` such that
   `(x^2-1)u(x)+(x-1)v(x)=x+1`.

<RevealSolution title="Guided solutions">

1. Since `x^3-1=(x-1)(x^2+x+1)` and `x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1)`, the monic gcd is
   `x-1`.
2. Substitute the displayed `f` and `g`, distribute, and collect terms. The
   coefficients of `x^4,x^3,x^2` cancel, leaving `x-1`.
3. `x^2-5` is irreducible over `Q` because `\sqrt5\notin Q`; reducible over
   `R` as `(x-\sqrt5)(x+\sqrt5)`; and therefore reducible over `C`.
4. Since `p` is irreducible, its only divisors up to constants are `1` and
   `p`. If `p\nmid a`, the gcd cannot have degree `\deg p`, so it must be
   `1`.
5. From part 4, if `p\nmid a`, then `\gcd(a,p)=1`. Choose `u,v` with
   `ua+vp=1`. Multiplying by `b` gives `uab+vpb=b`; both terms on the left
   are divisible by `p`, so `p\mid b`.
6. The gcd of `x^2-1` and `x-1` is `x-1`. Since `x-1` does not divide
   `x+1`, no such `u,v` exist.
</RevealSolution>
