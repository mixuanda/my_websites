## Why induction is needed

Many early MATH1025 statements are not about one number. They are about every
positive integer at once. Checking the first few cases can suggest a pattern,
but it does not prove the pattern. For example,

$$
1^3+2^3+\cdots+n^3=\left(\frac{n(n+1)}2\right)^2
$$

is true for `n=1,2,3,4`, but the statement claims infinitely many facts. A
finite table cannot justify all of them. Mathematical induction is the proof
principle that turns one verified starting point and one repeatable implication
into a proof for every positive integer.

The hidden idea is simple: once the first statement is true, and once truth of
one statement always forces truth of the next, the chain cannot break.

## Statements indexed by integers

<Definition title="Indexed statement">
An indexed statement is a family of statements `P(n)`, one for each integer
`n` in a specified range. The sentence `P(n)` may be true for some values of
`n` and false for others.
</Definition>

Before writing an induction proof, always state `P(n)` explicitly. This avoids
two common errors: proving a different formula from the one claimed, or using
the induction assumption without knowing exactly what it says.

<TheoremCard title="Principle of mathematical induction">
Let `P(n)` be a statement for each `n \in Z^+`. Suppose:

1. `P(1)` is true.
2. For every integer `k \ge 1`, if `P(k)` is true, then `P(k+1)` is true.

Then `P(n)` is true for every `n \in Z^+`.
</TheoremCard>

The second condition is not the statement `P(k+1)` by itself. It is a
conditional statement: assuming `P(k)`, prove `P(k+1)`. The proof is allowed to
use the assumption `P(k)`, but only after clearly declaring it.

## The standard proof format

An induction proof normally has four visible pieces:

1. Define `P(n)`.
2. Verify the base case.
3. Prove the induction step: assume `P(k)` for an arbitrary `k` in the allowed
   range and deduce `P(k+1)`.
4. Invoke the induction principle.

This format is not decoration. It records exactly where the infinite claim
enters the proof.

<WorkedExample title="Sum of cubes">
Prove that

$$
1^3+2^3+\cdots+n^3=\frac{n^2(n+1)^2}{4}
$$

for every `n \in Z^+`.

Let `P(n)` be the displayed identity.

For `n=1`, the left side is `1`, and the right side is

$$
\frac{1^2(1+1)^2}{4}=1.
$$

So `P(1)` is true.

Now assume `P(k)` is true for some `k \ge 1`, so

$$
1^3+2^3+\cdots+k^3=\frac{k^2(k+1)^2}{4}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
1^3+2^3+\cdots+k^3+(k+1)^3
&=\frac{k^2(k+1)^2}{4}+(k+1)^3 \\
&=(k+1)^2\left(\frac{k^2}{4}+k+1\right) \\
&=(k+1)^2\frac{(k+2)^2}{4}.
\end{aligned}
$$

This is exactly `P(k+1)`. Therefore, by induction, the formula holds for all
positive integers `n`.
</WorkedExample>

The key line in the example is not the algebra alone. It is the moment where
the assumed formula for the first `k` cubes is used to rewrite the first part
of the sum for `k+1`.

## Divisibility induction

Induction is not limited to closed-form sums. It also proves divisibility
statements.

<Definition title="Divisibility">
For integers `m` and `n`, we say that `m` is divisible by `n`, and write
`n \mid m`, if there exists an integer `q` such that `m=nq`.
</Definition>

<WorkedExample title="A divisibility proof">
Prove that `3 \mid (n^3-n)` for every `n \in Z^+`.

Let `P(n)` be the statement `3 \mid (n^3-n)`.

For `n=1`, we have `1^3-1=0=3\cdot 0`, so `P(1)` is true.

Assume `P(k)` for some `k \ge 1`. Then `k^3-k=3q` for some `q \in Z`.
Compute

$$
\begin{aligned}
(k+1)^3-(k+1)
&=k^3+3k^2+3k+1-k-1\\
&=(k^3-k)+3k^2+3k\\
&=3q+3k^2+3k\\
&=3(q+k^2+k).
\end{aligned}
$$

Since `q+k^2+k` is an integer, `3 \mid ((k+1)^3-(k+1))`. Thus `P(k+1)` is
true, and induction proves the claim.
</WorkedExample>

The proof works because the induction assumption gives a precise multiple of
`3`, not merely a vague pattern.

## What can go wrong

<CommonMistake title="The induction step must work immediately after the base case">
A famous false proof claims that all objects in every finite set have the same
property by comparing two overlapping `n`-element subsets of an
`(n+1)`-element set. The argument fails at the transition from `n=1` to `n=2`:
the two one-element subsets do not overlap. A proof with a true base case but a
broken first transition proves nothing.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Do not prove only examples">
Showing `P(1)`, `P(2)`, and `P(3)` may help you discover the statement, but it
does not replace the induction step. The proof must explain why an arbitrary
true case forces the next one.
</CommonMistake>

## Variations

Sometimes the first relevant integer is not `1`. If a statement begins at
`n_0`, the base case is `P(n_0)`, and the induction step must prove
`P(k+1)` from `P(k)` for every `k \ge n_0`.

Sometimes a problem naturally splits into even and odd cases. Then an induction
step from `P(k)` to `P(k+2)` may be used, but it needs the correct starting
case for the parity being proved.

<TheoremCard title="Strong induction">
Let `P(n)` be a statement for each `n \in Z^+`. Suppose:

1. `P(1)` is true.
2. For every `k \ge 1`, if `P(1),P(2),\dots,P(k)` are all true, then `P(k+1)`
   is true.

Then `P(n)` is true for every `n \in Z^+`.
</TheoremCard>

Strong induction is useful when the next case depends on several earlier cases,
not only the immediately previous one.

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="In an induction proof, what is the difference between `P(k)` and `P(k+1)`?">
State the role of each one in the induction step.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`P(k)` is the induction assumption. `P(k+1)` is the next statement that must be
proved from that assumption.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is checking `n=1,2,3` not a proof of a formula for all positive integers?">
Think about the size of the claim.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The formula claims infinitely many cases. A finite list of checks may suggest a
pattern but does not prove the arbitrary transition from one case to the next.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If a statement is claimed for all integers `n \ge 5`, what should the base case be?">
Use the first allowed integer.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The base case should be `n=5`, and the induction step should run from an
arbitrary `k \ge 5` to `k+1`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

1. Prove by induction that `1+2+\cdots+n=n(n+1)/2` for every `n \in Z^+`.
2. Prove that `4 \mid (5^n-1)` for every `n \in Z^+`.
3. Prove that `n^2<2^n` for every integer `n \ge 5`.
4. Explain precisely why the overlapping-subset argument in the false proof
   fails at the first nontrivial transition.

## Guided solutions

1. Let `P(n)` be the summation formula. The base case is immediate. For the
   step, add `k+1` to the formula for `1+\cdots+k` and simplify to
   `(k+1)(k+2)/2`.
2. Base case: `5-1=4`. If `5^k-1=4q`, then
   `5^{k+1}-1=5(5^k)-1=5(4q+1)-1=20q+4=4(5q+1)`.
3. Start at `n=5`. If `k^2<2^k` and `k \ge 5`, then
   `(k+1)^2=k^2+2k+1<2^k+2^k=2^{k+1}` because `2k+1<2^k` for `k \ge 5`.
4. The two subsets used in the induction step overlap only when `n \ge 2`.
   The step from one object to two objects therefore has no shared object, so
   the argument cannot transfer the property.
