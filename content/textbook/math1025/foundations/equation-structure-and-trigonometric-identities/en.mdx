## Why this chapter matters

This chapter is about structure, not memorization. In trigonometry, the same
expression can be rewritten in several useful ways, but those rewrites are only
valuable if you know what they preserve:

- some steps preserve the solution set exactly;
- some steps only produce candidate solutions;
- some steps are valid only when the domain is checked first.

The goal is to read trig expressions as objects with geometry, symmetry, and
algebraic behavior.

## Radian measure and standard position

<Definition title="Radian measure">
If an angle subtends an arc of length `s` on a circle of radius `r`, then its
radian measure is

$$
\theta = \frac{s}{r}.
$$
</Definition>

When `r=1`, the radian measure is literally the arc length on the unit circle.
That is why radians are the natural unit for trigonometry and calculus.

Since one full revolution is `2\pi` radians or `360^\circ`, we have

$$
\pi \text{ radians} = 180^\circ,
$$

so

$$
1 \text{ radian} = \frac{180}{\pi}^\circ,
\qquad
1^\circ = \frac{\pi}{180} \text{ radians}.
$$

<Definition title="Angles in standard position">
An angle in the `xy`-plane is in standard position if its vertex is at the
origin and its initial ray lies on the positive `x`-axis.

- Counterclockwise rotation is positive.
- Clockwise rotation is negative.
</Definition>

From now on, every angle in this chapter is measured in radians unless the
problem explicitly says otherwise.

<WorkedExample title="Convert between degrees and radians">
The angle `150^\circ` equals

$$
150^\circ = 150 \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{5\pi}{6}.
$$

Likewise, `-45^\circ = -\pi/4`.
</WorkedExample>

## Area and arc length

Two useful geometric formulas follow immediately from the definition of radian
measure:

$$
s = r\theta,
\qquad
A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta.
$$

These formulas are not separate facts to memorize. They are the same angle
measure translated into length and area.

## Trigonometric functions on the unit circle

<Definition title="Trigonometric functions">
Place an angle `\theta` in standard position on a circle of radius `r`. Let the
terminal ray meet the circle at `P(x,y)`. Then

$$
\sin\theta = \frac{y}{r},
\qquad
\cos\theta = \frac{x}{r},
\qquad
\tan\theta = \frac{y}{x},
$$

and

$$
\csc\theta = \frac{r}{y},
\qquad
\sec\theta = \frac{r}{x},
\qquad
\cot\theta = \frac{x}{y}.
$$
</Definition>

On the unit circle, `r=1`, so sine and cosine become the `y`- and `x`-
coordinates directly. That is the geometric reason the unit circle is so useful:
it turns trigonometric values into coordinates.

Some values are undefined because their denominators vanish. For example,
`tan(\pi/2)` is undefined because `\cos(\pi/2)=0`.

<WorkedExample title="Exact values from the unit circle">
The standard values at `0`, `\pi/6`, `\pi/4`, `\pi/3`, and `\pi/2` are:

| `\theta` | `\sin\theta` | `\cos\theta` | `\tan\theta` |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `0` | `0` | `1` | `0` |
| `\pi/6` | `1/2` | `\sqrt3/2` | `1/\sqrt3` |
| `\pi/4` | `\sqrt2/2` | `\sqrt2/2` | `1` |
| `\pi/3` | `\sqrt3/2` | `1/2` | `\sqrt3` |
| `\pi/2` | `1` | `0` | undefined |

These values are the building blocks for the rest of the chapter.
</WorkedExample>

<CommonMistake title="Do not treat degrees as the default">
If a problem says `\pi/3`, it means radians, not degrees. In this course,
`60^\circ` and `\pi/3` are the same angle written in different units.
</CommonMistake>

## Symmetry and periodicity

The unit circle immediately gives the basic symmetry rules:

$$
\sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta,
\qquad
\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta,
\qquad
\tan(-\theta) = -\tan\theta.
$$

Reflection across the `y`-axis and the origin also gives the familiar angle
shifts such as `\pi-\theta`, `\pi+\theta`, and `2\pi+\theta`.

<TheoremCard title="Periodicity of trigonometric functions">
$$
\sin(\theta+2\pi)=\sin\theta,
\qquad
\cos(\theta+2\pi)=\cos\theta,
$$

$$
\sec(\theta+2\pi)=\sec\theta,
\qquad
\csc(\theta+2\pi)=\csc\theta,
$$

$$
\tan(\theta+\pi)=\tan\theta,
\qquad
\cot(\theta+\pi)=\cot\theta.
$$
</TheoremCard>

Periodicity is not merely a trick for reducing angles. It tells you what part of
the circle controls each function.

<WorkedExample title="Use symmetry to simplify a value">
Because `\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta`, we get

$$
\sin(-\pi/6) = -\sin(\pi/6) = -\frac12.
$$

Because `\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta`, we get

$$
\cos(-\pi/6) = \cos(\pi/6) = \frac{\sqrt3}{2}.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## The basic identities

<TheoremCard title="Pythagorean identities">
$$
\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1,
\qquad
1+\tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta,
\qquad
1+\cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta.
$$
</TheoremCard>

The first identity is the coordinate statement `x^2+y^2=1` on the unit circle.
The other two follow by dividing through by `\cos^2\theta` or `\sin^2\theta`
where those quantities are nonzero.

<WorkedExample title="Turn one identity into another">
Starting from

$$
\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1,
$$

divide by `\cos^2\theta` to obtain

$$
\tan^2\theta + 1 = \sec^2\theta.
$$

This step is valid only where `\cos\theta \neq 0`.
</WorkedExample>

<CommonMistake title="Do not divide without checking the denominator">
If a step divides by `\sin\theta` or `\cos\theta`, the identity may become
invalid at the angles where that quantity is zero. Always keep track of the
restricted domain.
</CommonMistake>

## Compound angle formulas

The compound angle formulas are the workhorse identities of the chapter. They
let you turn sums and differences of angles into algebraic expressions.

<CollapsibleProof title="Why the cosine difference formula is the key step">
Take two points on the unit circle:

$$
A=(\cos\alpha,\sin\alpha),
\qquad
B=(\cos\beta,\sin\beta).
$$

The distance formula gives

$$
AB^2=(\cos\alpha-\cos\beta)^2+(\sin\alpha-\sin\beta)^2.
$$

Expanding this expression and simplifying yields

$$
AB^2 = 2 - 2\cos\alpha\cos\beta - 2\sin\alpha\sin\beta.
$$

On the other hand, `AB` also depends on the angle difference `\alpha-\beta`,
so the same geometric quantity can be written as

$$
AB^2 = 2 - 2\cos(\alpha-\beta).
$$

Comparing the two formulas gives

$$
\cos(\alpha-\beta)=\cos\alpha\cos\beta+\sin\alpha\sin\beta.
$$

Once this identity is known, the remaining compound-angle formulas follow by
substituting `\beta\mapsto -\beta` and by taking ratios where appropriate.
</CollapsibleProof>

<TheoremCard title="Compound angle formulas">
$$
\sin(\alpha+\beta)=\sin\alpha\cos\beta+\cos\alpha\sin\beta
$$

$$
\sin(\alpha-\beta)=\sin\alpha\cos\beta-\cos\alpha\sin\beta
$$

$$
\cos(\alpha+\beta)=\cos\alpha\cos\beta-\sin\alpha\sin\beta
$$

$$
\cos(\alpha-\beta)=\cos\alpha\cos\beta+\sin\alpha\sin\beta
$$

$$
\tan(\alpha+\beta)=\frac{\tan\alpha+\tan\beta}{1-\tan\alpha\tan\beta}
$$

$$
\tan(\alpha-\beta)=\frac{\tan\alpha-\tan\beta}{1+\tan\alpha\tan\beta}
$$
</TheoremCard>

These formulas explain most of the angle-algebra manipulations you will see in
later work.

<WorkedExample title="Derive a sine addition formula from the cosine one">
If you know

$$
\cos(\alpha-\beta)=\cos\alpha\cos\beta+\sin\alpha\sin\beta,
$$

then replacing `\alpha` by `\pi/2-\alpha` and `\beta` by `-\beta` gives

$$
\sin(\alpha-\beta)=\sin\alpha\cos\beta-\cos\alpha\sin\beta.
$$

The other addition and subtraction formulas follow in the same way.
</WorkedExample>

## Double angle, product-to-sum, and sum-to-product

<TheoremCard title="Double angle formulas">
$$
\sin(2\alpha)=2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha
$$

$$
\cos(2\alpha)=\cos^2\alpha-\sin^2\alpha
=2\cos^2\alpha-1
=1-2\sin^2\alpha
$$

$$
\tan(2\alpha)=\frac{2\tan\alpha}{1-\tan^2\alpha}
$$
</TheoremCard>

These are just the compound-angle formulas with the same angle used twice.

<TheoremCard title="Product-to-sum formulas">
$$
\sin\alpha\cos\beta=\frac12\bigl(\sin(\alpha+\beta)+\sin(\alpha-\beta)\bigr)
$$

$$
\cos\alpha\sin\beta=\frac12\bigl(\sin(\alpha+\beta)-\sin(\alpha-\beta)\bigr)
$$

$$
\sin\alpha\sin\beta=-\frac12\bigl(\cos(\alpha+\beta)-\cos(\alpha-\beta)\bigr)
$$

$$
\cos\alpha\cos\beta=\frac12\bigl(\cos(\alpha+\beta)+\cos(\alpha-\beta)\bigr)
$$
</TheoremCard>

<TheoremCard title="Sum-to-product formulas">
$$
\sin A+\sin B = 2\sin\frac{A+B}{2}\cos\frac{A-B}{2}
$$

$$
\sin A-\sin B = 2\cos\frac{A+B}{2}\sin\frac{A-B}{2}
$$

$$
\cos A+\cos B = 2\cos\frac{A+B}{2}\cos\frac{A-B}{2}
$$

$$
\cos A-\cos B = -2\sin\frac{A+B}{2}\sin\frac{A-B}{2}
$$
</TheoremCard>

## Worked examples

<WorkedExample title="Rewrite a linear combination as a single sine">
Express `\sqrt3\cos x - \sin x` in the form `r\sin(x+\alpha)`.

We compare

$$
r\sin(x+\alpha)=r\sin x\cos\alpha+r\cos x\sin\alpha.
$$

Matching coefficients gives

$$
r\sin\alpha=\sqrt3,
\qquad
r\cos\alpha=-1.
$$

So `r=\sqrt{3+1}=2`, and we may choose `\alpha=2\pi/3`. Therefore

$$
\sqrt3\cos x-\sin x = 2\sin\!\left(x+\frac{2\pi}{3}\right).
$$

If we solve `\sqrt3\cos x-\sin x=1`, then

$$
2\sin\!\left(x+\frac{2\pi}{3}\right)=1,
$$

so

$$
\sin\!\left(x+\frac{2\pi}{3}\right)=\frac12.
$$

Hence

$$
x+\frac{2\pi}{3}=\frac{\pi}{6}+2k\pi
\quad\text{or}\quad
x+\frac{2\pi}{3}=\frac{5\pi}{6}+2k\pi,
$$

which gives

$$
x=-\frac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi
\quad\text{or}\quad
x=-\frac{\pi}{6}+2k\pi,
\qquad k\in Z.
$$
</WorkedExample>

<WorkedExample title="A compact algebraic identity">
Show that

$$
\cos^4\theta+\sin^4\theta=\frac{1+\cos^2(2\theta)}{2}
=\frac{3+\cos(4\theta)}{4}.
$$

Start with

$$
(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta)^2=1.
$$

Expanding gives

$$
\sin^4\theta+\cos^4\theta+2\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta=1.
$$

So

$$
\sin^4\theta+\cos^4\theta
=1-2\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta.
$$

Use `\sin(2\theta)=2\sin\theta\cos\theta` to get

$$
\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta=\frac14\sin^2(2\theta),
$$

and then use `\sin^2(2\theta)=1-\cos^2(2\theta)` or
` \cos(4\theta)=1-2\sin^2(2\theta)` to reach the stated forms.
</WorkedExample>

<CommonMistake title="Back-substitution is not optional">
After using identities to solve a trigonometric equation, always check the
candidate solutions in the original equation. A transformation may introduce
extra roots or hide domain restrictions.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Convert `210^\circ` to radians.">
Use the degree-radian conversion formula.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`210^\circ = 210 \cdot \pi/180 = 7\pi/6`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What are `\sin(-\theta)`, `\cos(-\theta)`, and `\tan(-\theta)`?">
Use symmetry of the unit circle.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta`, `\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta`, and
`\tan(-\theta)=-\tan\theta`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is `\tan(\pi/2)` undefined?">
Look at the denominator in the definition.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Because `\tan\theta=\sin\theta/\cos\theta`, and `\cos(\pi/2)=0`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Show the route from `\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1` to `1+\tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta`.">
State the division step carefully.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Divide both sides by `\cos^2\theta` where `\cos\theta \neq 0`, then rewrite
` \sin^2\theta/\cos^2\theta = \tan^2\theta` and `1/\cos^2\theta = \sec^2\theta`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

1. Convert `330^\circ` to radians and compute `\sin(330^\circ)` and
   `\cos(330^\circ)`.
2. Use a periodicity identity to simplify `\tan(\theta+\pi)`.
3. Prove `1+\tan^2\theta=\sec^2\theta` from the Pythagorean identity.
4. Rewrite `\sqrt3\cos x-\sin x` as `r\sin(x+\alpha)` and solve
   `\sqrt3\cos x-\sin x = 1`.
5. Show that `\cos^4\theta+\sin^4\theta = (3+\cos 4\theta)/4`.

The point of the exercises is not speed. It is to make the identities feel like
tools that you can derive, explain, and reuse.
