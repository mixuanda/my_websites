## Why this chapter starts here

Linear algebra keeps reusing the same two moves: add things together, then scale
them by real numbers. A vector space is the setting where those moves make
sense and obey the usual rules.

<Definition title="Vector space">
A vector space over the real numbers is a non-empty set `V` with addition and
scalar multiplication such that the usual vector laws hold: closure under
addition and scalar multiplication, commutativity, associativity, a zero vector,
additive inverses, distributivity, and scalar one.
</Definition>

## Familiar examples

The notes build the idea from several examples:

- `R^n` with usual vector addition and scalar multiplication.
- `M_{mn}`, the set of `m × n` real matrices with usual matrix addition and
  scalar multiplication.
- The set of all functions from `R` to `R`.
- `P_n`, the polynomials in one variable of degree at most `n`.

These look different, but the same algebraic rules still work.

<WorkedExample title="One idea, two different-looking examples">
Take `u = (1, 2)` and `v = (3, -1)` in `R^2`.

Then

$$
u + v = (4, 1), \qquad 2u = (2, 4).
$$

Now take

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 3 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A + B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 3 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

The objects are different, but the same addition-and-scaling logic is at work.
</WorkedExample>

## What the axioms are doing

The axioms are not just formal decoration. They guarantee that the usual moves
you already trust in `R^n` also work in more abstract settings.

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Shape alone is not enough">
Not every set with numbers in it is a vector space. You still need closure and
the rest of the vector laws. A set can look familiar and still fail one of the
axioms.
</CommonMistake>

## A small proof worth remembering

<CollapsibleProof title="Why the zero vector is unique">
Suppose `0` and `0'` are both zero vectors in the same vector space.

Then `0 = 0 + 0' = 0'`. So there is only one zero vector.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="Which of these is a vector space with the usual operations: `R^n`, `M_{mn}`, or the set of all functions from `R` to `R`?">
Think about which examples are explicitly listed in the notes.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
All of them are vector spaces over `R` with the usual operations.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Can a vector space have two different zero vectors at the same time?">
Use the proof above, or test the defining property directly.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. The zero vector is unique.
</RevealSolution>
