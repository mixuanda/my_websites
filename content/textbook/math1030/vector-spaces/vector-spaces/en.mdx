Chapter 6 marks a real shift in the course. Earlier chapters focused on
particular matrix procedures: solving systems, row reduction, invertibility, and
null space. A vector space asks a broader question:

What algebraic structure is shared by all the settings where we can add objects
and multiply them by scalars?

That question matters because linear algebra is not only about column vectors in
`R^n`. It also applies to matrix spaces, polynomial spaces, function spaces, and
later to subspaces cut out by equations. If the same algebraic rules hold in all
those settings, then one theory can govern all of them.

## Why the chapter begins with structure rather than computation

In `R^n`, you already know how to do two things:

1. add vectors;
2. multiply a vector by a real number.

Those two operations are so familiar that it is easy to forget what makes them
work well. They are not merely defined. They satisfy a network of rules:

- addition stays inside the set;
- scalar multiplication stays inside the set;
- addition is associative and commutative;
- there is a genuine zero vector;
- every vector has an additive inverse;
- scalar multiplication interacts with addition in the expected distributive way.

The purpose of the definition is to isolate exactly those rules and make them
the starting point.

## The geometric model: Euclidean space `R^n`

The first model is still geometric. A vector in `R^n` is an ordered `n`-tuple of
real numbers, usually written as a column vector. In `R^2` and `R^3`, such
vectors can be pictured as directed line segments.

For example, in `R^2`,

$$
u = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix},
\qquad
v = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Their sum is

$$
u + v = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},
$$

and for a scalar `\alpha = 2`,

$$
2u = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Geometrically, vector addition can be drawn by the head-to-tail rule or by the
parallelogram law. Scalar multiplication stretches or reverses direction,
depending on the sign of the scalar.

That geometric picture is useful, but it is not the full definition of linear
algebra. The full definition must also cover settings that have no obvious
picture in the plane.

## Formal definition

<Definition title="Vector space">
Let `V` be a nonempty set equipped with an addition operation and a scalar
multiplication by real numbers. We say that `V` is a **vector space over `R`**
if the following axioms hold for all vectors `u, v, w \in V` and all scalars
`\alpha, \beta \in R`:

1. `u + v \in V` (closure under addition);
2. `\alpha u \in V` (closure under scalar multiplication);
3. `u + v = v + u` (commutativity of addition);
4. `u + (v + w) = (u + v) + w` (associativity of addition);
5. there exists a vector `0 \in V` such that `u + 0 = u` for all `u`;
6. for every `u \in V`, there exists `-u \in V` such that `u + (-u) = 0`;
7. `\alpha(\beta u) = (\alpha\beta)u`;
8. `\alpha(u + v) = \alpha u + \alpha v`;
9. `(\alpha + \beta)u = \alpha u + \beta u`;
10. `1u = u`.
</Definition>

This definition deserves to be read slowly. It says that a vector space is not
merely a set of objects that look like vectors. It is a set together with two
operations that satisfy these ten structural laws.

In particular, the zero vector and additive inverse are part of the algebraic
structure. They are not visual conventions.

## Standard examples

The standard examples all satisfy the same axioms even though the underlying
objects look very different.

<WorkedExample title="Four standard vector spaces">
The following are all vector spaces over `R` with their usual operations.

1. **Euclidean space `R^n`.** Vectors are `n`-tuples of real numbers.

2. **Matrix space `M_{mn}` or `R^{m \times n}`.** Vectors are `m \times n`
   real matrices, and the vector-space operations are ordinary matrix addition
   and scalar multiplication.

3. **Function space `F = \{f : R \to R\}`.** The sum and scalar multiple are
   defined pointwise:

   $$
   (f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x),
   \qquad
   (\alpha f)(x) = \alpha f(x).
   $$

4. **Polynomial space `P_n`.** The elements are real polynomials of degree at
   most `n`, with the usual polynomial addition and scalar multiplication.

These examples already show the real point of the definition. Linear algebra is
not tied to one shape of object. It is tied to one family of algebraic laws.
</WorkedExample>

### The function-space example

Function spaces are often the first place where students see that "vector" does
not mean "arrow in the plane."

If `f, g : R \to R`, then `f + g` is another function from `R` to `R`, and
`\alpha f` is also a function from `R` to `R`. The zero vector is the zero
function

$$
0(x) = 0 \quad \text{for all } x \in R,
$$

and the additive inverse of `f` is the function `-f` defined by

$$
(-f)(x) = -f(x).
$$

Every vector-space axiom is checked pointwise. That is exactly why this is a
vector space.

### The polynomial-space example

The same pattern works for polynomials. If

$$
p(x) = a_0 + a_1x + \cdots + a_nx^n,
\qquad
q(x) = b_0 + b_1x + \cdots + b_nx^n,
$$

then `p + q` and `\alpha p` are still polynomials of degree at most `n`. So the
set `P_n` is closed under the operations, and the other axioms follow from the
usual algebra of real numbers.

## A warning: the operations matter

One of the most important remarks is that a vector space is determined not only
by its underlying set, but also by the operations chosen on that set.

The same set can support different vector-space structures.

<WorkedExample title="A nonstandard vector space on the same underlying set">
Let

$$
C = \{(x_1, x_2) \mid x_1, x_2 \in R\}.
$$

Define operations by

$$
(x_1, x_2) \oplus (y_1, y_2) = (x_1 + y_1 + 1,\ x_2 + y_2 + 1),
$$

and

$$
\alpha \odot (x_1, x_2) = (\alpha x_1 + \alpha - 1,\ \alpha x_2 + \alpha - 1).
$$

This is not the usual vector-space structure on `R^2`, but it still satisfies
the vector-space axioms. In this structure, the zero vector is

$$
0_C = (-1, -1),
$$

not `(0, 0)`.
</WorkedExample>

This example is valuable because it forces you to think structurally. The phrase
"zero vector" means the additive identity for the chosen operation, not the
visually obvious point `(0, 0)` from the usual Euclidean structure.

## First structural theorems

After the definition, the first structural theorems establish facts that may
feel obvious from `R^n`, but which should now be proved from the axioms alone.

<TheoremCard title="Cancellation law for vector addition">
If `v, u, w \in V` and

$$
v + w = u + w,
$$

then

$$
v = u.
$$
</TheoremCard>

<CollapsibleProof title="Proof of the cancellation law">
By the additive-inverse axiom, there exists `-w \in V` such that

$$
w + (-w) = 0.
$$

Starting from `v + w = u + w`, add `-w` to both sides:

$$
(v + w) + (-w) = (u + w) + (-w).
$$

By associativity,

$$
v + (w + (-w)) = u + (w + (-w)).
$$

Since `w + (-w) = 0`, this becomes

$$
v + 0 = u + 0.
$$

Using the zero-vector axiom, we conclude

$$
v = u.
$$
</CollapsibleProof>

<TheoremCard title="Uniqueness of the zero vector and additive inverse">
In any vector space:

1. the zero vector is unique;
2. each vector has a unique additive inverse.
</TheoremCard>

<CollapsibleProof title="Why the zero vector is unique">
Suppose `0_1` and `0_2` both satisfy the zero-vector axiom. Then

$$
0_1 + 0_2 = 0_2
$$

because `0_1` acts as a zero vector, and also

$$
0_1 + 0_2 = 0_1
$$

because `0_2` acts as a zero vector. Hence

$$
0_1 = 0_2.
$$
</CollapsibleProof>

<CollapsibleProof title="Why the additive inverse is unique">
Suppose both `v` and `u` are additive inverses of `w`. Then

$$
v + w = 0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
u + w = 0.
$$

So

$$
v + w = u + w.
$$

By the cancellation law, `v = u`. Therefore the additive inverse of a vector is
unique.
</CollapsibleProof>

These theorems are elementary, but they matter because they justify later
arguments. Once uniqueness is proved, you may speak of **the** zero vector and
**the** additive inverse without ambiguity.

## What students often overlook

<CommonMistake title="Closure is necessary, but it is not enough">
A set may be closed under one or both operations and still fail to be a vector
space. The full axiom list matters. Closure alone does not guarantee the
existence of a zero vector, additive inverses, or distributive laws.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="The zero vector depends on the chosen operations">
In the usual structure on `R^2`, the zero vector is `(0, 0)`. In the nonstandard
example above, the zero vector is `(-1, -1)`. The phrase "zero vector" refers to
the additive identity of the current vector-space structure, not to a visually
preferred coordinate pair.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is the set of all functions `f : R \to R` a vector space with pointwise addition and scalar multiplication?">
Answer by identifying the operations and the zero vector, not by appealing to
intuition alone.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Pointwise addition and scalar multiplication keep us inside the set of functions
from `R` to `R`. The zero vector is the zero function `0(x) = 0`, and the other
axioms hold because they reduce pointwise to the usual arithmetic laws in `R`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Can the same underlying set carry two different vector-space structures?">
Think about the nonstandard example on ordered pairs.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Yes. The same underlying set can support different choices of addition and
scalar multiplication. What matters is the full algebraic structure, not only
the set of elements.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Explain why `P_2`, the polynomials of degree at most `2`, is closed under addition and scalar multiplication.">
Use the degree bound directly.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
If `p(x)` and `q(x)` both have degree at most `2`, then each has the form

$$
a_0 + a_1x + a_2x^2.
$$

Adding them still produces a polynomial with no power above `x^2`, and
multiplying by a scalar only rescales the coefficients. So both operations stay
inside `P_2`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the uniqueness of the zero vector need a proof instead of being built into the definition directly?">
Answer from the axiomatic viewpoint.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The definition says that a vector with the zero-vector property exists, not that
there is exactly one such vector. Uniqueness must be deduced from the axioms so
that later arguments can refer unambiguously to the zero vector.
</RevealSolution>
