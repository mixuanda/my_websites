## Minimal spanning sets

A basis is the cleanest possible spanning set: it spans the space, and it has no
redundant vectors.

<Definition title="Basis">
A set of vectors `{u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_m}` is a basis for a vector space `V` if:

1. the vectors are linearly independent;
2. `Span{u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_m} = V`.
</Definition>

The notes stress that the same space can have more than one basis, but every
basis has the same number of vectors.

<WorkedExample title="The standard basis of `R^3`">
Let

$$
e_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
e_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
e_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

The notes show that these vectors are linearly independent and that they span
`R^3`.

So `{e_1, e_2, e_3}` is a basis for `R^3`.
</WorkedExample>

## Dimension

Once you know what a basis is, dimension is just a count.

<Definition title="Dimension">
The dimension of a vector space `V`, written `dim(V)`, is the number of
vectors in a basis of `V`.
</Definition>

The special case `dim({0}) = 0` is included in the notes because the zero space
has no linearly independent vectors.

The chapter also records familiar dimension facts:

- `dim(R^m) = m`.
- `dim(M_{mn}) = mn` for matrix spaces.
- `dim(P_n) = n + 1` for polynomials of degree at most `n`.

## Why dimension is powerful

Dimension tells you what is possible and what is not. If a space has dimension
`m`, then:

- any `m` linearly independent vectors form a basis;
- any `m` vectors that span the space form a basis;
- fewer than `m` vectors cannot span the space.

That is the quickest way to test many basis problems.

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Spanning is not enough">
A set can span a space and still fail to be a basis if it is not linearly
independent. Always check both conditions.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="What is `dim(R^3)`?">
Count the vectors in the standard basis.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`dim(R^3) = 3`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Can two vectors span `R^3`?">
Use the dimension rule from the chapter.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. `R^3` has dimension 3, so fewer than 3 vectors cannot span it.
</RevealSolution>
