Subspaces appear as soon as we notice that many linear problems do not use the
whole ambient vector space. The solution set of a homogeneous system, the set
of symmetric matrices, and the set of polynomials vanishing at a chosen point
are all smaller collections inside larger vector spaces.

The point of this section is to decide exactly when such a subset still carries
the full linear structure. A subspace is not merely a geometrically nice subset.
It is a subset on which the inherited addition and scalar multiplication still
satisfy the vector-space laws.

## Why only a few axioms need checking

Suppose `V` is already a vector space and `W \subseteq V`. If we use on `W` the
same addition and scalar multiplication already defined on `V`, then we do not
need to reprove associativity, commutativity, or the distributive laws from the
ground up. Those identities are already true in `V`.

The real question is narrower:

- does addition of two vectors in `W` stay inside `W`?
- does scalar multiplication stay inside `W`?
- does `W` still contain the vectors needed for the vector-space structure,
  especially the zero vector?

That is why the subspace test is a closure test.

<Definition title="Subspace definition">
Let `V` be a vector space. A subset `W \subseteq V` is called a **subspace** of
`V` if:

1. `W` is nonempty;
2. `u + v \in W` for all `u, v \in W`;
3. `\alpha u \in W` for all `u \in W` and all `\alpha \in R`.
</Definition>

The tutorial packet also emphasizes an equivalent formulation that is often
more convenient in proofs.

<TheoremCard title="Equivalent subspace test">
Let `V` be a vector space and `W \subseteq V`. Then `W` is a subspace of `V` if
and only if:

1. `0 \in W`;
2. for every `u, v \in W` and every `\alpha, \beta \in R`, the vector

   $$
   \alpha u + \beta v
   $$

   also belongs to `W`.
</TheoremCard>

<CollapsibleProof title="Why the equivalent test works">
Assume first that `W` is a subspace. Because `W` is nonempty, choose some
`w \in W`. Since `W` is closed under scalar multiplication,

$$
0w = 0 \in W.
$$

Now let `u, v \in W` and let `\alpha, \beta \in R`. Closure under scalar
multiplication gives `\alpha u \in W` and `\beta v \in W`, and then closure
under addition gives

$$
\alpha u + \beta v \in W.
$$

Conversely, suppose `0 \in W` and `\alpha u + \beta v \in W` whenever
`u, v \in W` and `\alpha, \beta \in R`. Then `W` is nonempty because it
contains `0`. If `u, v \in W`, choose `\alpha = \beta = 1` to get

$$
u + v \in W.
$$

If `u \in W` and `\kappa \in R`, choose `v = 0` and `\alpha = \kappa`,
`\beta = 0` to get

$$
\kappa u + 0 \cdot 0 = \kappa u \in W.
$$

So the original three-part definition is satisfied.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Standard examples from equations

The master notes begin with subsets defined by simple linear equations. These
examples matter because they show exactly what "through the origin" and
"homogeneous" mean in linear algebra.

<WorkedExample title="A line through the origin in `R^2`">
Let

$$
W = \left\{(x, y) \in R^2 : y = 2x\right\}.
$$

To prove that `W` is a subspace of `R^2`, check the three defining conditions.

1. `W` is nonempty because `(0, 0)` satisfies `0 = 2 \cdot 0`.
2. If `(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2) \in W`, then `y_1 = 2x_1` and `y_2 = 2x_2`.
   Therefore

   $$
   (y_1 + y_2) = 2(x_1 + x_2),
   $$

   so `(x_1 + x_2, y_1 + y_2) \in W`.
3. If `(x, y) \in W` and `\alpha \in R`, then `y = 2x`, so

   $$
   \alpha y = 2(\alpha x),
   $$

   which means `(\alpha x, \alpha y) \in W`.

Hence `W` is a subspace of `R^2`.
</WorkedExample>

The geometry matches the algebra: `W` is a line through the origin. The phrase
"through the origin" is not decorative. It is the visible sign that the zero
vector belongs to the set.

<WorkedExample title="A plane cut out by one homogeneous equation">
Now let

$$
W = \left\{(x, y, z) \in R^3 : x + 2y + 3z = 0\right\}.
$$

Again we check the definition.

1. `W` is nonempty because `(0, 0, 0)` satisfies the equation.
2. If `u = (u_1, u_2, u_3)` and `v = (v_1, v_2, v_3)` lie in `W`, then

   $$
   u_1 + 2u_2 + 3u_3 = 0,
   \qquad
   v_1 + 2v_2 + 3v_3 = 0.
   $$

   Adding these equations gives

   $$
   (u_1 + v_1) + 2(u_2 + v_2) + 3(u_3 + v_3) = 0,
   $$

   so `u + v \in W`.
3. If `u \in W` and `\alpha \in R`, then

   $$
   \alpha u_1 + 2\alpha u_2 + 3\alpha u_3
   =
   \alpha(u_1 + 2u_2 + 3u_3)
   =
   \alpha \cdot 0
   =
   0,
   $$

   so `\alpha u \in W`.

Thus `W` is a subspace of `R^3`.
</WorkedExample>

This example is the model case for a set defined by a **homogeneous linear
equation**. The zero right-hand side is what makes the closure argument work.

## Consequences of the subspace test

Once `W` is known to be a subspace, several facts follow immediately.

<TheoremCard title="Every subspace contains `0` and additive inverses">
Let `W` be a subspace of a vector space `V`. Then:

1. `0 \in W`;
2. if `u \in W`, then `-u \in W`.
</TheoremCard>

<CollapsibleProof title="Proof of the first consequences">
Because `W` is nonempty, choose some `u \in W`. Closure under scalar
multiplication gives

$$
0u = 0 \in W.
$$

Now let `u \in W`. Since `-1 \in R`, closure under scalar multiplication gives

$$
(-1)u = -u \in W.
$$
</CollapsibleProof>

Two immediate examples now become unavoidable:

- `{0}` is a subspace of every vector space, so it is the **smallest**
  subspace;
- `V` itself is a subspace of `V`, so it is the **largest** subspace.

## Null spaces are subspaces

The first important family of subspaces coming from matrix theory is the null
space.

<TheoremCard title="The null space of a matrix is a subspace">
Let `A` be an `m \times n` matrix, and let

$$
N(A) = \left\{x \in R^n : Ax = 0\right\}.
$$

Then `N(A)` is a subspace of `R^n`.
</TheoremCard>

<CollapsibleProof title="Why `N(A)` is a subspace">
First, `0 \in N(A)` because

$$
A0 = 0.
$$

Now let `u, v \in N(A)` and let `\alpha \in R`. Then `Au = 0` and `Av = 0`, so

$$
A(u + v) = Au + Av = 0 + 0 = 0,
$$

and

$$
A(\alpha u) = \alpha(Au) = \alpha \cdot 0 = 0.
$$

Therefore `u + v \in N(A)` and `\alpha u \in N(A)`, so `N(A)` is a subspace of
`R^n`.
</CollapsibleProof>

This theorem explains why the phrase **null space** is not just convenient
terminology. The solution set to a homogeneous system really is a vector space.

<WorkedExample title="A homogeneous equation defines a null space">
Let

$$
A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
N(A)
=
\left\{
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y \\
z
\end{bmatrix}
:
x + 2y + 3z = 0
\right\}.
$$

So the plane from the earlier example is exactly the null space of a matrix.
Solving for `x` gives

$$
x = -2y - 3z,
$$

and therefore every vector in the null space has the form

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
x \\
y \\
z
\end{bmatrix}
=
y \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
+
z \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

So this subspace is a plane through the origin generated by two vectors.
</WorkedExample>

## A broader pattern: matrix conditions that preserve subspaces

The tutorial sheet includes a stronger example than a null space. Instead of
requiring `Ax = 0`, it considers vectors whose images under `A` land inside an
already known subspace.

<WorkedExample title="Preimage of a subspace under matrix multiplication">
Let `A` be a `4 \times 6` matrix, let `V` be a subspace of `R^4`, and define

$$
W = \left\{x \in R^6 : Ax \in V\right\}.
$$

Then `W` is a subspace of `R^6`.

The proof is a direct application of the subspace test:

1. `0 \in W` because `A0 = 0`, and `0 \in V`;
2. if `x, y \in W`, then `Ax, Ay \in V`, so

   $$
   A(x + y) = Ax + Ay \in V,
   $$

   which means `x + y \in W`;
3. if `x \in W` and `\alpha \in R`, then `Ax \in V`, so

   $$
   A(\alpha x) = \alpha(Ax) \in V,
   $$

   which means `\alpha x \in W`.

The null space is the special case where `V = \{0\}`.
</WorkedExample>

This viewpoint is worth remembering. Many subspaces are best described not by
listing their elements, but by writing a condition that is preserved by linear
operations.

## Common ways a subset fails

The subspace test is short, but it is unforgiving. A set can fail in more than
one way.

<CommonMistake title="Containing `0` is necessary, but not sufficient">
The set

$$
\left\{(x, y) \in R^2 : x + y = 1\right\}
$$

is not a subspace because it does not contain `(0, 0)`.

But failing to contain `0` is not the only danger. The tutorial also gives a
quadratic example

$$
S = \left\{x \in R^4 : x^T C x = 0\right\},
$$

which may contain `0` and still fail to be a subspace. The reason is that for
`w = u + v`,

$$
w^T C w = u^T C u + v^T C v + 2u^T C v,
$$

and the cross term `2u^T C v` need not vanish. So closure under addition can
break even when a set looks symmetric and still contains `0`.
</CommonMistake>

In practice, the safest habit is this:

1. check `0`;
2. check addition;
3. check scalar multiplication.

Do not rely on pictures or intuition alone.

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is `E = \{f(x) \in P_n : f(1) = 0\}` a subspace of `P_n`?">
Use the value at `x = 1` as the quantity that has to stay unchanged under the
subspace test.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Solution">
The zero polynomial belongs to `E` because `0(1) = 0`. If `f, g \in E`, then
`f(1) = g(1) = 0`, so

$$
(f + g)(1) = f(1) + g(1) = 0.
$$

If `\alpha \in R` and `f \in E`, then

$$
(\alpha f)(1) = \alpha f(1) = \alpha \cdot 0 = 0.
$$

So `E` is closed under addition and scalar multiplication, hence it is a
subspace of `P_n`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Let `W = \{X \in M_{p,p}(R) : AX - XA = O_{p \times p}\}`. Why does this set form a subspace?">
Treat the defining equation `AX - XA = O_{p \times p}` the same way you treated
`Ax = 0`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Solution">
The zero matrix belongs to `W` because

$$
A O_{p \times p} - O_{p \times p} A = O_{p \times p}.
$$

If `X, Y \in W` and `\kappa, \lambda \in R`, then

$$
A(\kappa X + \lambda Y) - (\kappa X + \lambda Y)A
=
\kappa(AX - XA) + \lambda(AY - YA)
=
O_{p \times p}.
$$

So `\kappa X + \lambda Y` also belongs to `W`, and the equivalent subspace test
applies.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is `\{(x, y) \in R^2 : x + y = 1\}` not a subspace, even though it is still a line?">
Answer this without drawing a graph.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Solution">
It fails at the first necessary step: the zero vector is not in the set, because

$$
0 + 0 \neq 1.
$$

Since every subspace must contain `0`, this line cannot be a subspace.
</RevealSolution>
