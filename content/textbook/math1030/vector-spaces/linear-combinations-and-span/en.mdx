## Building new vectors

A linear combination is what you get when you multiply vectors by scalars and
add the results.

<Definition title="Linear combination">
Let `u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_n` be vectors in a vector space `V`, and let
`\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots, \alpha_n` be scalars. Then

$$
\alpha_1 u_1 + \alpha_2 u_2 + \cdots + \alpha_n u_n
$$

is a linear combination of `u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_n`.
</Definition>

The important part is that every coefficient is allowed to be any real number,
including `0`.

<WorkedExample title="A vector can be written as a combination">
In the notes,

$$
u_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix},
\qquad
u_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -5 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix},
\qquad
w = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -6 \\ -16 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

The example shows that `w = 8u_1 - 2u_2`.

So `w` is a linear combination of `u_1` and `u_2`.
</WorkedExample>

## What span means

If you take all linear combinations of a list of vectors, you get their span.

<Definition title="Span">
The span of `u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_n` is the set of all linear combinations of
those vectors. It is written as `Span{u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_n}`.
</Definition>

The span is the collection of everything you can build from the given vectors.

Example-wise, the notes show that `Span{e_1, e_2}` is the `x_1x_2`-plane in
`R^3`, while `Span{e_1, e_2, e_3} = R^3`.

## Why span is useful

The notes also connect span to subspaces: the span of any collection of vectors
is always a subspace.

<CollapsibleProof title="Why the span is a subspace">
Take any vectors `v` and `w` in `Span{u_1, \ldots, u_n}`.

By definition, each of them is a linear combination of `u_1, \ldots, u_n`.
Adding those combinations gives another linear combination, and scaling one of
them by a real number also gives another linear combination.

So the span is closed under addition and scalar multiplication. Therefore it is
a subspace.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Span is not just positive multiples">
Span allows all real coefficients, not only positive ones. It also includes the
zero combination, so `0` is always in the span.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="Is the zero vector always in the span of any list of vectors?">
Think about what happens when every coefficient is `0`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Yes. The all-zero coefficients give the zero vector.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Does `Span{e_1, e_2}` in `R^3` contain `(2, -1, 0)`?">
Use the geometric picture of the `x_1x_2`-plane.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Yes. The vector has third coordinate `0`, so it lies in that plane.
</RevealSolution>
