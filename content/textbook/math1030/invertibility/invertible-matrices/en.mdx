## What invertible means

A square matrix is invertible if there is another matrix that undoes it.
If `A` is invertible, then `A^{-1}` exists and

$$
AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I.
$$

<Definition title="Invertible matrix">
An invertible matrix has a matrix inverse, so multiplying by that inverse
recovers the identity matrix.
</Definition>

## How to think about it

Invertibility is not just a symbol game. It tells you whether the matrix has a
reversible effect.
That is why row reduction is so useful: it lets you test whether the matrix can
be turned into the identity matrix.

<WorkedExample title="Read invertibility from row reduction">
Suppose row operations transform `A` into `I`.
Then the same operations applied to `[A | I]` produce `[I | A^{-1}]`.

This is the practical reason we keep row-reducing augmented blocks in the later
pages of the course.
</WorkedExample>

Step through `[A | I]` below and watch what you are really waiting for. The key
moment is not “many operations happened,” but “the left block became `I`.”

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Simple-looking is not the same as invertible">
Do not guess from appearance alone. The real test is whether the matrix can be
reduced to the identity.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="If `A` is invertible, what is `AA^{-1}`?">
Use the defining property of an inverse.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It is the identity matrix `I`.
</RevealSolution>
