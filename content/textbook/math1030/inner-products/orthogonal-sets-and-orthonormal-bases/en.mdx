---
title: "9.2 Orthogonal sets and orthonormal bases"
description: "Use orthogonality to build orthogonal and orthonormal bases, then read coefficients without solving a linear system every time."
---

Once an inner product is available, you can single out vector families whose
geometry is especially simple. Orthogonal sets are the first major example.

They matter because orthogonality replaces elimination. In an orthogonal basis,
coordinates can be read off directly from inner products instead of solving a
new linear system every time.

## Orthogonality

<Definition title="Orthogonal vectors">
Two vectors `v,w\in\mathbb{R}^m` are **orthogonal** if

$$
\langle v,w\rangle=0.
$$

This is written as

$$
v\perp w.
$$
</Definition>

The point is not merely that the vectors look perpendicular in a picture. The
definition works in every dimension because it is stated algebraically through
the inner product.

<WorkedExample title="Check orthogonality by inner product">
Let

$$
v=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\2\\3
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
w=
\begin{bmatrix}
-1\\-1\\1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
\langle v,w\rangle=1(-1)+2(-1)+3(1)=0.
$$

So `v` and `w` are orthogonal.
</WorkedExample>

## Orthogonal sets

<Definition title="Orthogonal set">
A finite set `S=\{v_1,\dots,v_k\}` in `\mathbb{R}^m` is **orthogonal** if:

1. every vector in the set is nonzero;
2. `v_i\perp v_j` whenever `i\neq j`.
</Definition>

The nonzero requirement matters. If zero were allowed, every set containing `0`
would be vacuously orthogonal, which would destroy the structural theorems.

<TheoremCard title="Orthogonal nonzero sets are automatically linearly independent">
If `S=\{v_1,\dots,v_k\}` is an orthogonal subset of `\mathbb{R}^m`, then
`S` is linearly independent.
</TheoremCard>

<CollapsibleProof title="Why orthogonal sets are independent">
Suppose

$$
\alpha_1v_1+\cdots+\alpha_kv_k=0.
$$

Take the inner product with `v_i`. Every cross term disappears because the set
is orthogonal:

$$
0=\left\langle \alpha_1v_1+\cdots+\alpha_kv_k,\;v_i\right\rangle
=\alpha_i\langle v_i,v_i\rangle
=\alpha_i\|v_i\|^2.
$$

Since `v_i\neq0`, we have `\|v_i\|^2>0`, so `\alpha_i=0`. This holds for every
`i`, so the relation is trivial.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Coordinates in an orthogonal basis

Orthogonality is powerful because it makes coordinates explicit.

<TheoremCard title="Coefficient formula in an orthogonal set">
Suppose `S=\{v_1,\dots,v_k\}` is an orthogonal set and

$$
v=\alpha_1v_1+\cdots+\alpha_kv_k.
$$

Then for each `i`,

$$
\alpha_i=\frac{\langle v,v_i\rangle}{\|v_i\|^2}.
$$

Hence

$$
v=
\frac{\langle v,v_1\rangle}{\|v_1\|^2}v_1
+\cdots+
\frac{\langle v,v_k\rangle}{\|v_k\|^2}v_k.
$$
</TheoremCard>

This formula is one of the main practical rewards of orthogonality. There is no
need to solve a system to recover the coefficients.

<WorkedExample title="Recover coordinates from inner products">
Let

$$
v_1=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\1\\1
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
v_2=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\-1\\0
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
v_3=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\1\\-2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

This is an orthogonal set. Let

$$
v=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\2\\3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
\alpha_1=\frac{\langle v,v_1\rangle}{\|v_1\|^2}
=\frac{6}{3}=2,
$$

$$
\alpha_2=\frac{\langle v,v_2\rangle}{\|v_2\|^2}
=\frac{-1}{2}=-\frac12,
$$

$$
\alpha_3=\frac{\langle v,v_3\rangle}{\|v_3\|^2}
=\frac{-3}{6}=-\frac12.
$$

So

$$
v=2v_1-\frac12v_2-\frac12v_3.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Orthogonal and orthonormal bases

<Definition title="Orthogonal basis">
Let `V` be a subspace of `\mathbb{R}^m`. A subset `S` of `V` is an
**orthogonal basis** for `V` if `S` is both:

1. a basis of `V`;
2. an orthogonal set.
</Definition>

Because orthogonal nonzero sets are automatically linearly independent, to check
that an orthogonal set is an orthogonal basis you only need to check that it
spans the subspace.

<TheoremCard title="Orthogonal basis criterion">
Let `V` be a subspace of `\mathbb{R}^m`. If `S` is an orthogonal subset of `V`,
then `S` is an orthogonal basis for `V` if and only if `S` spans `V`.
</TheoremCard>

<Definition title="Orthonormal basis">
A set `S=\{v_1,\dots,v_k\}` is **orthonormal** if it is orthogonal and every
vector has norm `1`. Equivalently,

$$
\langle v_i,v_j\rangle=
\begin{cases}
1,& i=j,\\
0,& i\neq j.
\end{cases}
$$

An orthonormal set that is also a basis is an **orthonormal basis**.
</Definition>

For orthonormal bases, the coordinate formula becomes even cleaner.

<TheoremCard title="Coordinate formula in an orthonormal basis">
If `S=\{v_1,\dots,v_k\}` is an orthonormal set and

$$
v\in\operatorname{span}\{v_1,\dots,v_k\},
$$

then

$$
v=\langle v,v_1\rangle v_1+\cdots+\langle v,v_k\rangle v_k.
$$
</TheoremCard>

The denominators disappear because every norm is `1`.

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Orthogonal does not mean 'already a basis'">
An orthogonal set is automatically linearly independent, but it may still fail
to span the space you care about. Orthogonality gives independence for free; it
does not give spanning for free.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="Can an orthogonal set contain the zero vector?">
Use the definition, not only the equation `⟨0,v⟩=0`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. By definition, an orthogonal set requires every vector in the set to be
nonzero.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why are the standard basis vectors e_1,\dots,e_m orthogonal?">
Compute their pairwise inner products.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
If `i\neq j`, then `e_i^Te_j=0` because the `1` entries occur in different
coordinates. So the standard basis vectors are pairwise orthogonal.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If an orthonormal basis is used, what is the coefficient of v_i in the expansion of v?">
Use the orthonormal coordinate formula.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It is `\langle v,v_i\rangle`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Show that `\{(1,2),(-2,1)\}` is an orthogonal basis of `\mathbb{R}^2`.">
Check orthogonality first, then spanning.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The inner product is

$$
(1,2)\cdot(-2,1)=-2+2=0,
$$

so the vectors are orthogonal. Since there are two nonzero orthogonal vectors in
`\mathbb{R}^2`, they are linearly independent and therefore form a basis of
`\mathbb{R}^2`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Let `u_1=(1,0,1)` and `u_2=(1,-2,1)`. Find the coefficients of `v=(2,-2,2)` in this orthogonal basis.">
Use the orthogonal coefficient formula directly.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
First compute the norms:

$$
\|u_1\|^2=2,\qquad \|u_2\|^2=6.
$$

Then

$$
\alpha_1=\frac{\langle v,u_1\rangle}{\|u_1\|^2}
=\frac{4}{2}=2,
\qquad
\alpha_2=\frac{\langle v,u_2\rangle}{\|u_2\|^2}
=\frac{6}{6}=1.
$$

So

$$
v=2u_1+u_2.
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Normalize the orthogonal basis `\{(3,4),(4,-3)\}`.">
Divide each vector by its norm.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Each vector has norm `5`, so an orthonormal basis is

$$
\left\{
\left(\frac35,\frac45\right),
\left(\frac45,-\frac35\right)
\right\}.
$$
</RevealSolution>
