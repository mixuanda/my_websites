Up to this point, most of the course has studied linear structure: span,
independence, basis, eigenvectors, and diagonalization. Inner products add
geometry. They let you talk about length, perpendicularity, and angle inside
the same vector spaces.

The key idea is that geometry can be encoded algebraically by one scalar-valued
operation.

## Why this section matters

When you write

$$
\langle v,w\rangle=v^Tw,
$$

you are not introducing a new symbol for decoration. You are introducing the
operation that later controls orthogonality, projection, orthonormal bases, and
the inequalities that make Euclidean geometry work in `\mathbb{R}^m`.

<Definition title="Standard inner product">
For vectors `v,w\in\mathbb{R}^m`, the **inner product** of `v` and `w` is

$$
\langle v,w\rangle
=\sum_{i=1}^m v_iw_i
=v^Tw.
$$

It is also called the **dot product**.
</Definition>

<WorkedExample title="Compute inner products directly">
If

$$
v=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\2
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
w=
\begin{bmatrix}
3\\4
\end{bmatrix},
$$

then

$$
\langle v,w\rangle=1\cdot3+2\cdot4=11.
$$

If

$$
u=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\2\\3
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
z=
\begin{bmatrix}
4\\5\\6
\end{bmatrix},
$$

then

$$
\langle u,z\rangle=1\cdot4+2\cdot5+3\cdot6=32.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Basic inner-product properties

<TheoremCard title="Linearity, symmetry, and positivity">
For vectors `u,v,w\in\mathbb{R}^m` and scalars `\alpha,\beta\in\mathbb{R}`,
the standard inner product satisfies:

1.

$$
\langle v+w,u\rangle=\langle v,u\rangle+\langle w,u\rangle;
$$

2.

$$
\langle \alpha v,w\rangle=\alpha\langle v,w\rangle;
$$

3.

$$
\langle v,w\rangle=\langle w,v\rangle;
$$

4.

$$
\langle v,v\rangle\ge0,
$$

and equality holds if and only if `v=0`.
</TheoremCard>

The first two properties say the inner product is linear. The third says it is
symmetric. The fourth says the inner product measures genuine size rather than
an arbitrary signed quantity.

<CollapsibleProof title="Why positivity is the key structural axiom">
By definition,

$$
\langle v,v\rangle=v_1^2+v_2^2+\cdots+v_m^2.
$$

A sum of squares cannot be negative. It equals `0` only when every square is
`0`, which means every coordinate of `v` is `0`.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Norm and unit vectors

<Definition title="Norm">
For `v\in\mathbb{R}^m`, the **norm** of `v` is

$$
\|v\|=\sqrt{\langle v,v\rangle}.
$$

So the norm is the length induced by the inner product.
</Definition>

<TheoremCard title="Basic properties of the norm">
For every `v\in\mathbb{R}^m` and every scalar `\alpha\in\mathbb{R}`:

1.

$$
\|v\|\ge0,
$$

and `\|v\|=0` if and only if `v=0`;

2.

$$
\|\alpha v\|=|\alpha|\,\|v\|.
$$
</TheoremCard>

<Definition title="Unit vector">
A vector `v` is a **unit vector** if

$$
\|v\|=1.
$$

If `v\neq0`, then

$$
\frac{v}{\|v\|}
$$

is the normalization of `v` to a unit vector.
</Definition>

<WorkedExample title="Compute a norm and normalize">
Let

$$
v=
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\2\\3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
\|v\|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2+3^2}=\sqrt{14}.
$$

So the corresponding unit vector is

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{14}}
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\2\\3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Inner product and norm determine each other

The inner product determines the norm immediately through
`\|v\|^2=\langle v,v\rangle`. The reverse relationship is also important.

<TheoremCard title="Conversion formulas">
For vectors `u,v\in\mathbb{R}^m`:

1.

$$
\|u\pm v\|^2=\|u\|^2+\|v\|^2\pm2\langle u,v\rangle;
$$

2. the polarization identity is

$$
\langle u,v\rangle=\frac12\bigl(\|u+v\|^2-\|u\|^2-\|v\|^2\bigr);
$$

3. the parallelogram identity is

$$
\|u+v\|^2+\|u-v\|^2=2\|u\|^2+2\|v\|^2.
$$
</TheoremCard>

These formulas explain why inner-product geometry is so rigid: once you know
how length behaves, the inner product can be recovered from it.

## Angles between vectors

In `\mathbb{R}^2` and `\mathbb{R}^3`, the inner product can be rewritten in the
familiar geometric form

$$
\langle x,y\rangle=\|x\|\,\|y\|\cos\theta,
$$

where `\theta` is the angle between the nonzero vectors `x` and `y`.

So

$$
\theta=\arccos\!\left(\frac{\langle x,y\rangle}{\|x\|\,\|y\|}\right).
$$

In particular, `x` and `y` are perpendicular exactly when

$$
\langle x,y\rangle=0.
$$

This observation becomes the definition of orthogonality in arbitrary
`\mathbb{R}^m`.

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="The inner product is a number, not a vector">
Students sometimes see `v^Tw` and think the result is another vector because it
comes from multiplying vectors. It is not. The inner product always produces a
single scalar. That is why it can be used to measure angle and length.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="What is `\langle (1,2),(3,4)\rangle`?">
Multiply matching coordinates and add.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`1\cdot3+2\cdot4=11`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If `\|v\|=0`, what must v be?">
Use the positivity theorem for the norm.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`v=0`. A vector has norm `0` exactly when it is the zero vector.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If `v\neq0`, why is `v/\|v\|` a unit vector?">
Apply the scalar rule for norms.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">

$$
\left\|\frac{v}{\|v\|}\right\|
=\frac{1}{\|v\|}\|v\|
=1.
$$
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Compute the norm of `\begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix}`.">
Use `\|v\|=\sqrt{\langle v,v\rangle}`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">

$$
\left\|
\begin{bmatrix}
3\\4
\end{bmatrix}
\right\|
=\sqrt{3^2+4^2}
=5.
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Normalize `\begin{bmatrix}2\\-2\\1\end{bmatrix}` to a unit vector.">
First compute its norm.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The norm is

$$
\sqrt{2^2+(-2)^2+1^2}=\sqrt9=3.
$$

So the normalized vector is

$$
\frac13
\begin{bmatrix}
2\\-2\\1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Suppose nonzero vectors x and y satisfy `\langle x,y\rangle=0`. What can you say about their angle in `\mathbb{R}^2` or `\mathbb{R}^3`?">
Use the cosine formula.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Since

$$
\langle x,y\rangle=\|x\|\,\|y\|\cos\theta
$$

and both norms are nonzero, we get `\cos\theta=0`. Hence `\theta=90^\circ`.
</RevealSolution>
