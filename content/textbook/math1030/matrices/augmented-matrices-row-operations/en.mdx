## From equations to rows

When we write a linear system as a matrix, each equation becomes one row.
The coefficients go first, and the constants go in the last column.

That full table is called an augmented matrix.

<Definition title="Augmented matrix">
An augmented matrix places the coefficient matrix and the constant column in one
table so you can row-reduce the whole system at once.
</Definition>

## The three row operations

The local notes use three elementary row operations:

1. swap two rows,
2. multiply a row by a nonzero number,
3. add a multiple of one row to another row.

These operations preserve the solution set, so they are safe for solving a
system.

<WorkedExample title="Turn a system into an augmented matrix">
Consider

$$
x + 2y = 5, \qquad 3x - y = 4.
$$

Its augmented matrix is

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & | & 5 \\
3 & -1 & | & 4
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Now use the row operation `R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 3R_1` to begin eliminating the
`x`-term in the second row.
</WorkedExample>

## Why the row operations are allowed

Row operations are just a matrix version of equivalent equation operations.
They do not change which number lists satisfy the system.

to see that editing a row means editing one equation in a controlled way, not

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Do not change only the constants">
If you change one side of an equation but not the other, you are no longer
working with an equivalent system.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="Which row operation keeps the solution set unchanged?">
Think about swapping rows, scaling a row by `0`, or adding one row to another.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Swapping rows and adding a multiple of one row to another are safe, and scaling
a row is safe only when the multiplier is nonzero.
</RevealSolution>
