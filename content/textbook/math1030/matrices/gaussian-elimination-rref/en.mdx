## The goal of elimination

Gaussian elimination is a way to simplify an augmented matrix by row
operations until the solution becomes easy to read.

The point is not to perform random moves. Each move should make the structure
clearer.

<Definition title="RREF">
Reduced row-echelon form, or RREF, is the simplified matrix form that makes pivots
and free variables easy to read.
</Definition>

## What to look for

In a row-reduced matrix, you want to see the pivots, the free variables, and any
contradiction row such as `[0 0 0 | 1]`.

That last kind of row tells you the system has no solution.

## A simple example

<WorkedExample title="Start elimination in a small system">
Take the augmented matrix

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & | & 5 \\
3 & -1 & | & 4
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Use `R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 3R_1` to remove the first entry below the pivot:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & | & 5 \\
0 & -7 & | & -11
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

From here you can continue until the matrix is in RREF.
</WorkedExample>

Pause here and step through one elimination path. After each move, ask which
entry became easier to read and why that move was chosen.

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="REF is not yet RREF">
It is easy to stop too early. In RREF, each pivot column has zeros everywhere
else, not just below the pivot.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="Is `[0 0 0 | 1]` a harmless row?">
Think about what equation that row represents.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. It means `0 = 1`, which is a contradiction, so the system has no solution.
</RevealSolution>
