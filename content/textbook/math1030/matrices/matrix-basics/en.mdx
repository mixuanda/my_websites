Matrices first appear in the course as a convenient way to record a system of
linear equations. Very quickly, though, they become objects in their own right.
To work rigorously later, you need to separate several ideas that beginners
often blur together:

- what a matrix is;
- when two matrices are equal;
- which operations are defined before multiplication even enters the story;
- how a matrix records the data of a linear system.

This note lays down that language carefully.

## What a matrix is

<Definition title="Matrix">
A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers arranged in rows and columns.
</Definition>

If a matrix has `m` rows and `n` columns, we say it is an `m \times n` matrix.
The entries are usually real numbers in this course, although the formal
definitions make sense over other number systems as well.

The point of the rectangular format is not decoration. A row records one line
of data, a column records another line of data, and the position of an entry
matters. Later, multiplication and row operations will depend on that position.

## Read the size before you touch the entries

The size of a matrix is written as `m × n`.

- `m` is the number of rows.
- `n` is the number of columns.

If `m = n`, the matrix is square.

Two matrices with different sizes are different kinds of objects. A `2 \times 3`
matrix and a `3 \times 2` matrix are not even comparable entry by entry, because
their row-column positions do not match.

## Read entries one by one

If `A` is a matrix, then `a_{ij}` means the entry in row `i` and column `j`.
That notation matters because it tells you exactly where a number lives inside
the array.

<WorkedExample title="Reading a matrix carefully">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & 0 \\
3 & -1 & 4
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

This matrix has 2 rows and 3 columns, so its size is `2 × 3`.
Its entry in row 2, column 3 is `4`.
</WorkedExample>

The notation `a_{ij}` is not optional bookkeeping. It is the language used in
definitions such as matrix equality, matrix addition, and matrix multiplication.

## Matrix equality is entrywise

Two matrices are equal only when they have the same size and every
corresponding entry matches.

<Definition title="Matrix equality">
Let `A = [a_{ij}]` and `B = [b_{ij}]` be matrices.

Then `A = B` if and only if:

1. `A` and `B` have the same size, and
2. `a_{ij} = b_{ij}` for every row index `i` and column index `j`.
</Definition>

This means that proving two matrices are equal is often an entry-by-entry
argument.

<WorkedExample title="Using matrix equality to solve for an unknown entry">
Suppose

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
3 & x
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
3 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The two matrices already agree in three positions. Equality of matrices forces
the remaining position to agree as well, so `x = 5`.
</WorkedExample>

## Addition and scalar multiplication come first

Before matrix multiplication appears, there are two basic operations you should
already read confidently.

<Definition title="Addition and scalar multiplication">
Let `A = [a_{ij}]` and `B = [b_{ij}]` be matrices of the same size, and let `c`
be a scalar.

- The sum `A + B` is the matrix obtained by adding corresponding entries:
  `(A + B)_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}`.
- The scalar multiple `cA` is the matrix obtained by multiplying every entry of
  `A` by `c`: `(cA)_{ij} = c a_{ij}`.
</Definition>

The phrase "of the same size" is essential. Matrix addition is not defined for
matrices of different sizes.

<WorkedExample title="Compute a sum and a scalar multiple">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -2 \\
0 & 3
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 1 \\
-5 & 2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A + B =
\begin{bmatrix}
5 & -1 \\
-5 & 5
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
2A =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -4 \\
0 & 6
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Every entry is handled separately, but the size stays `2 \times 2`.
</WorkedExample>

The zero matrix is the matrix whose entries are all `0`. For each size it plays
the role of the additive identity:

$$
A + O = A.
$$

## A matrix records a linear system compactly

One reason matrices matter so early is that they package a linear system in a
form that is easier to transform systematically.

Consider the system

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_1 + 2x_2 - x_3 &= 4, \\
3x_1 - x_2 + 5x_3 &= 7.
\end{aligned}
$$

Its coefficient matrix is

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & -1 \\
3 & -1 & 5
\end{bmatrix},
$$

its unknown vector is

$$
x =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3
\end{bmatrix},
$$

and its constant vector is

$$
b =
\begin{bmatrix}
4 \\ 7
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So the whole system can be recorded as

$$
Ax = b.
$$

This compact form is not a shortcut that hides meaning. It gathers the same
coefficients, variables, and constants into an object that later supports row
operations, null-space language, and invertibility tests.

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="Do not swap rows and columns">
The first index is the row number, not the column number. `a_{23}` means row 2,
column 3.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Different sizes cannot be added">
Matrix addition is defined entrywise. If the positions do not line up, there is
no operation to perform.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="Can a `2 × 3` matrix be added to a `3 × 2` matrix?">
Answer from the definition of matrix addition, not from visual guesswork.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. Matrix addition is only defined for matrices of the same size.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If `A` is a `4 × 2` matrix, what does the symbol `a_{31}` mean?">
Name both the row and the column.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It is the entry in row 3 and column 1 of `A`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercise

<QuickCheck prompt="Write the coefficient matrix and constant vector for the system `x_1 - x_2 = 3`, `2x_1 + x_2 = 0`.">
Keep the order of the variables fixed.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Using the variable order `(x_1, x_2)`, the coefficient matrix is

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1 \\
2 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
$$

and the constant vector is

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
3 \\ 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
</RevealSolution>
