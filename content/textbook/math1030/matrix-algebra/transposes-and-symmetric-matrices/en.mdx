The transpose is a simple operation with big consequences. It swaps rows and
columns, but that small move lets you define several important matrix families
and prove useful algebraic rules.

This note has two goals:

1. understand what `A^T` really does;
2. learn how to recognize symmetric and skew-symmetric matrices from the
   diagonal outward.

## Intuition first: reflect across the main diagonal

When you transpose a matrix, the entry in row `i`, column `j` moves to row `j`,
column `i`.

The easiest picture is a reflection across the main diagonal. Entries on the
diagonal stay where they are. Entries off the diagonal trade places with their
partners on the other side.

That is why symmetry questions are always about comparing pairs like `a_{12}`
and `a_{21}`.

## Definitions

<Definition title="Transpose, symmetric matrix, and skew-symmetric matrix">
If `A` is an `m × n` matrix, then its transpose `A^T` is the `n × m` matrix
defined by

$$
[A^T]_{ij} = [A]_{ji}.
$$

A square matrix is **symmetric** if `A^T = A`.

A square matrix is **skew-symmetric** if `A^T = -A`.
</Definition>

Notice the word **square**. If `A` is not square, then `A` and `A^T` do not
even have the same size, so the equations `A^T = A` and `A^T = -A` cannot be
true.

## Worked example

<WorkedExample title="Read symmetry from matching entries">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 & 4 \\
-1 & 3 & 0 \\
4 & 0 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 & 4 \\
-1 & 3 & 0 \\
4 & 0 & 5
\end{bmatrix}
= A.
$$

So `A` is symmetric.

Now consider

$$
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & 2 & -1 \\
-2 & 0 & 5 \\
1 & -5 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then each off-diagonal entry changes sign when reflected across the diagonal,
and every diagonal entry is `0`. Therefore `B^T = -B`, so `B` is
skew-symmetric.
</WorkedExample>

## Why diagonal entries matter in the skew-symmetric case

If `A` is skew-symmetric, then `A^T = -A`.

Look at a diagonal entry `a_{ii}`. Transposing does not move it, so the rule
forces

$$
a_{ii} = -a_{ii}.
$$

That means

$$
2a_{ii} = 0,
$$

so every diagonal entry must be `0`.

This is one of the fastest ways to reject a candidate skew-symmetric matrix:
if even one diagonal entry is nonzero, the matrix cannot be skew-symmetric.

<TheoremCard title="Transpose reverses multiplication order">
Whenever the product `AB` is defined,

$$
(AB)^T = B^T A^T.
$$
</TheoremCard>

This rule is important because transpose does not merely act on each matrix
separately. It also reverses the order of multiplication. Students often forget
that reversal.

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="Thinking every square matrix is symmetric">
Being square is only the first requirement. You still need the reflected
entries to agree.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Forgetting that skew-symmetric means a sign change">
For symmetry you compare equal entries. For skew-symmetry you compare opposite
entries with opposite signs.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Writing (AB)^T = A^T B^T">
Transpose reverses the multiplication order. The correct formula is
`(AB)^T = B^T A^T`.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Why must a symmetric or skew-symmetric matrix be square?">
Answer using the sizes of `A` and `A^T`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
If `A` is `m × n`, then `A^T` is `n × m`, so `A` can equal `A^T` or `-A` only
when `m = n`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What must the diagonal entries of a skew-symmetric matrix be?">
Give the exact value.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Every diagonal entry must be `0`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Explain in words why `A + A^T` is always symmetric.">
You do not need a full formal proof, but you must mention transpose.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
If you transpose `A + A^T`, you get
`(A + A^T)^T = A^T + (A^T)^T = A^T + A = A + A^T`, so the matrix equals its own
transpose and is therefore symmetric.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Explain in words why `A - A^T` is always skew-symmetric.">
Again, focus on what happens after taking the transpose.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
If you transpose `A - A^T`, you get
`(A - A^T)^T = A^T - A = -(A - A^T)`, so the transpose is the negative of the
original matrix.
</RevealSolution>
