This note is the point where matrices stop being only containers and start
behaving like objects you can calculate with. The first three operations are
the easiest ones: add two matrices, subtract one matrix from another, and
multiply a matrix by a scalar.

The key beginner idea is simple:

- Addition compares matching positions.
- Subtraction compares matching positions.
- Scalar multiplication keeps the same shape and rescales every entry.

If the matching positions do not exist, the operation is not defined.

## Intuition first: same shape is not a decoration

When you add ordinary numbers, you do not worry about shape. Matrices are
different. A matrix records information in a particular row-and-column layout,
so the location of each entry matters.

If two matrices have different sizes, then row 2, column 3 in one matrix may
have no matching partner in the other matrix. In that case, entrywise addition
or subtraction cannot even begin.

This is why students often hear the phrase "same size" before they see any
formula. The size condition is part of the meaning of the operation.

## Definitions

<Definition title="Matrix addition and scalar multiplication">
Suppose `A` and `B` are both `m × n` matrices.

- The sum `A + B` is the `m × n` matrix obtained by adding corresponding
  entries.
- The difference `A - B` is defined by `A + (-B)`.
- If `c` is a scalar, then `cA` is the `m × n` matrix obtained by multiplying
  every entry of `A` by `c`.
</Definition>

In symbols, if `A = [a_{ij}]` and `B = [b_{ij}]`, then

$$
[A + B]_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}, \qquad [cA]_{ij} = c a_{ij}.
$$

<Definition title="Zero matrix and additive inverse">
The zero matrix is the matrix whose entries are all `0`. It plays the same role
for matrix addition that the number `0` plays for ordinary addition.

The additive inverse of `A` is the matrix `-A`, obtained by negating every
entry of `A`.
</Definition>

## What subtraction really means

Subtraction is not a completely new rule. It is just addition after you first
build the additive inverse.

That means:

$$
A - B = A + (-B).
$$

This is worth saying out loud because it explains several later algebraic
patterns. Whenever you are unsure how to handle subtraction, rewrite it as
addition with negatives and the structure becomes clearer.

## Worked example

<WorkedExample title="Compute each operation carefully">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
0 & -1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
3 & 1 \\
-2 & 4
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A + B =
\begin{bmatrix}
1+3 & 2+1 \\
0+(-2) & -1+4
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 3 \\
-2 & 3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Also,

$$
A - B =
\begin{bmatrix}
1-3 & 2-1 \\
0-(-2) & -1-4
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
-2 & 1 \\
2 & -5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

If `c = -2`, then

$$
-2A =
\begin{bmatrix}
-2 & -4 \\
0 & 2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Why the zero matrix matters

The zero matrix is not just a boring special case. It tells you what the
"do-nothing" matrix for addition looks like.

If `O` is the zero matrix of the same size as `A`, then

$$
A + O = A.
$$

This matters later because every algebraic system needs an identity element for
its addition. For matrices, that identity depends on the size. A `2 × 2` zero
matrix cannot replace a `3 × 3` zero matrix.

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="Checking the entries but forgetting the size">
Even if two matrices contain many similar numbers, you cannot add them unless
their sizes match exactly.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Thinking scalar multiplication changes the size">
Multiplying by a scalar changes the entries, not the shape. If `A` is `m × n`,
then `cA` is still `m × n`.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is matrix addition only defined for matrices of the same size?">
Answer in one sentence using the phrase "corresponding entries."
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Matrix addition is entrywise, so each entry of one matrix must have a
corresponding entry in the other matrix.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If `A` is a `3 × 4` matrix, what is the size of `-A`?">
Focus on shape, not on the signs of the entries.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`-A` is still a `3 × 4` matrix.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Let `A` be `2 × 3` and `B` be `3 × 2`. Which of `A + B`, `A - B`, and `2A` are defined?">
State each one separately.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
`A + B` is not defined because the sizes do not match. `A - B` is not defined
for the same reason. `2A` is defined because scalar multiplication only needs
one matrix and one scalar.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is the zero matrix called the additive identity?">
Use the equation that expresses its identity role.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
It is called the additive identity because for every matrix `A` of the same
size, we have `A + O = A`.
</RevealSolution>
