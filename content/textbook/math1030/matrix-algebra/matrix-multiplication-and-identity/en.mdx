description: "Learn when matrix products are defined, how the row-by-column rule works, and why identity matrices matter for systems and inverses."
---

Matrix multiplication is the first matrix operation that genuinely mixes rows
with columns. It is also the operation that lets matrices encode composition,
systems of equations, and later inverse matrices. Because of that, you should
not memorize the rule as a pattern of symbols only. You should know what the
dimensions are doing at each step.

## Why multiplication is more subtle than addition

Addition and scalar multiplication act entry by entry. Matrix multiplication is
different. To compute one output entry, you compare one row of the left matrix
with one column of the right matrix.

That is why dimensions matter so strictly.

<Definition title="When a matrix product is defined">
If `A` is an `m \times n` matrix and `B` is an `n \times p` matrix, then the
product `AB` is defined and is an `m \times p` matrix.

If the number of columns of `A` does not equal the number of rows of `B`, then
the product `AB` is undefined.
</Definition>

The inner dimensions must match. The outer dimensions tell you the size of the
result.

## The row-by-column rule

<Definition title="Matrix multiplication">
Suppose `A = [a_{ij}]` is an `m \times n` matrix and `B = [b_{jk}]` is an
`n \times p` matrix.

Then the `(i,k)` entry of `AB` is

$$
(AB)_{ik} = a_{i1}b_{1k} + a_{i2}b_{2k} + \cdots + a_{in}b_{nk}.
$$

So each output entry is the dot-product-style combination of row `i` of `A`
with column `k` of `B`.
</Definition>

This rule explains three important facts at once:

- multiplication is not entrywise;
- the inner dimensions must match;
- the output entry uses every matched position in the row and column.

<WorkedExample title="Compute a product carefully">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
3 & -1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 0 \\
5 & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then `AB` is defined because both matrices are `2 \times 2`. Its entries are:

$$
(AB)_{11} = 1 \cdot 4 + 2 \cdot 5 = 14,
$$

$$
(AB)_{12} = 1 \cdot 0 + 2 \cdot 1 = 2,
$$

$$
(AB)_{21} = 3 \cdot 4 + (-1) \cdot 5 = 7,
$$

$$
(AB)_{22} = 3 \cdot 0 + (-1) \cdot 1 = -1.
$$

So

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
14 & 2 \\
7 & -1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Matrix-vector multiplication is a system statement

If `x` is a column vector, then `Ax` is a special case of matrix multiplication.
It packages the left-hand sides of a linear system into one object.

For

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & -1 \\
3 & -1 & 5
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
x =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3
\end{bmatrix},
$$

we have

$$
Ax =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 + 2x_2 - x_3 \\
3x_1 - x_2 + 5x_3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So the system `Ax = b` is not merely shorthand. It is a matrix product whose
entries reproduce the equations of the system.

## Identity matrices do nothing, on purpose

<Definition title="Identity matrix">
For each positive integer `n`, the identity matrix `I_n` is the `n \times n`
square matrix with `1` on the main diagonal and `0` everywhere else.
</Definition>

For example,

$$
I_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
I_3 =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The identity matrix matters because it preserves any compatible matrix:

$$
AI_n = A, \qquad I_m A = A
$$

whenever the sizes match.

<WorkedExample title="Why multiplying by the identity changes nothing">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 \\
4 & 3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
AI_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 \\
4 & 3
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 \\
4 & 3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The first column of `AI_2` reproduces the first column of `A`, and the second
column reproduces the second column of `A`.
</WorkedExample>

That is exactly why inverse matrices are defined through the identity later: if
`A^{-1}` exists, then `AA^{-1} = I`.

## Multiplication is usually not commutative

One of the first conceptual shocks in linear algebra is that

$$
AB \ne BA
$$

in general.

Sometimes both products are defined and differ. Sometimes one product is
defined and the other is not. So order matters twice: it matters for meaning,
and it matters for the final answer.

## Read the product by columns as well as by entries

The row-by-column rule is the standard local computation rule, but it is not
the only useful interpretation.

Write the columns of `B` as

$$
B = [b_1\ b_2\ \cdots\ b_p].
$$

Then the product can be read as

$$
AB = [Ab_1\ Ab_2\ \cdots\ Ab_p].
$$

So each column of `AB` is obtained by applying `A` to the corresponding column
of `B`.

<WorkedExample title="One product read column by column">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
3 & -1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 0 \\
5 & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

If

$$
b_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
4 \\ 5
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
b_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\ 1
\end{bmatrix},
$$

then

$$
Ab_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
14 \\ 7
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
Ab_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 \\ -1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Therefore

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
14 & 2 \\
7 & -1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

This is the same answer as the entrywise row-by-column computation. The point
is that matrix multiplication packages several matrix-vector products together.
</WorkedExample>

## Matrix multiplication represents composition

The multiplication rule is not arbitrary. It is the rule that makes matrices
encode linear transformations in sequence.

If a vector `x` is first sent to `Bx`, and then that result is sent to
`A(Bx)`, the combined effect is

$$
(AB)x.
$$

That is why the inner dimensions must match. The output of the first map must
be a valid input for the second one.

<TheoremCard title="Associativity matches repeated composition">
Whenever the products are defined,

$$
A(BC) = (AB)C.
$$

So we may regroup a chain of matrix products without changing the final linear
transformation.
</TheoremCard>

This does **not** mean that order may be changed. Associativity lets us change
parentheses, not the order of the factors themselves.

<WorkedExample title="Grouping may change, but order may not">
Suppose `A` is `2 \times 3`, `B` is `3 \times 4`, and `C` is `4 \times 2`.

Then both `AB` and `BC` are defined, so both `(AB)C` and `A(BC)` make sense,
and associativity says they are equal.

But `BA` is not defined at all, because the inner dimensions `4` and `2` do not
match. So matrix multiplication is associative, but not commutative.
</WorkedExample>

## Standard basis vectors explain why columns behave so cleanly

The standard basis vectors make the column interpretation precise. In `R^n`,
the vector `e_k` has a `1` in position `k` and `0` everywhere else. If `A` is
an `m \times n` matrix, then `Ae_k` is exactly the `k`th column of `A`.

This is why the identity matrix behaves so naturally. The columns of `I_n` are
`e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n`, so right-multiplying by `I_n` simply reproduces the
columns of `A` one by one.

This also explains why a compatible zero matrix on the right forces the product
to be zero: every column of the zero matrix is the zero vector, so every column
of the product is `A0 = 0`.

## The first algebra laws worth remembering

Once multiplication is defined, the next issue is how it interacts with the
other matrix operations you already know.

Whenever the sizes are compatible, matrix multiplication satisfies:

$$
A(B + C) = AB + AC,
\qquad
(A + B)C = AC + BC,
$$

and scalar multiplication may be moved in or out:

$$
(cA)B = c(AB) = A(cB).
$$

The zero matrix is the simplest sanity check for these rules. If `0` is a
compatible zero matrix, then

$$
A0 = 0,
\qquad
0A = 0.
$$

The reason is that every row-by-column product uses only zero entries from the
zero matrix, so every output entry is zero as well.

These identities are basic, but they matter because later arguments about
inverse matrices, row operations, and block-matrix computation assume them
silently. If you do not know them explicitly, longer calculations become much
harder to audit.

## Practice-set patterns that reveal the main traps

The assignment-style problems on matrix algebra usually test more than raw
arithmetic. A product with unknown entries may be asking whether you can read
dimensions, identify which row and column make one output entry, and remember
that matrix factors cannot usually be rearranged.

<WorkedExample title="Recover unknowns from a partially known product">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & 1 & 3 \\
1 & 0 & 2 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
a & b \\
1 & 1 \\
b & a \\
1 & 2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Suppose

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & c \\
3 & d
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The product is defined because `A` is `2 \times 4` and `B` is `4 \times 2`,
so `AB` must be `2 \times 2`. Computing only the entries we need gives

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
a+b+5 & a+b+8 \\
a+2b+1 & 2a+b+2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Comparing the first column with the given matrix gives

$$
a+b+5=1,
\qquad
a+2b+1=3.
$$

Thus

$$
a+b=-4,
\qquad
a+2b=2.
$$

Subtracting the first equation from the second gives `b=6`, and then
`a=-10`. The remaining entries are

$$
c=a+b+8=4,
\qquad
d=2a+b+2=-12.
$$
</WorkedExample>

This kind of problem is not really about multiplying every entry in sight. It
is about extracting the few equations that the known product entries force.

<WorkedExample title="Expand products without pretending matrices commute">
Let `A` and `B` be square matrices of the same size. Then

$$
(5A-B)(2A+3B)
= 5A(2A+3B)-B(2A+3B).
$$

Now distribute on the right:

$$
5A(2A+3B)-B(2A+3B)
=10A^2+15AB-2BA-3B^2.
$$

The middle terms are `15AB` and `-2BA`. They cannot be combined into
`13AB` unless you already know that `AB=BA`.
</WorkedExample>

The same warning explains a common false shortcut:

$$
(A+B)(A-B)
=A^2-AB+BA-B^2.
$$

This equals `A^2-B^2` only under the extra condition `AB=BA`. Real-number
algebra hides this issue because real-number multiplication is commutative.
Matrix algebra does not.

<TheoremCard title="Lower triangular matrices are closed under multiplication">
A `3 \times 3` lower triangular matrix has the shape

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
* & 0 & 0 \\
* & * & 0 \\
* & * & *
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

If `A` and `B` are both lower triangular `3 \times 3` matrices, then `AB` is
also lower triangular.
</TheoremCard>

Here is the reason in one explicit calculation. Write

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
a_1 & 0 & 0 \\
b_1 & c_1 & 0 \\
d_1 & e_1 & f_1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
a_2 & 0 & 0 \\
b_2 & c_2 & 0 \\
d_2 & e_2 & f_2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
a_1a_2 & 0 & 0 \\
b_1a_2+c_1b_2 & c_1c_2 & 0 \\
d_1a_2+e_1b_2+f_1d_2 & e_1c_2+f_1e_2 & f_1f_2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The entries above the diagonal stay zero because every row-by-column product
there is forced to use a zero from one side or the other. This is a useful
example of a *shape property* being preserved by multiplication.

<WorkedExample title="A zero product does not force a zero factor">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & -1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Neither factor is the zero matrix, yet

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \cdot 1 + (-1) \cdot 1
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So matrix multiplication behaves differently from real-number multiplication:
`AB = 0` does not imply `A = 0` or `B = 0`.
</WorkedExample>

<TheoremCard title="The identity matrix is unique">
If `E` is an `n \times n` matrix such that

$$
EA = A \qquad \text{and} \qquad AE = A
$$

for every compatible `n \times n` matrix `A`, then `E = I_n`.
</TheoremCard>

<CollapsibleProof title="Why no second identity matrix can exist">
Take `A = I_n`. Then the defining property of `E` gives `EI_n = I_n`.
But right-multiplying any matrix by `I_n` leaves it unchanged, so `EI_n = E`.
Therefore `E = I_n`.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="Matrix multiplication is not entrywise multiplication">
The entry `(AB)_{ik}` is not `a_{ik}b_{ik}`. It is built from the whole `i`th
row of `A` and the whole `k`th column of `B`.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Defined products can still appear in only one order">
If `A` is `2 \times 3` and `B` is `3 \times 4`, then `AB` is defined but `BA`
is not. Never assume the reverse order makes sense automatically.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Column language belongs to the right-hand factor">
If `B=[b_1\ b_2]`, then `AB=[Ab_1\ Ab_2]`. The columns of the product are
linear combinations of the columns of `A`, using weights from the corresponding
columns of `B`. Do not write `AB=[a_1b_1\ a_2b_2]`; that expression does not
match the definition of matrix multiplication.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Binomial formulas need commutation hypotheses">
The formula `(A+B)^2=A^2+2AB+B^2` is not automatic for matrices. The actual
expansion is

$$
(A+B)^2=A^2+AB+BA+B^2.
$$

You may combine the middle terms only when `AB=BA`.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="If `A` is `2 × 3` and `B` is `3 × 5`, what is the size of `AB`?">
Use the inner dimensions to test whether the product is defined, then read the
outer dimensions.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`AB` is defined and has size `2 × 5`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What does multiplying by `I_n` do to a compatible matrix?">
Answer in one sentence.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It leaves the matrix unchanged.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If the columns of `B` are `b_1` and `b_2`, how should you read the columns of `AB`?">
Use the column interpretation of matrix multiplication.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The columns of `AB` are `Ab_1` and `Ab_2`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="In `(5A-B)(2A+3B)`, what is the coefficient of `BA` after expansion?">
Keep `AB` and `BA` as different terms.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The coefficient of `BA` is `-2`, because the term comes from `-B(2A)`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercise

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does `Ax = 0` always have at least one solution, no matter what `A` is?">
Think of `x` as a column vector.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Take the zero vector `x = 0`. Every entry of `A0` is a linear combination of
the entries of `0`, so every entry is `0`. Hence `A0 = 0`, and the homogeneous
system always has the trivial solution.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Explain why `BA` may be undefined even when `AB` is defined.">
Answer in terms of the inner dimensions, not just by giving one example.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
For `AB` to be defined, the number of columns of `A` must equal the number of
rows of `B`. For `BA` to be defined, the number of columns of `B` must equal
the number of rows of `A`. These are different conditions, so one order may be
legal while the reverse order is not.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Let `A` and `B` be the matrices from the worked example with unknowns. If the first column of `AB` is `[1,3]^T`, find `a` and `b`.">
Use only the equations coming from the first column.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The first column of `AB` gives

$$
a+b+5=1,
\qquad
a+2b+1=3.
$$

So

$$
a+b=-4,
\qquad
a+2b=2.
$$

Subtracting gives `b=6`, and substituting into `a+b=-4` gives `a=-10`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the product of two lower triangular `3 × 3` matrices remain lower triangular?">
Do not multiply every entry. Explain why the entries above the diagonal stay zero.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
An entry above the diagonal has row index smaller than column index. In the
row-by-column sum for such an entry, each term is forced to use either an
above-diagonal zero from the left lower triangular matrix or an above-diagonal
zero from the right lower triangular matrix. Hence every entry above the
diagonal is zero, so the product is lower triangular.
</RevealSolution>
