Once matrix multiplication is available, structure starts to matter. Some
matrices are easier to understand because their entries line up in a special
pattern. The transpose is one of the main tools for detecting that structure.

## Transpose swaps rows and columns

<Definition title="Transpose">
If `A = [a_{ij}]` is an `m \times n` matrix, then its transpose `A^T` is the
`n \times m` matrix whose `(j,i)` entry is `a_{ij}`.

Equivalently,

$$
(A^T)_{ji} = a_{ij}.
$$
</Definition>

So every row of `A` becomes a column of `A^T`, and every column of `A` becomes
a row.

<WorkedExample title="Compute a transpose">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 4 & -2 \\
0 & 3 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
4 & 3 \\
-2 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The `2 \times 3` matrix becomes a `3 \times 2` matrix because rows and columns
swap roles.
</WorkedExample>

Two basic identities are worth learning early:

$$
(A^T)^T = A,
\qquad
(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T.
$$

When products are defined, transpose also reverses the order:

$$
(AB)^T = B^T A^T.
$$

The reversal is not cosmetic. It reflects the fact that the row-column pairing
has been turned around.

## Symmetric and skew-symmetric matrices

Some square matrices agree with their transpose; others differ from it in a
very controlled way.

<Definition title="Symmetric and skew-symmetric matrices">
Let `A` be a square matrix.

- `A` is symmetric if `A^T = A`.
- `A` is skew-symmetric if `A^T = -A`.
</Definition>

For a symmetric matrix, reflecting across the main diagonal changes nothing. For
a skew-symmetric matrix, reflection changes every entry by a minus sign.

<WorkedExample title="Classify two matrices">
The matrix

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 \\
-1 & 3
\end{bmatrix}
$$

is symmetric because transposing it leaves the entries unchanged.

The matrix

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & 4 \\
-4 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
$$

is skew-symmetric because

$$
A^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & -4 \\
4 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
= -A.
$$
</WorkedExample>

Notice that every diagonal entry of a skew-symmetric real matrix must be `0`,
because the diagonal entry must equal its own negative.

## Order matters: commuting versus non-commuting matrices

The course notes emphasize that matrix multiplication is not commutative in
general. Still, some pairs of matrices do commute.

<Definition title="Commuting matrices">
Two square matrices `A` and `B` of the same size commute if

$$
AB = BA.
$$
</Definition>

Commuting matrices are special. They are not the default.

<TheoremCard title="Matrix multiplication is not commutative in general">
There exist square matrices `A` and `B` such that `AB \ne BA`.
</TheoremCard>

<WorkedExample title="A concrete non-commuting pair">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
1 & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 1 \\
1 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
BA =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
1 & 2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So `AB \ne BA`.
</WorkedExample>

This is why you must preserve order in every matrix identity. Reversing factors
changes the statement.

## Special matrices are easier to read because of their shape

Several standard matrix families are defined by the locations of zero entries.
These shapes matter because they simplify later computations.

- A diagonal matrix has nonzero entries allowed only on the main diagonal.
- An upper-triangular matrix has `0` below the main diagonal.
- A lower-triangular matrix has `0` above the main diagonal.

For such matrices, multiplication and invertibility often become easier to
analyze because the zero pattern survives useful operations.

## Block matrices group information on purpose

Sometimes a large matrix is best read as smaller submatrices pasted together.
That is called block notation.

For example,

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
A_{11} & A_{12} \\
A_{21} & A_{22}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

is a block matrix made from four smaller blocks. Block notation is not a new
kind of matrix. It is a disciplined way to see structure inside a large one.

When the sizes match correctly, block addition and block multiplication follow
the same formal patterns as ordinary matrix operations. The advantage is that
you can reason about large matrices one chunk at a time.

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="Do not transpose a product in the same order">
The correct identity is `(AB)^T = B^T A^T`, not `A^T B^T`. The order reverses.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Symmetric does not mean every pair of matrices commutes">
Symmetry is a property of one matrix. Commuting is a property of a pair of
matrices. They answer different questions.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="If `A` is `3 × 2`, what is the size of `A^T`?">
Swap rows and columns.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`A^T` is `2 × 3`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What can you say about the diagonal entries of a real skew-symmetric matrix?">
Use the equation `A^T = -A` on the diagonal.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Every diagonal entry must be `0`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercise

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is every diagonal matrix equal to its transpose?">
Answer from the location of its possible nonzero entries.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
In a diagonal matrix, every off-diagonal entry is already `0`. Transposing
swaps off-diagonal positions, but `0` stays `0`, and each diagonal entry stays
in the same place. So the whole matrix is unchanged by transposition.
</RevealSolution>
