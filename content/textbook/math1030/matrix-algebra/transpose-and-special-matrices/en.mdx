title: "3.2 Transpose and special matrices"
description: "Use transpose, symmetry, non-commuting products, and block structure to read matrix shape and algebraic behavior."

Once matrix multiplication is available, the shape of a matrix starts to matter
as much as its numerical entries. The transpose is the first tool in this
section that lets us read a matrix by its geometry: rows become columns, and
the main diagonal becomes the axis of reflection. That simple idea explains
symmetry, skew-symmetry, commuting patterns, and block structure.

## Transpose swaps rows and columns

<Definition title="Transpose">
If `A = [a_{ij}]` is an `m \times n` matrix, then its transpose `A^T` is the
`n \times m` matrix whose `(j,i)` entry is `a_{ij}`.

Equivalently,

$$
(A^T)_{ji} = a_{ij}.
$$

So every row of `A` becomes a column of `A^T`, and every column of `A`
becomes a row.
</Definition>

This is not a new operation that changes the numbers in a mysterious way. It is
purely a re-reading of the same entries in the opposite orientation.

<WorkedExample title="Compute a transpose">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 4 & -2 \\
0 & 3 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
4 & 3 \\
-2 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The `2 \times 3` matrix becomes a `3 \times 2` matrix because rows and columns
swap roles.
</WorkedExample>

Two basic identities should be treated as part of the definition-level
vocabulary of the subject:

$$
(A^T)^T = A,
\qquad
(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T,
\qquad
(cA)^T = cA^T.
$$

When products are defined, transpose reverses the order:

$$
(AB)^T = B^T A^T.
$$

That reversal is not a cosmetic detail. It reflects the fact that matrix
multiplication is built from row-column pairings, and transposition switches the
role of rows and columns.

<WorkedExample title="Transpose identities in action">
Take

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
3 & 0 \\
4 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A + B =
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 2 \\
4 & 6
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
(A + B)^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 4 \\
2 & 6
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

On the other hand,

$$
A^T + B^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
2 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
+
\begin{bmatrix}
3 & 4 \\
0 & 5
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 4 \\
2 & 6
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The product rule is similar, but the order matters:

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
11 & 10 \\
4 & 5
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
(AB)^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
11 & 4 \\
10 & 5
\end{bmatrix},
$$

while

$$
B^T A^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
3 & 4 \\
0 & 5
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
2 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
11 & 4 \\
10 & 5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
</WorkedExample>

<TheoremCard title="Basic properties of transpose">
For compatible matrices:

1. `(A^T)^T = A`
2. `(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T`
3. `(cA)^T = cA^T`
4. `(AC)^T = C^T A^T`
</TheoremCard>

The proof of the product rule is worth reading carefully once. To compare
`(AC)^T` and `C^T A^T`, you compare their corresponding entries. The transpose
does not change the numbers involved in the summation; it only changes the
direction in which those numbers are arranged.

## Symmetric and skew-symmetric matrices

<Definition title="Symmetric and skew-symmetric matrices">
Let `A` be a square matrix.

- `A` is symmetric if `A^T = A`.
- `A` is skew-symmetric if `A^T = -A`.
</Definition>

Symmetry says the matrix matches its reflection across the main diagonal.
Skew-symmetry says the reflected matrix is the negative of the original.

<WorkedExample title="Recognize symmetry">
The matrix

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 & 4 \\
-1 & 3 & 0 \\
4 & 0 & 5
\end{bmatrix}
$$

is symmetric, because the `(i,j)` and `(j,i)` entries agree.

The matrix

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & 2 & -1 \\
-2 & 0 & 4 \\
1 & -4 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
$$

is skew-symmetric, because the transpose changes every off-diagonal entry's
sign and leaves the diagonal as `0`.
</WorkedExample>

Three quick consequences are especially useful:

- every diagonal entry of a real skew-symmetric matrix is `0`
- the zero matrix is both symmetric and skew-symmetric
- the identity matrix is symmetric

The third item is a special case of the fact that diagonal matrices are fixed
by transpose.

<TheoremCard title="Useful transpose-based identities">
For every square matrix `A`,

$$
(A + A^T)^T = A + A^T,
\qquad
(A - A^T)^T = -(A - A^T).
$$

So `A + A^T` is symmetric and `A - A^T` is skew-symmetric.
</TheoremCard>

<ProofSketch title="Why the identities hold">
Apply the basic transpose rules:

$$
(A + A^T)^T = A^T + (A^T)^T = A^T + A = A + A^T.
$$

For the difference,

$$
(A - A^T)^T = A^T - (A^T)^T = A^T - A = -(A - A^T).
$$
</ProofSketch>

The previous result is the starting point for the most important structural
fact in this section.

<TheoremCard title="Decomposition into symmetric and skew-symmetric parts">
Every square matrix `A` can be written uniquely as

$$
A = S + K,
$$

where `S` is symmetric and `K` is skew-symmetric. In fact,

$$
S = \frac{1}{2}(A + A^T),
\qquad
K = \frac{1}{2}(A - A^T).
$$
</TheoremCard>

The proof is a good model for how the course turns rough intuition into a
formal argument.

<WorkedExample title="Decompose a matrix">
Let

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 1 \\
2 & -1 & 3 \\
4 & 2 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & 4 \\
0 & -1 & 2 \\
1 & 3 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So

$$
\frac{1}{2}(A + A^T)
=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1 & \frac{5}{2} \\
1 & -1 & \frac{5}{2} \\
\frac{5}{2} & \frac{5}{2} & 0
\end{bmatrix},
$$

and

$$
\frac{1}{2}(A - A^T)
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & -1 & -\frac{3}{2} \\
1 & 0 & \frac{1}{2} \\
\frac{3}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The first matrix is symmetric, the second is skew-symmetric, and their sum is
`A`.
</WorkedExample>

There is also a very useful symmetric pattern that appears whenever a product is
paired with its transpose.

<TheoremCard title="Products with a transpose are symmetric">
If the product is defined, then both `A^T A` and `A A^T` are symmetric.
</TheoremCard>

<ProofSketch title="One-line proof">
For `A^T A`,

$$
(A^T A)^T = A^T (A^T)^T = A^T A.
$$

The same calculation works for `A A^T`.
</ProofSketch>

This identity is one of the main reasons transpose shows up again in later
topics such as orthogonality, projections, and least-squares style arguments.

## Commuting and non-commuting matrices

<Definition title="Commuting matrices">
Two matrices `A` and `B` of the same size commute if

$$
AB = BA.
$$
</Definition>

For addition, commutativity is automatic. For multiplication, it is exceptional.
This is one of the most important differences between scalar algebra and matrix
algebra.

The zero matrix and the identity matrix commute with every square matrix of the
same size. Diagonal matrices of the same size also commute with one another,
because their products remain diagonal and the diagonal entries multiply in the
ordinary commutative way.

<WorkedExample title="A non-commuting pair">
Take

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
0 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
B =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
1 & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
AB =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 1 \\
1 & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
BA =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
1 & 2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So `AB \ne BA`.
</WorkedExample>

That kind of example is not a curiosity. It is the reason matrix identities must
always preserve the order of factors.

<TheoremCard title="A useful skew-symmetric commuting test">
If `A` and `B` are skew-symmetric square matrices, then

$$
AB \text{ is symmetric } \iff AB = BA.
$$
</TheoremCard>

<ProofSketch title="Why this is true">
Because `A^T = -A` and `B^T = -B`,

$$
(AB)^T = B^T A^T = (-B)(-A) = BA.
$$

So `AB` is symmetric exactly when `(AB)^T = AB`, and that happens exactly when
`BA = AB`.
</ProofSketch>

<WorkedExample title="Symmetric times skew-symmetric, with commutation">
Let

$$
S =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 0 \\
0 & 3
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
K =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & 1 \\
-1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Here `S` is symmetric and `K` is skew-symmetric, but they do not commute:

$$
SK =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & 2 \\
-3 & 0
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
KS =
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & 3 \\
-2 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

If two matrices of this kind do commute, then their product is skew-symmetric,
because

$$
(SK)^T = K^T S^T = (-K)S = -KS = -SK.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Special matrices

Special matrices are defined by where the zero entries must be.
That makes them much easier to read and often much easier to multiply.

<Definition title="Diagonal, upper-triangular, and lower-triangular matrices">
- A diagonal matrix has all off-diagonal entries equal to `0`.
- An upper-triangular matrix has all entries below the main diagonal equal to `0`.
- A lower-triangular matrix has all entries above the main diagonal equal to `0`.
</Definition>

<WorkedExample title="Transpose of a triangular matrix">
If

$$
U =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 2 & 3 \\
0 & 4 & 5 \\
0 & 0 & 6
\end{bmatrix},
$$

then

$$
U^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
2 & 4 & 0 \\
3 & 5 & 6
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So the transpose of an upper-triangular matrix is lower-triangular.
</WorkedExample>

Two structural facts are worth keeping in mind:

- the product of two diagonal matrices is diagonal
- the product of two upper-triangular matrices is upper-triangular
- the product of two lower-triangular matrices is lower-triangular

For diagonal matrices, the product is especially simple:

$$
\operatorname{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_n)\,
\operatorname{diag}(e_1,\dots,e_n)
=
\operatorname{diag}(d_1 e_1,\dots,d_n e_n).
$$

That is why diagonal matrices of the same size commute with each other.

<CommonMistake title="Do not confuse the matrix with its zero pattern">
An upper-triangular matrix may have many nonzero entries above the diagonal, but
never below it. A diagonal matrix is more restrictive: every off-diagonal entry
must be zero.
</CommonMistake>

## Block matrices

Large matrices are often easier to read if we group entries into blocks. A block
matrix is still just a matrix; the blocks are a way of organizing it.

<Definition title="Block matrix">
If a matrix is partitioned by drawing horizontal and vertical cuts, the resulting
submatrices are called blocks.
</Definition>

For example,

$$
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 2 & 1 \\
3 & 4 & 0 & -1 \\
5 & 2 & 6 & 3 \\
7 & 8 & 1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
A_{11} & A_{12} \\
A_{21} & A_{22}
\end{bmatrix},
$$

where

$$
A_{11} =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 \\
3 & 4
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
A_{12} =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 1 \\
0 & -1
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
A_{21} =
\begin{bmatrix}
5 & 2 \\
7 & 8
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
A_{22} =
\begin{bmatrix}
6 & 3 \\
1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

At the block level, transpose simply swaps the blocks and transposes each block:

$$
A^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
A_{11}^T & A_{21}^T \\
A_{12}^T & A_{22}^T
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

This is the same row/column swap principle as before, just applied to grouped
pieces instead of individual entries.

<WorkedExample title="Block multiplication">
Suppose the partition sizes are compatible, and write

$$
M =
\begin{bmatrix}
A & B \\
C & D
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
N =
\begin{bmatrix}
E & F \\
G & H
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
MN =
\begin{bmatrix}
AE + BG & AF + BH \\
CE + DG & CF + DH
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The formula looks exactly like ordinary `2 \times 2` matrix multiplication, but
each entry is now a block product.
</WorkedExample>

The key condition is compatibility: the inner block sizes must match so that
each block product is meaningful. If the partitions do not align, block
multiplication is not even defined.

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="Transpose reverses products">
The correct identity is `(AB)^T = B^T A^T`, not `A^T B^T`.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Symmetric does not mean commuting">
Symmetric is a property of one matrix. Commuting is a property of two matrices.
They are different statements.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="A skew-symmetric real matrix has a zero diagonal">
If `A^T = -A`, then each diagonal entry must equal its own negative, so every
diagonal entry is `0`.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Block sizes must line up">
You may only multiply block matrices when the blocks are partitioned
compatibly. Treating blocks as letters is only valid when the block products
actually make sense.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="If `A` is `3 × 2`, what is the size of `A^T`?">
Swap rows and columns.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`A^T` is `2 × 3`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What must every diagonal entry of a real skew-symmetric matrix be?">
Use `A^T = -A` on the diagonal.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Every diagonal entry must be `0`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Which identity is correct: `(AB)^T = A^T B^T` or `(AB)^T = B^T A^T`?">
Remember that transpose reverses the order of multiplication.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`(AB)^T = B^T A^T`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If `S` is symmetric and `K` is skew-symmetric, what is `S^T` and what is `K^T`?">
Read the definitions directly.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`S^T = S` and `K^T = -K`.
</RevealSolution>

## Guided exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Find the symmetric and skew-symmetric parts of `A = [[2,1],[4,3]]`.">
Use `S = 1/2 (A + A^T)` and `K = 1/2 (A - A^T)`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
First compute

$$
A^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 4 \\
1 & 3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
S = \frac{1}{2}
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 5 \\
5 & 6
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & \frac{5}{2} \\
\frac{5}{2} & 3
\end{bmatrix},
$$

and

$$
K = \frac{1}{2}
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & -3 \\
3 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & -\frac{3}{2} \\
\frac{3}{2} & 0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Check that `S` is symmetric, `K` is skew-symmetric, and `A = S + K`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Let `A` and `B` be symmetric matrices. If `AB = BA`, why is `AB` symmetric?">
Use the transpose identity and the commuting assumption.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Because

$$
(AB)^T = B^T A^T = BA = AB.
$$

So `AB` equals its transpose, which is exactly the definition of symmetric.
</RevealSolution>
