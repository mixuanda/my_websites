The previous note studies determinants from the row-operation viewpoint. This
note turns the picture sideways.

Transpose lets us move information between rows and columns. Once that symmetry
is in place, column expansion and column operations become just as legitimate as
their row versions. The chapter then closes with two classical formulas:
adjoints and Cramer's rule.

## Transpose does not change determinant

<TheoremCard title="Determinant of a transpose">
For every square matrix `A`,

$$
\det(A^T)=\det(A).
$$
</TheoremCard>

This theorem is conceptually important. A determinant is defined by expanding
along rows, but it does not secretly prefer rows over columns. Transpose swaps
the two viewpoints without changing the final scalar.

<TheoremCard title="Cofactor expansion along any column">
For any fixed column `j` of a square matrix `A=[a_{ij}]`,

$$
\det(A)=a_{1j}A_{1j}+a_{2j}A_{2j}+\cdots+a_{nj}A_{nj}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\det(A)=\sum_{i=1}^n (-1)^{i+j}a_{ij}M_{ij}.
$$
</TheoremCard>

So you may expand along a row or along a column. The right choice is whichever
one produces the cleanest minors.

<WorkedExample title="A column expansion with many zeros">
Let

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
1&7&0\\
6&9&8\\
0&1&5
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The third column has one zero, so expand along that column:

$$
\det(A)=
0\cdot A_{13}
+8A_{23}
+5A_{33}.
$$

That gives

$$
\det(A)
=-8
\begin{vmatrix}
1&7\\
0&1
\end{vmatrix}
+5
\begin{vmatrix}
1&7\\
6&9
\end{vmatrix}
=-8(1)+5(9-42)=-173.
$$

The same answer would come from row expansion, but the chosen column makes the
arithmetic shorter.
</WorkedExample>

## Column operations obey the same pattern

Because transpose preserves determinant, every row-operation rule has a column
version.

<TheoremCard title="How column operations change determinant">
Let `B` be obtained from a square matrix `A` by one elementary column
operation.

1. Swapping two columns multiplies the determinant by `-1`.
2. Multiplying one column by `\beta` multiplies the determinant by `\beta`.
3. Replacing one column with itself plus a multiple of another column leaves the
   determinant unchanged.
</TheoremCard>

<WorkedExample title="Use column operations to create zeros">
Consider

$$
C=
\begin{bmatrix}
3&2&-1\\
4&1&6\\
-3&-1&2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Apply the column operations `-C_2+C_1` and `C_3+C_1`:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
3&2&-1\\
4&1&6\\
-3&-1&2
\end{bmatrix}
\longrightarrow
\begin{bmatrix}
1&2&0\\
3&1&9\\
-2&-1&-1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

These are column-addition operations, so the determinant is unchanged. Now
expand along the third column:

$$
\det(C)=
9
\begin{vmatrix}
1&2\\
-2&-1
\end{vmatrix}
-1
\begin{vmatrix}
1&2\\
3&1
\end{vmatrix}
=9(3)-(-5)=22.
$$

The point is not that column operations are always superior. The point is that
you may use whichever direction creates more zeros with less bookkeeping.
</WorkedExample>

## Adjoint matrices package all cofactors at once

The cofactor of one entry helps in one expansion. The adjoint matrix collects
all cofactors into one object.

<Definition title="Adjoint matrix">
For an `n\times n` matrix `A=[a_{ij}]`, first form the cofactor matrix

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
A_{11}&A_{12}&\cdots&A_{1n}\\
A_{21}&A_{22}&\cdots&A_{2n}\\
\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\
A_{n1}&A_{n2}&\cdots&A_{nn}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The **adjoint matrix** is the transpose of that cofactor matrix:

$$
\operatorname{adj}(A)=
\begin{bmatrix}
A_{11}&A_{21}&\cdots&A_{n1}\\
A_{12}&A_{22}&\cdots&A_{n2}\\
\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\
A_{1n}&A_{2n}&\cdots&A_{nn}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
</Definition>

<TheoremCard title="Adjoint identity and inverse formula">
For every square matrix `A`,

$$
A\,\operatorname{adj}(A)=\det(A)I.
$$

If `\det(A)\neq0`, then `A` is invertible and

$$
A^{-1}=\frac{1}{\det(A)}\operatorname{adj}(A).
$$
</TheoremCard>

This formula is conceptually clean, but in numerical work it is usually less
efficient than row reduction. Its value is that it exposes the algebraic
structure of inverse matrices.

<WorkedExample title="Recover the 2×2 inverse formula from the adjoint">
Let

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
a&b\\
c&d
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\det(A)=ad-bc\neq0.
$$

The cofactor matrix is

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
d&-c\\
-b&a
\end{bmatrix},
$$

so

$$
\operatorname{adj}(A)=
\begin{bmatrix}
d&-b\\
-c&a
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Therefore

$$
A^{-1}=
\frac{1}{ad-bc}
\begin{bmatrix}
d&-b\\
-c&a
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The familiar inverse formula is really the adjoint identity written in the
`2\times2` case.
</WorkedExample>

## Cramer's rule solves one coordinate at a time

<TheoremCard title="Cramer's rule">
Let `A` be an invertible `n\times n` matrix and let `b\in\mathbb{R}^n`. For
each `j`, let `M_j` be the matrix obtained by replacing the `j`th column of
`A` with `b`.

If `x` is the unique solution of

$$
Ax=b,
$$

then

$$
x_j=\frac{\det(M_j)}{\det(A)}.
$$
</TheoremCard>

Cramer's rule is beautiful because each coordinate is isolated by one
determinant ratio. It is not the fastest method for large systems, but it is a
clean theoretical formula for square invertible systems.

<WorkedExample title="Solve a 2×2 system with Cramer's rule">
Solve

$$
\begin{cases}
2x_1+x_2=5,\\
x_1+3x_2=7.
\end{cases}
$$

Write

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
2&1\\
1&3
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
b=
\begin{bmatrix}
5\\
7
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

First compute

$$
\det(A)=2\cdot3-1\cdot1=5.
$$

Replace the first column by `b`:

$$
M_1=
\begin{bmatrix}
5&1\\
7&3
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\det(M_1)=15-7=8.
$$

Replace the second column by `b`:

$$
M_2=
\begin{bmatrix}
2&5\\
1&7
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\det(M_2)=14-5=9.
$$

So

$$
x_1=\frac{8}{5},
\qquad
x_2=\frac{9}{5}.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Cramer's rule is not a universal system solver">
Cramer's rule requires a square coefficient matrix and a nonzero determinant.
If the system is rectangular or singular, the rule is not available. Even when
it applies, it is usually less efficient than Gaussian elimination for large
systems.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="Does transpose change the determinant of a square matrix?">
State the theorem directly.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. `\det(A^T)=\det(A)`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What column operation leaves the determinant unchanged?">
Think of the column analogue of a type-III row operation.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Adding a scalar multiple of one column to another column leaves the determinant
unchanged.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="When may Cramer's rule be used?">
Identify the structural hypotheses on A.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
When the coefficient matrix is square and invertible, equivalently when its
determinant is nonzero.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Use a column expansion to compute `\det\begin{bmatrix}1&0&2\\3&4&5\\0&0&6\end{bmatrix}`.">
Choose the column with the most zeros.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Expand along the second column:

$$
\det=
4(-1)^{2+2}
\begin{vmatrix}
1&2\\
0&6
\end{vmatrix}
=4(6)=24.
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="For `A=\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{bmatrix}`, write down `\operatorname{adj}(A)`.">
Compute the four cofactors first, then transpose the cofactor matrix.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The cofactors are `A_{11}=4`, `A_{12}=-3`, `A_{21}=-2`, and `A_{22}=1`. Hence

$$
\operatorname{adj}(A)=
\begin{bmatrix}
4&-2\\
-3&1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Use Cramer's rule to find x_1 for the system `x_1+x_2=3`, `2x_1-x_2=0`.">
You only need `\det(A)` and `\det(M_1)`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Here

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
1&1\\
2&-1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
b=
\begin{bmatrix}
3\\
0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
\det(A)=-1-2=-3,
\qquad
M_1=
\begin{bmatrix}
3&1\\
0&-1
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\det(M_1)=-3.
$$

So

$$
x_1=\frac{\det(M_1)}{\det(A)}=1.
$$
</RevealSolution>
