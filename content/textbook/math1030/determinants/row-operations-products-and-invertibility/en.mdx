description: "Track exactly how row operations change determinants, then connect that behavior to multiplicativity, inverse matrices, and invertibility tests."

The previous note defines determinants by cofactor expansion. That definition is
correct, but it is not the way you want to compute every determinant by hand.
The real power comes from combining determinants with row operations.

This note answers three questions:

- how does each elementary row operation affect the determinant;
- why does `\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)` hold for square matrices;
- why is `\det(A)\neq0` exactly the same as saying that `A` is invertible.

## Why row operations matter

Row reduction changes a matrix into a friendlier one. The determinant is useful
only if we know precisely how much that friendly matrix differs from the
original.

<TheoremCard title="How the three row operations change determinant">
Let `A` be a square matrix.

1. If `B` is obtained from `A` by swapping two rows once, then

$$
\det(B)=-\det(A).
$$

2. If `B` is obtained from `A` by multiplying one row by a nonzero scalar
   `\beta`, then

$$
\det(B)=\beta\det(A).
$$

3. If `B` is obtained from `A` by replacing one row with itself plus a multiple
   of another row, then

$$
\det(B)=\det(A).
$$
</TheoremCard>

These are exact formulas, not rough heuristics. They are the bookkeeping rules
that let elimination and determinants work together.

## A row-reduction algorithm for determinants

Suppose you row-reduce a square matrix `A` to an upper triangular matrix `U`.
Then:

1. track how many row swaps you used;
2. track every row-scaling factor you introduced;
3. leave row-addition steps alone, because they do not change the determinant;
4. read `\det(U)` from the diagonal product.

Then undo the sign and scaling changes to recover `\det(A)`.

<WorkedExample title="Evaluate a determinant by row reduction">
Let

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
1&2&3\\
2&5&7\\
3&8&10
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Apply row operations:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
1&2&3\\
2&5&7\\
3&8&10
\end{bmatrix}
\xrightarrow{R_2-2R_1,\ R_3-3R_1}
\begin{bmatrix}
1&2&3\\
0&1&1\\
0&2&1
\end{bmatrix}
\xrightarrow{R_3-2R_2}
\begin{bmatrix}
1&2&3\\
0&1&1\\
0&0&-1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Only row-addition operations were used, so the determinant did not change
during the reduction. The final matrix is upper triangular, so

$$
\det(A)=1\cdot1\cdot(-1)=-1.
$$
</WorkedExample>

<WorkedExample title="A row swap changes the sign">
Let

$$
B=
\begin{bmatrix}
0&1\\
4&3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Swap the two rows:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
0&1\\
4&3
\end{bmatrix}
\xrightarrow{R_1\leftrightarrow R_2}
\begin{bmatrix}
4&3\\
0&1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The new matrix is triangular, so its determinant is `4`. Because one row swap
was used,

$$
\det(B)=-4.
$$

That agrees with the direct `2\times2` calculation:

$$
\det(B)=0\cdot3-1\cdot4=-4.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## Multiplicativity is the structural theorem

<TheoremCard title="Determinant of a product">
If `A` and `B` are square matrices of the same size, then

$$
\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B).
$$
</TheoremCard>

This theorem is deeper than it first looks. Matrix multiplication is not
commutative, and usually `AB\neq BA`. Even so,

$$
\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)=\det(B)\det(A)=\det(BA).
$$

So determinants forget some of the order-sensitive detail of matrix
multiplication while preserving a very important scalar invariant.

<CollapsibleProof title="Why row-operation matrices lead to multiplicativity">
Every elementary row operation can be encoded by an elementary matrix `E`, and
for such matrices we know

$$
\det(EA)=\det(E)\det(A).
$$

If `A` is invertible, it can be written as a product of elementary matrices.
That reduces the product theorem to repeated use of the elementary case. If `A`
is singular, then both sides are `0`, so the same formula still holds.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Determinant and invertibility say the same thing

<TheoremCard title="Invertibility test by determinant">
For a square matrix `A`, the following are equivalent:

1. `A` is invertible.
2. `\det(A)\neq0`.
</TheoremCard>

This turns determinants into a full invertibility test, not merely a numerical
summary.

If a row-reduced form of `A` has a zero row, then its determinant is `0`, so
`A` is not invertible. If row reduction reaches the identity matrix, then the
diagonal product is nonzero, so `A` is invertible.

<TheoremCard title="Inverse and powers">
If `A` is invertible, then

$$
\det(A^{-1})=\frac{1}{\det(A)}.
$$

More generally, for every positive integer `m`,

$$
\det(A^m)=(\det(A))^m.
$$

If `A` is invertible, the same power rule remains true for negative integers as
well.
</TheoremCard>

<WorkedExample title="Find the parameter that makes a matrix singular">
Let

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
2&1&0&1\\
0&1&1&1\\
1&0&0&x\\
0&2&3&1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Expand along the first column:

$$
\det(A)=
2
\begin{vmatrix}
1&1&1\\
0&0&x\\
2&3&1
\end{vmatrix}
+
\begin{vmatrix}
1&0&1\\
1&1&1\\
2&3&1
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

The first `3\times3` determinant is easiest by expanding along its second row:

$$
\begin{vmatrix}
1&1&1\\
0&0&x\\
2&3&1
\end{vmatrix}
=-x
\begin{vmatrix}
1&1\\
2&3
\end{vmatrix}
=-x.
$$

The second determinant equals `-1`, so

$$
\det(A)=2(-x)+(-1)=-2x-1.
$$

Therefore `A` is singular exactly when `\det(A)=0`, that is,

$$
x=-\frac12.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## A practical checklist for hand computation

When you compute a determinant by elimination, keep the following discipline.

- Use row-addition steps as freely as you like; they preserve the determinant.
- Delay row scaling unless it creates a major simplification, because each
  scaling factor must later be divided back out.
- Count row swaps carefully; each one flips the sign once.
- Stop as soon as you reach an upper triangular matrix or a zero row.

This is exactly the same kind of discipline you already use in solving linear
systems. The difference is that the final goal is a scalar, not a solution
vector.

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Row-equivalent matrices do not usually have the same determinant">
Students often remember that row-equivalent matrices describe the same solution
set for a linear system and then incorrectly conclude that they must have the
same determinant. That is false.

Only row-addition steps preserve the determinant. Row swaps change its sign, and
row scaling multiplies it by the scaling factor.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="If one row of A is multiplied by 5, what happens to `\det(A)`?">
Use the row-scaling rule directly.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The determinant is multiplied by `5`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If A has a zero row after row reduction, what can you conclude about `\det(A)`?">
Think about the determinant of the echelon form first.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`\det(A)=0`. A row-echelon form with a zero row is singular, so its determinant
is `0`, and therefore the original matrix also has determinant `0`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Can a square matrix with determinant `0` be invertible?">
Use the equivalence theorem.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. A square matrix is invertible exactly when its determinant is nonzero.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Use row reduction to compute `\det\begin{bmatrix}1&1&0\\2&3&1\\0&1&4\end{bmatrix}`.">
Try to avoid row scaling so the bookkeeping stays short.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Subtract `2R_1` from `R_2`:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
1&1&0\\
2&3&1\\
0&1&4
\end{bmatrix}
\xrightarrow{R_2-2R_1}
\begin{bmatrix}
1&1&0\\
0&1&1\\
0&1&4
\end{bmatrix}
\xrightarrow{R_3-R_2}
\begin{bmatrix}
1&1&0\\
0&1&1\\
0&0&3
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Only row-addition steps were used, so

$$
\det=1\cdot1\cdot3=3.
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Suppose `\det(A)=4` and `\det(B)=-3` for two `3\times3` matrices. What is `\det(AB)`?">
Use multiplicativity.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">

$$
\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)=4(-3)=-12.
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If `\det(A)=6`, what is `\det(A^{-1})`?">
Assume A is invertible.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">

$$
\det(A^{-1})=\frac{1}{\det(A)}=\frac16.
$$
</RevealSolution>
