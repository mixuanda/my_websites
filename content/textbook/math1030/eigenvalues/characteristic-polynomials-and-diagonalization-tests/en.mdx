The null-space criterion

$$
\det(A-\lambda I)=0
$$

tells you how to test a proposed eigenvalue. The next step is to package that
test into one polynomial whose roots are exactly the eigenvalues of the matrix.

That polynomial is strong enough to limit how many eigenvalues a matrix can
have, describe repeated eigenvalues, and give practical tests for
diagonalization.

## Characteristic polynomial

<Definition title="Characteristic polynomial">
Let `A` be an `n\times n` matrix. The polynomial

$$
p_A(x)=\det(A-xI_n)
$$

is called the **characteristic polynomial** of `A`.
</Definition>

The variable `x` is only an indeterminate. Once you substitute a scalar
`\lambda`, the value `p_A(\lambda)` becomes the determinant of `A-\lambda I`.

<TheoremCard title="Eigenvalues are exactly the roots of the characteristic polynomial">
Let `A` be an `n\times n` matrix and `\lambda` a scalar. Then the following are
equivalent:

1. `\lambda` is an eigenvalue of `A`;
2. `\lambda` is a root of `p_A(x)`.
</TheoremCard>

This theorem is just the determinant test from the previous note written in a
more organized way:

$$
\lambda\text{ is an eigenvalue}
\iff
\det(A-\lambda I)=0
\iff
p_A(\lambda)=0.
$$

<TheoremCard title="Basic shape of the characteristic polynomial">
If `A` is an `n\times n` matrix, then `p_A(x)` is a degree-`n` polynomial whose
leading coefficient is `(-1)^n` and whose constant term is `\det(A)`.
</TheoremCard>

So the characteristic polynomial is never an arbitrary expression. Its degree is
fixed by the matrix size, and its constant term already remembers the
determinant.

## First examples

<WorkedExample title="A 2×2 characteristic polynomial">
Let

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
3&2\\
3&-2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
p_A(x)=
\det
\begin{bmatrix}
3-x&2\\
3&-2-x
\end{bmatrix}
=(3-x)(-2-x)-6.
$$

Simplifying gives

$$
p_A(x)=x^2-x-12=(x-4)(x+3).
$$

Therefore the eigenvalues are `4` and `-3`.
</WorkedExample>

<WorkedExample title="A repeated root">
Let

$$
B=
\begin{bmatrix}
2&3\\
0&2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
p_B(x)=
\det
\begin{bmatrix}
2-x&3\\
0&2-x
\end{bmatrix}
=(2-x)^2=(x-2)^2.
$$

So `2` is the only eigenvalue, but it appears with multiplicity `2`.
</WorkedExample>

## Algebraic and geometric multiplicity

Repeated roots need extra language.

<Definition title="Algebraic multiplicity and geometric multiplicity">
Suppose the characteristic polynomial factors as

$$
p_A(x)=(-1)^n(x-\lambda_1)^{m_1}\cdots(x-\lambda_s)^{m_s},
$$

where `\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_s` are distinct roots.

1. The exponent `m_i` is the **algebraic multiplicity** of `\lambda_i`.
2. The dimension of the eigenspace `E_A(\lambda_i)` is the
   **geometric multiplicity** of `\lambda_i`.
</Definition>

Algebraic multiplicity comes from the polynomial. Geometric multiplicity comes
from the null space `N(A-\lambda I)`. They measure different things, and they do
not always agree.

<TheoremCard title="Multiplicity inequality">
If `\lambda` is an eigenvalue of an `n\times n` matrix `A`, then

$$
1\le m_g(\lambda)\le m_a(\lambda)\le n.
$$
</TheoremCard>

So every eigenspace has dimension at least `1`, but it can never be larger than
the algebraic multiplicity of its eigenvalue.

<WorkedExample title="A repeated eigenvalue with too small an eigenspace">
For

$$
B=
\begin{bmatrix}
2&3\\
0&2
\end{bmatrix},
$$

the characteristic polynomial is `(x-2)^2`, so the eigenvalue `2` has algebraic
multiplicity `2`.

Now solve `(B-2I)x=0`:

$$
B-2I=
\begin{bmatrix}
0&3\\
0&0
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

So `x_2=0` and `x_1` is free. The eigenspace is

$$
\operatorname{span}\left\{
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\0
\end{bmatrix}
\right\},
$$

which has dimension `1`. Therefore

$$
m_a(2)=2,\qquad m_g(2)=1.
$$

This mismatch is exactly why the matrix is not diagonalizable.
</WorkedExample>

## Distinct eigenvalues force independence

<TheoremCard title="Eigenvectors of distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent">
If `v_1,\dots,v_k` are eigenvectors of `A` corresponding to pairwise distinct
eigenvalues `\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_k`, then

$$
v_1,\dots,v_k
$$

are linearly independent.
</TheoremCard>

This theorem has two major consequences.

<TheoremCard title="Upper bound on the number of distinct eigenvalues">
An `n\times n` matrix can have at most `n` distinct eigenvalues.
</TheoremCard>

<TheoremCard title="Distinct eigenvalue test for diagonalizability">
If an `n\times n` matrix has exactly `n` distinct eigenvalues, then it is
diagonalizable.
</TheoremCard>

The converse is false. A diagonalizable matrix may still have repeated
eigenvalues. The identity matrix is the simplest example: every vector is an
eigenvector with eigenvalue `1`, yet the matrix is already diagonal.

## A sharper diagonalization criterion

Distinct eigenvalues are a sufficient test, but not the only one.

<TheoremCard title="Dimension-sum test for diagonalizability">
Suppose a real `n\times n` matrix `A` has distinct real eigenvalues
`\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_s`. Then `A` is diagonalizable if and only if

$$
\dim E_A(\lambda_1)+\cdots+\dim E_A(\lambda_s)=n.
$$
</TheoremCard>

This criterion says that diagonalization succeeds exactly when the eigenspaces
together contribute enough independent eigenvectors to form a basis of the whole
space.

<WorkedExample title="Repeated eigenvalue but still diagonalizable">
Let

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
2&1&1\\
1&2&1\\
1&1&2
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Its eigenvalues are `4` and `1`, with `1` repeated algebraically. But the
eigenspace for `4` is one-dimensional, while the eigenspace for `1` is
two-dimensional. Therefore

$$
1+2=3,
$$

which matches the matrix size. So `A` is diagonalizable even though one
eigenvalue is repeated.
</WorkedExample>

## Cayley-Hamilton gives a polynomial identity

The source notes continue one step further and relate the characteristic
polynomial back to the matrix itself.

<TheoremCard title="Cayley-Hamilton theorem">
If

$$
p_A(x)=c_0+c_1x+c_2x^2+\cdots+c_nx^n,
$$

then

$$
c_0I+c_1A+c_2A^2+\cdots+c_nA^n=0.
$$

Equivalently, `p_A(A)=0`.
</TheoremCard>

For diagonalizable matrices this is easy to believe: once `A` is written as
`SDS^{-1}`, the identity reduces to applying the scalar polynomial `p_A` to each
diagonal entry of `D`, and every diagonal entry is an eigenvalue, hence a root
of `p_A`.

The practical consequence is that high powers of `A` can be rewritten in terms
of lower powers `I,A,\dots,A^{n-1}`.

<WorkedExample title="A small Cayley-Hamilton identity">
Let

$$
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
1&4\\
0&1
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Its characteristic polynomial is

$$
p_A(x)=(1-x)^2=x^2-2x+1.
$$

Therefore Cayley-Hamilton says

$$
A^2-2A+I=0.
$$

So every higher power of `A` can be reduced using this quadratic relation.
</WorkedExample>

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Repeated eigenvalue does not automatically mean 'not diagonalizable'">
A repeated eigenvalue only tells you the algebraic multiplicity is greater than
`1`. Diagonalizability depends on whether the corresponding eigenspaces still
supply enough linearly independent eigenvectors. Repetition is a warning sign,
not a final verdict.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="What is the characteristic polynomial of `\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&5\end{bmatrix}`?">
Use `p_A(x)=\det(A-xI)`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">

$$
p_A(x)=
\begin{vmatrix}
2-x&0\\
0&5-x
\end{vmatrix}
=(2-x)(5-x).
$$
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If an `n\times n` matrix has n distinct eigenvalues, what can you conclude immediately?">
Use the distinct-eigenvalue theorem.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It is diagonalizable, because eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues
are linearly independent.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Can geometric multiplicity ever exceed algebraic multiplicity?">
Use the multiplicity inequality.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. For every eigenvalue `\lambda`,

$$
m_g(\lambda)\le m_a(\lambda).
$$
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Find the characteristic polynomial of `\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\-2&3\end{bmatrix}` and list its eigenvalues.">
Compute `\det(A-xI)` and factor the result.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">

$$
p_A(x)=
\begin{vmatrix}
-x&1\\
-2&3-x
\end{vmatrix}
=x^2-3x+2=(x-1)(x-2).
$$

So the eigenvalues are `1` and `2`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="A 4×4 matrix has four distinct eigenvalues. What is the dimension of the span of one chosen eigenvector from each eigenvalue?">
Use linear independence of eigenvectors with distinct eigenvalues.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The four chosen eigenvectors are linearly independent, so their span has
dimension `4`. Therefore they already form a basis of `\mathbb{R}^4`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Suppose `p_A(x)=x^3-6x^2+11x-6`. What matrix identity does Cayley-Hamilton give?">
Replace the variable `x` by the matrix `A`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">

$$
A^3-6A^2+11A-6I=0.
$$

That is the Cayley-Hamilton identity for this characteristic polynomial.
</RevealSolution>
