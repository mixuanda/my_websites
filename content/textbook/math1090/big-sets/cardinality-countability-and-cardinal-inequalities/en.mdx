Chapter 6 begins with a change of viewpoint. Earlier chapters built and studied
particular number systems. Here the question is broader: how can we compare the
sizes of sets, especially when the sets are infinite?

For finite sets, counting elements is enough. For infinite sets, the useful
notion is not ordinary counting but the existence of carefully chosen functions.
A bijection says that two sets have exactly matching elements. An injection says
that one set can be faithfully coded inside another.

## Same cardinality

<Definition title="Same cardinality">
Let `X` and `Y` be sets. We say that `X` and `Y` have the **same cardinality**,
and write

$$
|X|=|Y|,
$$

if there exists a bijection `f:X\to Y`.
</Definition>

This definition deliberately avoids saying that the elements have to look
similar. The two sets may have very different kinds of elements. What matters is
whether every element of `X` can be paired with exactly one element of `Y`, and
every element of `Y` is paired with exactly one element of `X`.

For finite sets this agrees with ordinary counting. If a set can be put in
bijection with a set of `n` labels, then it has `n` elements.

<Definition title="Finite set">
A set `X` is **finite** if

$$
|X|=|n|
$$

for some natural number `n`.

In that case we usually write simply `|X|=n`.
</Definition>

The notation `|X|` is therefore not a number in the usual sense for every set.
It is a symbol for the size, or cardinality, of `X`. For finite sets this size
is represented by a natural number. For infinite sets, we compare sizes through
maps.

## Countable and uncountable sets

<Definition title="Countable and uncountable">
A set `X` is **countable** if it is finite or if

$$
|X|=|N|.
$$

A set which is not countable is called **uncountable**.
</Definition>

Thus a countably infinite set is one whose elements can be listed in a sequence
without omission and without repetition:

$$
x_0,x_1,x_2,\ldots
$$

depending on the indexing convention for `N`. The exact starting index is not
important. What matters is that there is a bijection from the natural numbers to
the set.

<CommonMistake title="Countable does not mean finite">
In this chapter, countable includes finite sets and sets with the same
cardinality as `N`. The word "countable" is therefore broader than "finite".
</CommonMistake>

## The integers are countable

A useful exercise is to prove that

$$
|N|=|Z|.
$$

The key idea is that the integers can be arranged in a single infinite list.

<WorkedExample title="Enumerating the integers">
One convenient ordering is

$$
0,1,-1,2,-2,3,-3,\ldots .
$$

This list reaches every integer exactly once. It therefore gives a bijection
from `N` to `Z` after choosing the corresponding indexing convention.

For example, with `N=\{0,1,2,\ldots\}`, define

$$
f(0)=0,\qquad f(2k-1)=k,\qquad f(2k)=-k
$$

for `k\ge 1`. Then `f:N\to Z` is bijective.

<CollapsibleProof title="Why this proves countability">
Every positive integer appears at an odd index, every negative integer appears
at an even positive index, and `0` appears at index `0`. No two different
indices give the same integer, so the list is a genuine enumeration.
</CollapsibleProof>
</WorkedExample>

The example is important because it shows the first surprise about infinite
sets: a proper-looking enlargement of `N` can still have the same cardinality
as `N`.

## The rationals are countable

<TheoremCard title="The rationals are countable">
The set `Q` of rational numbers is countable.
</TheoremCard>

The proof first treats the positive rationals. It is enough
to enumerate `Q_+`, because then one may interleave positive rationals, negative
rationals, and zero.

The positive rational numbers can be organized by numerator and denominator. A
positive rational in lowest terms has the form

$$
\frac{p}{q}
$$

where `p,q\in N` are positive and share no common factor greater than `1`.
Place this rational at row `p` and column `q` in an infinite grid. Then traverse
the grid by diagonals:

$$
p+q=2,\quad p+q=3,\quad p+q=4,\quad \ldots
$$

and record the reduced fractions encountered along the way.

<WorkedExample title="The first few positive rationals by diagonals">
Following the diagonals and keeping only fractions in lowest terms gives a list
beginning like

$$
1,\ \frac12,\ 2,\ \frac13,\ 3,\ \frac14,\ \frac23,\ \frac32,\ 4,\ldots .
$$

The exact displayed beginning is less important than the mechanism: every
positive rational `p/q` in lowest terms appears on the diagonal `p+q`, and that
diagonal is reached after finitely many previous diagonals.
</WorkedExample>

![A diagonal scan of positive rational numbers](/images/textbook/math1090/cardinality-diagonal-grid.png)

*Figure. The diagonal scan is not a decorative picture; it is the mechanism that
turns a two-dimensional grid of positive rationals into one sequence.*

Why does this produce a bijection with `Q_+`?

- It is surjective because every positive rational has a lowest-term
  representative `p/q`, hence occurs somewhere in the grid.
- It is injective because using lowest terms prevents repetitions such as
  `1/1`, `2/2`, and `3/3` from all being recorded as the same rational.

Once `Q_+` is listed, the full set `Q` can be listed by alternating signs and
including zero:

$$
0,\ q_1,\ -q_1,\ q_2,\ -q_2,\ q_3,\ -q_3,\ldots .
$$

So `Q` is countable.

<CommonMistake title="A dense set can still be countable">
The rationals are dense in the real line, but density is not cardinality. The
diagonal enumeration shows that the rationals can still be placed into one
sequence.
</CommonMistake>

## Comparing cardinalities by injections

Bijections decide equality of cardinality. To compare unequal sizes, we
use injections.

<Definition title="Cardinal inequality">
Let `X` and `Y` be sets. We write

$$
|X|\le |Y|
$$

if there exists an injection `f:X\to Y`.

We write

$$
|X|<|Y|
$$

if `|X|\le |Y|` and `|X|\ne |Y|`.
</Definition>

An injection from `X` to `Y` means that `Y` has enough room to store a distinct
code for every element of `X`. This is why it is the correct analogue of
"less than or equal to" for cardinalities.

<WorkedExample title="A simple cardinal inequality">
The inclusion map

$$
i:N\to Z,\qquad i(n)=n
$$

is injective. Therefore `|N|\le |Z|`.

This alone does not prove equality. Equality requires a bijection, or two
opposite injections together with Cantor-Bernstein.
</WorkedExample>

## Cardinal inequality behaves like a partial order

<TheoremCard title="Cardinal inequalities form a partial-order pattern">
The relation `\le` on cardinalities is reflexive, antisymmetric, and
transitive. Consequently `<` is irreflexive and transitive.
</TheoremCard>

Here is the proof structure.

**Reflexivity.** The identity map

$$
id_X:X\to X
$$

is injective, so `|X|\le |X|`.

**Transitivity.** If `f:X\to Y` and `g:Y\to Z` are injections, then

$$
g\circ f:X\to Z
$$

is also an injection. Thus `|X|\le |Y|` and `|Y|\le |Z|` imply
`|X|\le |Z|`.

**Antisymmetry.** This is exactly the Cantor-Bernstein theorem.

<CommonMistake title="There is no set of all sets">
Strictly speaking, `\le` is not a relation on one giant set of all sets, because
there is no such set. The point is that the partial-order properties hold for
any collection of cardinalities that one is comparing.
</CommonMistake>

## Cantor-Bernstein theorem

<TheoremCard title="Cantor-Bernstein theorem">
Let `X` and `Y` be sets. If

$$
|X|\le |Y|
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
|Y|\le |X|,
$$

then

$$
|X|=|Y|.
$$
</TheoremCard>

In words: if `X` injects into `Y` and `Y` injects into `X`, then the two sets
actually have the same cardinality. This looks intuitively obvious, but the
proof is more delicate than the finite case.

Here is the standard sketch. Suppose

$$
f:X\to Y,\qquad g:Y\to X
$$

are injections. Since `g` embeds `Y` into `X`, the image `g(Y)` is a copy of
`Y` sitting inside `X`. The proof builds nested subsets

$$
A_0\supset B_0\supset A_1\supset B_1\supset\cdots
$$

of `X` by

$$
A_0=X,\qquad B_0=g(Y),
$$

and

$$
A_{n+1}=g(f(A_n)),\qquad B_{n+1}=g(f(B_n)).
$$

The map `g\circ f:X\to X` carries each difference layer

$$
A_n\setminus B_n
$$

bijectively onto the next difference layer

$$
A_{n+1}\setminus B_{n+1}.
$$

The desired bijection `h:X\to Y` is then assembled by using `f` on the layers
where `f` should be used, and using `g^{-1}` on the remaining part:

$$
h(x)=
\begin{cases}
f(x), & x\in A_n\setminus B_n\text{ for some }n,\\
g^{-1}(x), & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
$$

The point of the construction is not that one can guess the bijection by
inspection. The point is that the two injections provide enough structure to
divide `X` into pieces and define a genuine bijection piece by piece.

<CollapsibleProof title="Why the piecewise definition is plausible">
If `x` is not in any layer `A_n\setminus B_n`, then in particular it is not in
`A_0\setminus B_0=X\setminus g(Y)`. Hence `x\in g(Y)`, so `g^{-1}(x)` is
defined on that remaining part.

The layer condition prevents the two pieces of the definition from colliding.
The proof checks injectivity by showing that a collision between an
`f`-value and a `g^{-1}`-value would force an element into the next forbidden
layer. It checks surjectivity by starting with `y\in Y`, looking at `g(y)\in X`,
and deciding whether `g(y)` lies in one of the layers.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Common mistakes and subtle points

<CommonMistake title="Do not prove equality from one injection">
An injection `X\to Y` proves `|X|\le |Y|`. It does not by itself prove
`|X|=|Y|`. Equality requires a bijection, or injections in both directions plus
Cantor-Bernstein.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Do not count unreduced fractions as different rationals">
The symbols `1/1`, `2/2`, and `3/3` describe the same rational number. The
countability proof for `Q_+` avoids duplicates by using lowest terms.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="A surjection points in the opposite comparison direction">
If `f:X\to Y` is surjective, it is tempting to conclude immediately that
`|Y|\le |X|`. That conclusion is true with the axiom of choice, but the argument
postpone the proof until choice functions are introduced.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="What kind of function proves that two sets have the same cardinality?">
Name the function property and say what it does to elements.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
A bijection proves same cardinality. It pairs every element of the first set
with exactly one element of the second set and misses nothing.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the diagonal argument for Q+ not get stuck on any particular positive rational p/q?">
Think about the diagonal containing that fraction.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
If `p/q` is in lowest terms, it lies on the diagonal `p+q`. Since the diagonal
traversal eventually reaches every finite value of `p+q`, the fraction is
eventually listed.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If there are injections X to Y and Y to X, what theorem lets us conclude same cardinality?">
This is the antisymmetry part of cardinal comparison.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The Cantor-Bernstein theorem. It says that two opposite cardinal inequalities
force `|X|=|Y|`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Give an explicit enumeration of Z and explain why it has no repetitions.">
Start with 0, then alternate positive and negative integers.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Use

$$
0,1,-1,2,-2,3,-3,\ldots .
$$

Every positive integer `k` appears once, every negative integer `-k` appears
once, and `0` appears once. The positions are distinct, so no integer is
repeated.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Explain why the composition of two injections is injective.">
Use the definition of injective twice.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Let `f:X\to Y` and `g:Y\to Z` be injective. If

$$
(g\circ f)(x_1)=(g\circ f)(x_2),
$$

then `g(f(x_1))=g(f(x_2))`. Since `g` is injective, `f(x_1)=f(x_2)`. Since
`f` is injective, `x_1=x_2`. Hence `g\circ f` is injective.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does countability of Q+ imply countability of Q?">
Account for zero and negative rationals.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
If

$$
q_1,q_2,q_3,\ldots
$$

lists `Q_+`, then

$$
0,q_1,-q_1,q_2,-q_2,q_3,-q_3,\ldots
$$

lists all rational numbers. This gives an enumeration of `Q`.
</RevealSolution>
