Chapter 6 has already used bijections, injections, surjections, countability, and
the Axiom of Choice to compare sizes of sets. The final part of the chapter makes
an important warning explicit:

there is no single mathematical meaning of the word "large".

An interval can be short in length but as large as `R` in cardinality. The Cantor
set can have total removed length `1` and still have the same cardinality as the
real line. The rationals are countable, yet dense in `R`. The natural numbers are
well-ordered, while the integers and positive rationals are not well-ordered by
their usual orders.

These are not contradictions. They are different structures asking different
questions.

## Closed intervals and half-closed intervals

We have already used open intervals such as `(a,b)`. Now add the
closed version.

<Definition title="Closed interval">
Let `a,b\in R` with `a\le b`. The **closed interval** from `a` to `b` is

$$
[a,b]=\{x\in R\mid a\le x\le b\}.
$$
</Definition>

The endpoint convention matters:

- `(a,b)` excludes both endpoints;
- `[a,b]` includes both endpoints;
- `[a,b)` includes `a` but excludes `b`;
- `(a,b]` excludes `a` but includes `b`;
- `[a,\infty)` and `(-\infty,b]` are defined analogously.

This notation matters because later constructions often depend on
whether endpoints remain present. That will matter immediately for the Cantor
set: at each stage, closed intervals remain, so their endpoints are not removed.

<CommonMistake title="Do not treat interval notation as decoration">
The intervals `(0,1)` and `[0,1]` differ as subsets of `R`. They have the same
cardinality, but they are not the same set. In proofs, first decide whether you
are proving equality of sets, equality of cardinalities, or a statement about
length.
</CommonMistake>

## Intervals and cardinality

The interval `(0,1)` has the same cardinality as `R`.

<TheoremCard title="The interval `(0,1)` has cardinality `|R|`">
There is a bijection

$$
f:(0,1)\to R,\qquad
f(x)=\frac{2x-1}{x(x-1)}.
$$

Verifying that this function is bijective is a useful exercise.
</TheoremCard>

The proposition is deliberately striking. The interval `(0,1)` looks bounded and
short, while `R` is unbounded in both directions. Cardinality ignores metric
length and only asks whether the elements can be paired off exactly.

<WorkedExample title="Why boundedness does not control cardinality">
The open interval `(0,1)` is bounded in the usual order and metric on `R`. The
real line `R` is not bounded. Nevertheless, the displayed function gives a
one-to-one and onto correspondence between them.

So the statement

$$
|(0,1)|=|R|
$$

does not mean the two sets have the same length. It means there exists a
bijection between their elements.
</WorkedExample>

A useful exercise is to show that all intervals have the same cardinality.
For non-degenerate finite intervals, the first reduction is linear:

$$
x\longmapsto \frac{x-a}{b-a}
$$

maps `(a,b)` bijectively onto `(0,1)` when `a<b`. Half-infinite and infinite
intervals can then be compared to `(0,1)` through explicit bijections of the same
kind as the proposition above.

The lesson is that interval length and interval cardinality are measuring
different things.

## The Cantor set construction

The Cantor set is introduced to push this distinction further.

Start with

$$
C_0=[0,1].
$$

At each later stage, remove the open middle third of every interval that remains.

- Stage `0`:

  $$
  C_0=[0,1].
  $$

- Stage `1`: remove `(1/3,2/3)`, leaving

  $$
  C_1=\left[0,\frac13\right]\cup \left[\frac23,1\right].
  $$

- Stage `2`: remove the middle third from each of the two intervals in `C_1`,
  leaving

  $$
  C_2=
  \left[0,\frac19\right]\cup
  \left[\frac29,\frac13\right]\cup
  \left[\frac23,\frac79\right]\cup
  \left[\frac89,1\right].
  $$

- Stage `n`: `C_n` is the union of `2^n` closed intervals, each of length
  `(1/3)^n`.

<Definition title="Cantor set">
The **Cantor set** is the common intersection

$$
C=\bigcap_{n=0}^{\infty} C_n.
$$
</Definition>

The sets are nested:

$$
C_0\supset C_1\supset C_2\supset \cdots .
$$

This nesting is why the intersection is a meaningful object. A point belongs to
`C` exactly when it survives every stage of middle-third removal.

<WorkedExample title="Endpoints survive">
At stage 1, the open interval `(1/3,2/3)` is removed, but the endpoints `1/3`
and `2/3` stay.

At later stages, endpoints of remaining closed intervals again stay. Therefore
points such as

$$
0,\quad 1,\quad \frac13,\quad \frac23,\quad \frac19,\quad \frac29
$$

are not removed at the stage where they first appear as endpoints.
</WorkedExample>

## Ternary expansions and membership in `C`

Next give an arithmetic description using base `3`.

Every `x\in[0,1]` can be written in ternary form

$$
x=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{a_k}{3^k}
=(0.a_1a_2a_3\ldots)_3,
\qquad a_k\in\{0,1,2\}.
$$

As in decimal notation, representations are not always unique. For example, just
as `0.999...=1` in base `10`, one has

$$
(0.0222\ldots)_3=(0.1)_3.
$$

Use the non-terminating expansion when possible. With that
convention:

- removing `(1/3,2/3)` removes exactly the numbers whose first ternary digit is
  `1`;
- stage `2` removes numbers whose second ternary digit is `1`;
- in general, the Cantor set consists exactly of numbers in `[0,1]` that can be
  written in ternary using only the digits `0` and `2`.

<TheoremCard title="Ternary description of the Cantor set">
With the non-terminating ternary convention, a point `x\in[0,1]` belongs to `C`
if and only if it has a ternary expansion

$$
x=(0.a_1a_2a_3\ldots)_3
$$

where every digit `a_k` is either `0` or `2`.
</TheoremCard>

This description is useful because it replaces a geometric removal process by a
digit rule. Surviving every stage is the same as never using the digit `1` in the
ternary expansion.

## The length paradox

The construction removes many intervals. At stage `1`, it removes one interval
of length `1/3`. At stage `2`, it removes two intervals of length `1/9`. At
stage `n`, it removes

$$
2^{n-1}
$$

intervals, each of length

$$
\left(\frac13\right)^n.
$$

The total removed length is therefore

$$
L=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{2^{n-1}}{3^n}
=
\frac13\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac23\right)^k
=
\frac{1/3}{1-2/3}
=1.
$$

Since `[0,1]` has length `1`, this calculation suggests that the remaining set
should be extremely small. In the language of length, the Cantor set has no
length left after the removals.

But length is still not cardinality.

## The Cantor set has cardinality `|R|`

In fact, the Cantor set is not merely non-empty. It is uncountable,
and in fact has the same cardinality as `R`.

<TheoremCard title="The Cantor set has the same cardinality as `R`">
Let `S` be the set of all infinite binary sequences

$$
(s_1,s_2,s_3,\ldots),\qquad s_k\in\{0,1\}.
$$

Define

$$
f:S\to C,\qquad
f((s_1,s_2,s_3,\ldots))
=
\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{2s_k}{3^k}.
$$

This sends a binary sequence to the ternary expansion whose `k`th digit is
`0` if `s_k=0` and `2` if `s_k=1`. Thus the image lies in `C`.

This is a bijection. Since the set of infinite binary
sequences has cardinality `|2^N|`, and this cardinality is `|R|`, it follows that

$$
|C|=|R|.
$$
</TheoremCard>

<CommonMistake title="Zero length does not mean countable">
The Cantor set is often described as dust because it is produced by removing
open intervals at every scale. But the cardinality theorem says it still has as
many points as the real line. Length and cardinality are different measurements.
</CommonMistake>

## Density in the real line

The next section introduces a different notion of largeness.

<Definition title="Dense subset of `R`">
A subset `S\subset R` is **dense** if for every `r\in R` and every
`\epsilon>0`, there exists `s\in S` such that

$$
|r-s|<\epsilon.
$$
</Definition>

This definition does not ask how many elements `S` has. It asks whether elements
of `S` can approximate every real number arbitrarily well.

Equivalently, no matter where you stand on the real line and no matter how small
a tolerance you choose, some element of `S` lies within that tolerance.

## Integers are not dense

<TheoremCard title="`Z` is not dense in `R`">
Take

$$
r=\frac12,\qquad \epsilon=\frac14.
$$

For every integer `n`,

$$
\left|n-\frac12\right|\ge \frac12>\frac14.
$$

So no integer lies within distance `1/4` of `1/2`. Hence `Z` is not dense in
`R`.
</TheoremCard>

The proof only needs one failed target point and one failed tolerance. To show
that a set is not dense, you do not have to check every real number. You only
need to find a gap that the set cannot enter.

## Rationals are dense

<TheoremCard title="`Q` is dense in `R`">
Let `r\in R` and let `\epsilon>0`. By the Archimedean property, choose
`n\in N` such that

$$
n>\frac1\epsilon,
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
\frac1n<\epsilon.
$$

Choose the largest integer `m_0` satisfying

$$
m_0\le nr.
$$

Then

$$
m_0\le nr<m_0+1.
$$

Dividing by `n` gives

$$
0\le r-\frac{m_0}{n}<\frac1n<\epsilon.
$$

Thus

$$
q=\frac{m_0}{n}\in Q
$$

satisfies `|r-q|<\epsilon`. Therefore `Q` is dense in `R`.
</TheoremCard>

This proof shows exactly what density means: for any requested tolerance, a
rational approximation can be chosen inside that tolerance.

<CommonMistake title="Dense does not mean uncountable">
The rationals are dense in `R`, but earlier cardinality results show that `Q` is
countable. Density measures approximation, not cardinality.
</CommonMistake>

The same idea can be restricted to a subset of the real line.

<Definition title="Dense in a subset">
Let `T\subset R`. A subset `S\subset T` is **dense in `T`** if for every
`t\in T` and every `\epsilon>0`, there exists `s\in S` such that

$$
|t-s|<\epsilon.
$$
</Definition>

## Well-ordering

The last part of Chapter 6 turns from size and approximation to order.

<Definition title="Well-ordered set">
Let `(X,\le)` be a totally ordered set. We say `(X,\le)` is **well-ordered** if
every non-empty subset `S\subset X` has a minimum.
</Definition>

This is stronger than merely being totally ordered. A total order lets you compare
two elements. A well-order additionally says that every non-empty subcollection
has a first element.

<WorkedExample title="Finite initial segments of `N`">
In the von Neumann construction, a natural number `n` is identified with

$$
n=\{0,1,\ldots,n-1\}.
$$

Ordered by inclusion, this is the usual order on the finite initial segment.
Every non-empty subset has a least element, so each finite `n` is well-ordered.
</WorkedExample>

<TheoremCard title="The natural numbers are well-ordered">
The usual order on `N` is a well-order: every non-empty subset of `N` has a
minimum.
</TheoremCard>

The proof is by contradiction using induction. If a
non-empty subset `S\subset N` had no minimum, induction would show that
`0\notin S`, then `1\notin S`, then `2\notin S`, and so on. Hence no natural
number would lie in `S`, contradicting non-emptiness.

## Orders that are not well-orders

<TheoremCard title="`Z` is not well-ordered by the usual order">
The subset `Z\subset Z` has no minimum. For any `n\in Z`, the integer `n-1`
also lies in `Z` and satisfies `n-1<n`. Therefore no element can be first.
</TheoremCard>

<TheoremCard title="`Q^+` is not well-ordered by the usual order">
The subset `Q^+` has no minimum. For any positive rational `q`, the number
`q/2` is also positive rational and satisfies `q/2<q`.
</TheoremCard>

The second example is especially important because all elements are positive.
The failure is not caused by negative numbers. It is caused by an infinite
descending process with no first positive rational.

<CommonMistake title="A minimum is not the same as a lower bound">
The set `Q^+` has lower bounds in `R`, such as `0`, but `0\notin Q^+`. A
minimum of a set must belong to the set itself.
</CommonMistake>

## Countable sets can be well-ordered

Now separate two statements:

- a particular order may fail to be a well-order;
- the underlying set may still admit some other well-order.

<TheoremCard title="Every countable set can be well-ordered">
If `X` is finite, list it as

$$
X=\{x_1,\ldots,x_n\}
$$

and order the elements by their indices.

If `X` is countably infinite, choose a bijection

$$
f:N\to X.
$$

Define

$$
x\le_X y
\quad\text{if and only if}\quad
f^{-1}(x)\le f^{-1}(y)
\quad\text{in }N.
$$

This transports the well-ordering of `N` to `X`.
</TheoremCard>

For example, `Z` is not well-ordered by its usual order, but it is countable, so
it can be well-ordered by choosing an enumeration of its elements.

## The Well-Ordering Theorem

The chapter ends by connecting well-ordering to the Axiom of Choice.

<TheoremCard title="Well-Ordering Theorem">
The Axiom of Choice is equivalent to the statement:

every set `X` can be well-ordered.
</TheoremCard>

One direction is direct. If every set can be well-ordered, then for a family of
non-empty sets, well-order the union and choose from each member its least
element. That gives a choice function.

The other direction uses the Axiom of Choice to choose an element from every
non-empty subset of `X`, then tries to build an order by repeatedly choosing the
next unused element. This direction is more technical because making
the construction precise requires transfinite recursion, which is beyond the
course.

<CommonMistake title="The Well-Ordering Theorem is not saying the usual order works">
The usual order on `R`, `Z`, or `Q^+` may fail to be a well-order. The theorem
says that some well-order exists, assuming the Axiom of Choice. It does not give
a familiar or computationally useful order.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="At stage n of the Cantor construction, how many intervals remain and what is the length of each one?">
State both quantities in terms of `n`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
At stage `n`, the set `C_n` is the union of `2^n` closed intervals, each of
length `(1/3)^n`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the proof that `Z` is not dense only need to examine `r=1/2` and `epsilon=1/4`?">
Use the definition of not dense.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
To disprove density, it is enough to find one real number and one positive
tolerance such that no element of the proposed dense set lies within that
tolerance. The point `1/2` and radius `1/4` give such a gap for `Z`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is `Q` dense in `R` even though `Q` is countable?">
Name the two different ideas being compared.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Countability is a cardinality property. Density is an approximation property.
The rationals are countable, but the Archimedean argument shows that rational
numbers can approximate every real number arbitrarily closely.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is the usual order on `Z` not a well-order?">
Identify a non-empty subset with no minimum.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The subset `Z` itself has no minimum. If `n` were the minimum, then `n-1` would
be an integer smaller than `n`, a contradiction.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Use the ternary description to decide whether `(0.202020...)_3` lies in the Cantor set.">
Explain which digits matter.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The ternary expansion uses only the digits `0` and `2`. Under the convention
used here, this means the number survives every middle-third removal
stage. Therefore it lies in the Cantor set.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Prove that `Q^+` is not well-ordered by its usual order.">
Use the same ternary-expansion idea.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Assume `q\in Q^+`. Then `q/2\in Q^+` and `q/2<q`. So no positive rational can
be the minimum of `Q^+`. Since `Q^+` is a non-empty subset of itself with no
minimum, the usual order is not a well-order.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Let `X` be countably infinite and let `f:N\to X` be a bijection. Why does the transported order on `X` become a well-order?">
Use the well-ordering of `N`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
For a non-empty subset `S\subset X`, the preimage `f^{-1}(S)` is a non-empty
subset of `N`. Since `N` is well-ordered, `f^{-1}(S)` has a least element, say
`m`. Then `f(m)` is the least element of `S` under the transported order.
</RevealSolution>
