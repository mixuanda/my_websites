Prerequisite: you should already be comfortable reading short logical statements
from [1.1 Propositional logic](/en/notes/math1090/logic/propositional-logic).

## What the quantifiers say

<Definition title="Universal and existential quantifiers">
`∀x P(x)` means that `P(x)` is true for every allowed value of `x`.

`∃x P(x)` means that there is at least one allowed value of `x` for which
`P(x)` is true.
</Definition>

The allowed values of `x` come from the domain. Always keep the domain in mind.

## Negating quantifiers

The negation rules are small, but they matter a lot:

| Statement | Negation |
| --- | --- |
| `∀x P(x)` | `∃x ¬P(x)` |
| `∃x P(x)` | `∀x ¬P(x)` |

## Work through one example

<WorkedExample title="Negating a universal statement">
Original statement:

`For every real number x, x^2 >= 0.`

Negation:

`There exists a real number x such that x^2 < 0.`

Why this works:

1. Change "for every" to "there exists".
2. Negate the inside statement.
3. Keep the same domain.

<RevealSolution title="The pattern to remember">
When you negate a quantified statement, the outer quantifier flips and the
inside statement is negated.
</RevealSolution>
</WorkedExample>

## Common mistake

<CommonMistake title="Do not stop after changing only the quantifier">
`¬∀x P(x)` is not the same as `∀x ¬P(x)`. The first says "not every `x` works",
which is the same as "there exists an `x` for which `P(x)` fails".
</CommonMistake>

## Quick check

<QuickCheck prompt="Write the negation of `∃x (x is a student and x has submitted the form)`">
`∀x (x is not a student or x has not submitted the form)`.
</QuickCheck>
