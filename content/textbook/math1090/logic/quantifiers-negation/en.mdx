title: "1.3 Quantifiers and negation"
description: "Read predicates, domains, and quantifiers carefully, then negate quantified statements without losing the scope or meaning."
---

Quantifiers turn open statements into propositions. That is their main job in
the course: they let us talk about every allowed object, or at least one
allowed object, without writing out every case separately.

## Predicates and domains

<Definition title="Predicate">
A predicate is a statement with one or more free variables. It becomes a
proposition only after every free variable has been assigned a value or bound
by a quantifier.
</Definition>

<Definition title="Domain">
The domain is the set of allowed values for the variables.
</Definition>

The domain matters every time you read `∀` or `∃`. The same formal string can
mean different things in different domains.

For example, the statement `x^2 = 1` behaves differently over the natural
numbers, integers, rationals, and reals. If you do not state the domain, you do
not yet have a complete statement.

<CommonMistake title="Do not forget the domain">
`∀x P(x)` is incomplete unless it is clear which values `x` may take. In
practice, the domain is either stated explicitly or understood from context.
</CommonMistake>

## Universal and existential quantifiers

<Definition title="Quantifiers">
`∀x P(x)` means that `P(x)` is true for every allowed value of `x`.

`∃x P(x)` means that there is at least one allowed value of `x` for which
`P(x)` is true.
</Definition>

If you prefer words:

- `∀` means "for all" or "for every."
- `∃` means "there exists."

The statement is not finished until the quantifier is attached. For that
reason, `P(x)` alone is not a proposition, but `∀x P(x)` and `∃x P(x)` are.

<WorkedExample title="Read a quantified statement as English">
Let `P(x)` mean "x is divisible by 2" and let the domain be the integers.

Then `∀x P(x)` means:

"Every integer is divisible by 2."

That statement is false. By contrast, `∃x P(x)` means:

"There is an integer that is divisible by 2."

That statement is true, for example `x = 2`.

<RevealSolution title="What the quantifier is doing">
The quantifier tells you how many objects the statement is about. It does not
change the underlying predicate; it changes the logical force of the sentence.
</RevealSolution>
</WorkedExample>

## Negating quantified statements

<TheoremCard title="Quantifier negation">
The basic negation laws are:

$$
¬∀x\,P(x) \equiv ∃x\,¬P(x)
$$

$$
¬∃x\,P(x) \equiv ∀x\,¬P(x)
$$
</TheoremCard>

Read these carefully. The negation always does two things:

1. It flips the outer quantifier.
2. It negates the predicate inside.

That is the whole pattern.

<WorkedExample title="Negate the definition of a prime number">
The worksheet defines `n` to be prime by saying that, for every natural number
`d`, if `d` divides `n`, then `d = 1` or `d = n`.

In symbols:

$$
∀d ∈ N,\; d \mid n \to (d = 1 \lor d = n).
$$

Its negation is:

$$
∃d ∈ N \text{ such that } d \mid n \text{ and } d \neq 1 \text{ and } d \neq n.
$$

This is exactly the meaning of "n is not prime": there is a divisor other than
the trivial ones.

<RevealSolution title="Why the negation is right">
The negation does not say only that the universal statement fails in some vague
way. It identifies a concrete counterexample `d`.
</RevealSolution>
</WorkedExample>

<WorkedExample title="Negate a statement about students">
Suppose `Student(x)` means "x is a student" and `Submitted(x)` means "x has
submitted the form."

The statement

$$
∃x\,(Student(x) \land Submitted(x))
$$

means that at least one student has submitted the form.

Its negation is

$$
∀x\,(\neg Student(x) \lor \neg Submitted(x)).
$$

In words: every allowed object fails at least one of the two properties.

<RevealSolution title="How to read the negation">
`¬(A ∧ B)` becomes `¬A ∨ ¬B`, so the inside conjunction turns into a disjunction
after negation.
</RevealSolution>
</WorkedExample>

## Why quantifier order matters

The order of quantifiers is part of the meaning.

`∀x ∃y P(x, y)` says that for each `x`, there is a possibly different `y` that
works.

`∃y ∀x P(x, y)` says that one single `y` works for every `x`.

Those are very different statements. The first allows the choice of `y` to
depend on `x`. The second does not.

<WorkedExample title="One borrower per book is not the same as one person for all books">
Let `Book(b)` mean that `b` is a book, and `Borrows(x, b)` mean that `x`
borrows `b`.

The statement

$$
∀b\,(Book(b) \to ∃x\,Borrows(x, b))
$$

means that every book is borrowed by at least one person.

The statement

$$
∃x\,∀b\,(Book(b) \to Borrows(x, b))
$$

means that one person borrows every book.

The first statement can be true even when the second is false.

<RevealSolution title="What the example teaches">
When you move a quantifier across another quantifier, you usually change the
meaning. Do not do that unless you have checked the logic carefully.
</RevealSolution>
</WorkedExample>

## Common incorrect formalizations

<CommonMistake title="Implication is not conjunction">
If the intended meaning is "for every student, if the student enrolls, then the
student satisfies the prerequisite," then the correct shape is

$$
∀x\,(Student(x) \to (Enrolls(x) \to Prereq(x)))
$$

or, more naturally,

$$
∀x\,((Student(x) \land Enrolls(x)) \to Prereq(x)).
$$

Writing `Student(x) \land Enrolls(x)` without the implication would state a very
different and much stronger claim.
</CommonMistake>

<WorkedExample title="Formalize a course requirement">
Say that `Taken(x, m)` means "student `x` has taken mathematics course `m`."
Then the statement "Every student has taken at least one mathematics course"
can be written as

$$
∀x\,(Student(x) \to ∃m\,(MathCourse(m) \land Taken(x, m))).
$$

This is stronger and clearer than trying to compress the meaning into a single
unstructured sentence.

<RevealSolution title="Why the nested quantifier is necessary">
The existential quantifier must sit inside the universal one, because the
chosen course may depend on which student you are talking about.
</RevealSolution>
</WorkedExample>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Write the negation of `∀x P(x)` and simplify it.">
Flip the quantifier and negate the predicate.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`∃x ¬P(x)`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What is the negation of `∃x (x is a student and x has submitted the form)`?">
Use De Morgan's law inside the predicate.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`∀x (x is not a student or x has not submitted the form)`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Which statement is stronger: `∀x∃y P(x,y)` or `∃y∀x P(x,y)`?">
Ask whether one witness must work for all `x`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`∃y∀x P(x,y)` is stronger, because the same `y` must work for every `x`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Translate `∀b(Book(b) → ∃x Borrows(x,b))` into English.">
Read the quantifiers from the outside in.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Every book is borrowed by at least one person.
</RevealSolution>
