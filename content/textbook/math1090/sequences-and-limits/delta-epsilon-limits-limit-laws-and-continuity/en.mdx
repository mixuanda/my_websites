Chapter 5 now shifts from sequences to functions. The new difficulty is that a
sequence approaches its limit through the discrete parameter `n`, while a
function argument `x` can move toward a point `a` through infinitely many nearby
real values from both sides.

That is why the lecture notes introduce the `\delta`-`\varepsilon` definition.

## From sequence limits to function limits

In the previous chapter, the definition of

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}x_n=L
$$

said that every small tolerance around `L` can be achieved by going far enough
out in the sequence.

For functions, we want an analogous sentence:

as `x` approaches `a`, the function value `f(x)` approaches `L`.

But now “going far enough out” no longer makes sense. We need a new way to say
that `x` is close enough to `a`. That role is played by `\delta`.

## Open intervals and punctured domains

The notes first specify the kind of domains they want to use.

<Definition title="Open interval">
An **open interval** is a subset of `R` of one of the forms

$$
(b,c)=\{x\in R\mid b<x<c\},
$$

$$
(-\infty,c)=\{x\in R\mid x<c\},
$$

$$
(b,\infty)=\{x\in R\mid b<x\},
$$

where `b,c\in R` and `b<c`.
</Definition>

To define a limit at a point `a`, the notes consider functions defined on an
open interval with the point `a` removed. That removal is important: a limit is
about what happens **near** `a`, not necessarily what happens **at** `a`.

## The formal delta-epsilon definition

<Definition title="Limit of a function at a point">
Suppose `I` is an open interval containing `a\in R`, and let
`f:I\setminus\{a\}\to R`. We say

$$
\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L
$$

if and only if for every `\varepsilon>0`, there exists `\delta>0` such that

$$
0<|x-a|<\delta \implies |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon.
$$
</Definition>

Read the implication carefully:

- `0<|x-a|<\delta` means `x` is close to `a`, but not equal to `a`;
- `|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon` means the function value lands inside the
  `\varepsilon`-band around `L`.

So the definition says:

> every requested output accuracy `\varepsilon` can be guaranteed by forcing the
> input `x` into a sufficiently small punctured neighborhood of `a`.

<CommonMistake title="The limit definition ignores the value at a">
The condition `0<|x-a|` removes the point `a` itself. So `f(a)` may be
undefined, or may differ from `L`, while the limit still exists.
</CommonMistake>

## First example: `\lim_{x\to 3}(2x+1)=7`

Example 19 in the notes is the basic linear test case.

<WorkedExample title="Choosing `\delta=\varepsilon/2`">
Let `f(x)=2x+1`, `a=3`, and `L=7`. Then

$$
|f(x)-7|
=
|(2x+1)-7|
=
|2x-6|
=
2|x-3|.
$$

So in order to force `|f(x)-7|<\varepsilon`, it is enough to make

$$
|x-3|<\frac{\varepsilon}{2}.
$$

Therefore choose

$$
\delta=\frac{\varepsilon}{2}.
$$

Then whenever `0<|x-3|<\delta`,

$$
|f(x)-7|=2|x-3|<2\delta=2\cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2}=\varepsilon.
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{x\to 3}(2x+1)=7.
$$
</WorkedExample>

This example shows the standard proof pattern:

1. start with `|f(x)-L|`,
2. simplify it into an expression involving `|x-a|`,
3. choose `\delta` to make that expression smaller than `\varepsilon`.

## Two more basic examples from the notes

<WorkedExample title="Constant functions">
If `f(x)=c` for all `x`, then

$$
|f(x)-c|=|c-c|=0<\varepsilon
$$

for every `\varepsilon>0` and every `x`.

So any positive number can be chosen as `\delta`, and

$$
\lim_{x\to a}c=c.
$$
</WorkedExample>

<WorkedExample title="A function with a hole">
Consider

$$
f:(0,\infty)\setminus\{2\}\to R,\qquad
f(x)=\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}.
$$

The formula is undefined at `x=2`, but for `x\ne 2` it simplifies to

$$
f(x)=x+2.
$$

So near `x=2`,

$$
|f(x)-4|=|(x+2)-4|=|x-2|.
$$

Therefore choosing `\delta=\varepsilon` works. If `0<|x-2|<\delta`, then

$$
|f(x)-4|<\varepsilon.
$$

Hence

$$
\lim_{x\to 2}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}=4,
$$

even though `f(2)` is not defined.
</WorkedExample>

## How limits can fail to exist

The notes next explain how to show that a proposed limit does **not** exist.

To prove `\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\ne L`, one must find a single `\varepsilon>0` such
that no matter how small `\delta` is chosen, some point `x` with
`0<|x-a|<\delta` still satisfies

$$
|f(x)-L|\ge \varepsilon.
$$

Example 22 uses

$$
f(x)=\frac{|x|}{x},
$$

which equals `1` for `x>0` and `-1` for `x<0`.

<WorkedExample title="Why `|x|/x` has no limit at `0`">
Take `\varepsilon=1`. For any `\delta>0`, choose

$$
x_1=\frac{\delta}{2}>0,
\qquad
x_2=-\frac{\delta}{2}<0.
$$

Then both satisfy `|x_i|<\delta`, but

$$
f(x_1)=1,\qquad f(x_2)=-1.
$$

No single real number `L` can lie within distance `1` of both `1` and `-1`.
Therefore the function has no limit at `0`.
</WorkedExample>

This example captures a common failure mode: left-hand and right-hand behavior
do not agree.

## Algebra of limits

Once some basic limits are known, the notes show how to build new ones from old
ones.

<TheoremCard title="Limit laws">
If `\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L` and `\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=M`, then:

- `\lim_{x\to a}(f(x)+g(x))=L+M`,
- `\lim_{x\to a}(f(x)g(x))=LM`,
- if `M\ne 0`, then

$$
\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{L}{M}.
$$
</TheoremCard>

The proof of the sum law is the cleanest illustration: split the allowed error
`\varepsilon` into two halves and use the triangle inequality,

$$
|f(x)+g(x)-(L+M)|\le |f(x)-L|+|g(x)-M|.
$$

The product law needs one extra idea: before estimating the product error, one
must first show that `|f(x)|` stays bounded near `a`.

<WorkedExample title="Using the sum law">
The notes consider `f(x)=2x+1` and `g(x)=\sqrt{x}` near `x=4`. Once the basic
limits

$$
\lim_{x\to 4}(2x+1)=9,
\qquad
\lim_{x\to 4}\sqrt{x}=2
$$

are known, the sum law gives

$$
\lim_{x\to 4}\bigl((2x+1)+\sqrt{x}\bigr)=9+2=11.
$$
</WorkedExample>

## The sequential characterization

Theorem 3 in the notes connects function limits back to sequence limits.

<TheoremCard title="Sequential characterization of function limits">
Let `f:I\setminus\{a\}\to R`. Then

$$
\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L
$$

if and only if for every sequence `(x_n)` with `x_n\in I\setminus\{a\}` for all
`n` and `x_n\to a`, we have

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}f(x_n)=L.
$$
</TheoremCard>

This theorem is extremely useful because it gives two complementary methods:

1. to prove a limit exists, check every sequence approaching `a`;
2. to prove a limit does not exist, find two sequences approaching `a` that
   give incompatible output behavior.

Example 25 applies this idea to `f(x)=\sin(1/x)` near `0`.

<WorkedExample title="Two sequences proving that `\sin(1/x)` has no limit at `0`">
Take

$$
x_n=\frac{1}{2\pi n},
\qquad
y_n=\frac{1}{2\pi n+\pi/2}.
$$

Then `x_n\to 0` and `y_n\to 0`, but

$$
f(x_n)=\sin(2\pi n)=0\to 0,
$$

while

$$
f(y_n)=\sin(2\pi n+\pi/2)=1\to 1.
$$

Since two sequences approaching the same point produce different limiting
function values, `\lim_{x\to 0}\sin(1/x)` does not exist.
</WorkedExample>

## Continuity

One of the main purposes of limits is to make the idea of continuity precise.

<Definition title="Continuity at a point">
Let `f:I\to R` and `a\in I`. The function `f` is **continuous at `a`** if

$$
\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a).
$$

Equivalently, in `\varepsilon`-`\delta` language:

$$
\forall \varepsilon>0\ \exists \delta>0\text{ such that }
|x-a|<\delta \implies |f(x)-f(a)|<\varepsilon.
$$
</Definition>

Notice what changed: the condition `0<|x-a|` disappeared. Continuity really does
care about the function value at the point.

The notes give a discontinuous example:

$$
f(x)=
\begin{cases}
0,& x\ne 0,\\
1,& x=0.
\end{cases}
$$

Here the nearby values are all `0`, so the limit near `0` is not `f(0)=1`.
Therefore the function is discontinuous at `0`.

<CommonMistake title="A function can have a limit at a without being defined at a">
The hole example shows this clearly. Limits are about nearby values. Continuity
adds the extra requirement that the function is defined at the point and that
its value agrees with the limit.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the function-limit definition use `0 < |x-a| < \delta` instead of only `|x-a| < \delta`?">
Think about whether the value at `a` should matter for the limit.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Because the limit concerns points near `a`, not necessarily the point `a`
itself. The strict inequality `0<|x-a|` removes `x=a` from the condition.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="For the proof that `\lim_{x\to 3}(2x+1)=7`, what choice of delta works for a given epsilon?">
Use the identity `|f(x)-7|=2|x-3|`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Choose `\delta=\varepsilon/2`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why can `\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}` have a limit at `x=2` even though the formula is undefined there?">
Recall what the limit definition does and does not inspect.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Because the definition only checks points with `0<|x-2|<\delta`. It never asks
for a value at `x=2` itself, so a hole at the point does not prevent the limit
from existing.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Prove that `\lim_{x\to 4}\sqrt{x}=2`.">
Rationalize `|\sqrt{x}-2|` and also keep x near 4 so the denominator stays away
from zero.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
We use

$$
|\sqrt{x}-2|
=
\frac{|x-4|}{\sqrt{x}+2}.
$$

Choose

$$
\delta=\min(1,2\varepsilon).
$$

If `0<|x-4|<\delta`, then in particular `x\in(3,5)`, so `\sqrt{x}+2>2`. Hence

$$
|\sqrt{x}-2|
=
\frac{|x-4|}{\sqrt{x}+2}
<
\frac{|x-4|}{2}
<
\frac{\delta}{2}
\le \varepsilon.
$$

Therefore `\lim_{x\to 4}\sqrt{x}=2`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Use the sequential characterization to show again that `\lim_{x\to 0}\sin(1/x)` does not exist.">
You only need two carefully chosen sequences.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Take

$$
x_n=\frac{1}{2\pi n},
\qquad
y_n=\frac{1}{2\pi n+\pi/2}.
$$

Both sequences approach `0`. But

$$
\sin(1/x_n)=\sin(2\pi n)=0
$$

for all `n`, while

$$
\sin(1/y_n)=\sin(2\pi n+\pi/2)=1
$$

for all `n`. Since the image sequences have different limits, the function limit
at `0` cannot exist.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What extra condition must be added to ‘the limit of f(x) as x approaches a exists’ in order to conclude that f is continuous at a?">
Compare the definitions of limit and continuity.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
You must also have `f(a)` defined and equal to the limit:

$$
\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a).
$$

That equality is exactly the definition of continuity at `a`.
</RevealSolution>
