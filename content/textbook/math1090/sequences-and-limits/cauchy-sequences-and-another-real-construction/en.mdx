title: "5.2 Cauchy sequences and another model of the reals"
description: "Introduce Cauchy sequences as an internal convergence test and use equivalence classes of rational Cauchy sequences to sketch a second construction of R."
---

The previous note defined convergence by comparing sequence terms with a
previously known limit `L`. This note asks a harder question:

What if we want to recognize convergence **before** we already know the limit as
an existing real number?

That question leads to the notion of a **Cauchy sequence** and to a second
construction of the real numbers.

## Why we need an internal test for convergence

The lecture notes motivate the new definition with a sequence of rational
numbers whose terms appear to close in on some point of the line:

$$
1,\qquad
1+\frac12,\qquad
1+\frac12+\frac1{3\cdot 2},\qquad
1+\frac12+\frac1{3\cdot 2}+\frac1{4\cdot 3\cdot 2\cdot 1},\ \ldots
$$

Visually, the terms seem to gather around a limiting point. But if we are
trying to **construct** the real numbers from the rationals, then we do not yet
have the right to name that limiting point as an already available element of
`R`.

So instead of asking whether the sequence is close to some external point `L`,
we ask whether the terms are getting close to **each other**.

## The definition of Cauchy sequence

<Definition title="Cauchy sequence">
A sequence `(x_n)` is **Cauchy** if for every `\varepsilon>0`, there exists
`N` such that

$$
|x_n-x_m|<\varepsilon
$$

for all `n,m>N`.
</Definition>

This definition has the same quantifier shape as the limit definition, but the
comparison target has changed:

- for an ordinary limit, you compare `x_n` with a fixed number `L`;
- for a Cauchy condition, you compare late terms of the sequence with one
  another.

So a Cauchy sequence is one whose tail fits into narrower and narrower bands.

<CommonMistake title="Cauchy does not mean monotone">
A Cauchy sequence does not have to move only upward or only downward. The
definition says nothing about monotonicity. It only says that late terms become
uniformly close to one another.
</CommonMistake>

## Why convergent sequences are automatically Cauchy

The notes record the following proposition.

<TheoremCard title="If a sequence converges, then it is Cauchy">
Suppose `(x_n)` has a limit `L`. Then `(x_n)` is a Cauchy sequence.
</TheoremCard>

Although the source leaves the proof as an exercise, the idea is short and very
important.

<CollapsibleProof title="Proof idea using the triangle inequality">
Assume `x_n\to L`, and let `\varepsilon>0` be given.

Because `x_n\to L`, there exists `N` such that for every `n>N`,

$$
|x_n-L|<\frac{\varepsilon}{2}.
$$

Then whenever `n,m>N`, the triangle inequality gives

$$
|x_n-x_m|
=
|(x_n-L)+(L-x_m)|
\le |x_n-L|+|x_m-L|
<
\frac{\varepsilon}{2}+\frac{\varepsilon}{2}
=\varepsilon.
$$

So `(x_n)` is Cauchy.
</CollapsibleProof>

This theorem says that genuine convergence always forces the sequence tail to
compress.

## Equivalent Cauchy sequences

If Cauchy sequences are going to represent real numbers, then different
sequences that “head toward the same place” should count as the same real.

<Definition title="Equivalent Cauchy sequences">
Two Cauchy sequences `(x_n)` and `(y_n)` are **equivalent** if for every
`\varepsilon>0`, there exists `N` such that

$$
|x_n-y_m|<\varepsilon
$$

for all `n,m>N`.
</Definition>

This condition says that the two tails eventually lie arbitrarily close to one
another. Intuitively, they are describing the same limiting point on the line.

The notes then state that this notion of equivalence is really an equivalence
relation on the set of Cauchy sequences. The triangle inequality is the key tool
for checking symmetry and transitivity.

## The alternative construction of `R`

Now the lecture notes turn the idea into a definition.

<Definition title="Reals as equivalence classes of rational Cauchy sequences">
Let `R` be the set of equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences of rational
numbers. These equivalence classes form another model of the real numbers.
</Definition>

This is a big shift in viewpoint:

- in the Dedekind-cut model, a real number is a left/right split of `Q`;
- in the Cauchy-sequence model, a real number is a whole family of rational
  sequences that become indistinguishable in the limit.

Neither model is “more real” than the other. They are two rigorous ways to
build the same number system.

## How rationals sit inside the model

Exercise 54 asks how to think of rational numbers inside this construction. The
answer is natural: a rational `q` is represented by the constant Cauchy sequence

$$
(q,q,q,q,\ldots).
$$

<WorkedExample title="Representatives of `1/2`">
The real number `1/2` can be represented by the constant sequence

$$
\left(\frac12,\frac12,\frac12,\ldots\right).
$$

It can also be represented by other rational Cauchy sequences that converge to
the same point, for example

$$
\left(\frac12+\frac11,\frac12+\frac12,\frac12+\frac13,\frac12+\frac14,\ldots\right).
$$

The second sequence is not constant, but its terms get arbitrarily close to
`1/2`, so it belongs to the same equivalence class.
</WorkedExample>

The real number is therefore **not** any one representative sequence by itself.
It is the full equivalence class.

<CommonMistake title="A real number is an equivalence class, not a favourite representative">
Once this model is adopted, changing from one representative Cauchy sequence to
another equivalent one does not change the real number. The representative is a
description, not the object itself.
</CommonMistake>

## Why boundedness matters

The notes take for granted the lemma that every Cauchy sequence is bounded. That
fact is crucial because it allows multiplication to work cleanly.

If `(x_n)` and `(y_n)` are Cauchy, then the notes prove:

- `(x_n+y_n)` is Cauchy;
- `(x_ny_n)` is Cauchy.

For multiplication, one needs a common bound `M` so that

$$
|x_my_m-x_ny_n|
\le
|x_m|\cdot |y_m-y_n|+|y_n|\cdot |x_m-x_n|
\le
M|y_m-y_n|+M|x_m-x_n|.
$$

Once the two original sequences are Cauchy, the right-hand side can be made as
small as desired.

## Operations and order on equivalence classes

After establishing that termwise sums and products of Cauchy sequences stay
Cauchy, the notes define addition and multiplication on equivalence classes.

So if `[(x_n)]` and `[(y_n)]` are real numbers in this model, then:

$$
[(x_n)] + [(y_n)] := [(x_n+y_n)],
$$

$$
[(x_n)] \cdot [(y_n)] := [(x_ny_n)].
$$

The notes also define an order by eventual comparison of representatives: one
class is below another when sufficiently late terms of a representative of the
first lie below sufficiently late terms of a representative of the second.

The serious work is then to check that all these definitions are
well-defined. That means the answer does not depend on which representative
sequences you chose.

## The conclusion of the construction

<TheoremCard title="The reals form a complete ordered field">
The lecture notes conclude that the real numbers obtained from rational Cauchy
sequences form a complete ordered field.
</TheoremCard>

The course does not prove every detail in class. Some parts are left as
exercises, including the well-definedness of operations and the final proof of
completeness. But conceptually the message is clear:

- Cauchy sequences capture the idea of internal convergence;
- equivalence classes prevent different approximating sequences from giving
  different names to the same real;
- completeness is built in, because every Cauchy sequence should already have a
  place to land in the completed system.

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="What is the difference between the definitions of ‘convergent’ and ‘Cauchy’?">
Focus on what each definition compares `x_n` with.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
A convergent sequence compares each late term `x_n` with a fixed limit `L`,
while a Cauchy sequence compares late terms `x_n` and `x_m` directly with one
another.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="How does the rational number q appear inside the Cauchy-sequence model of R?">
Think of the simplest possible Cauchy sequence.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It appears as the equivalence class of the constant sequence
`(q,q,q,q,\ldots)`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is boundedness useful when proving that products of Cauchy sequences are Cauchy?">
Look at the estimate for `|x_my_m-x_ny_n|`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Boundedness lets us replace `|x_m|` and `|y_n|` by a common constant `M`, so the
product difference can be controlled by the small Cauchy differences
`|x_m-x_n|` and `|y_m-y_n|`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Show that every constant rational sequence is Cauchy.">
Use the fact that all pairwise differences are zero.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Let `x_n=q` for all `n`, where `q\in Q`. Then for every `n,m`,

$$
|x_n-x_m|=|q-q|=0.
$$

So for any `\varepsilon>0`, every `N` works. Hence every constant rational
sequence is Cauchy.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why should two equivalent Cauchy sequences be thought of as the same real number?">
Explain it in terms of their tails.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Because equivalence means that for every tolerance `\varepsilon>0`, the tails of
the two sequences eventually lie within `\varepsilon` of one another. So the two
sequences cannot be distinguished by their limiting behavior; they describe the
same point of the completed number line.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Suppose a sequence converges to L. Which theorem from this note tells you something immediately about the distances |x_n-x_m| for large n,m?">
Name the theorem and the conclusion.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The theorem says that every convergent sequence is Cauchy. Therefore, once `n`
and `m` are large enough, the distance `|x_n-x_m|` must be small.
</RevealSolution>
