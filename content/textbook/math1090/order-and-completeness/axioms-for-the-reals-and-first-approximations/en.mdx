## Why define the reals before constructing them?

In earlier chapters, the pattern was:

1. decide what structure we want,
2. build a model that has that structure,
3. check the construction carefully.

The lecture notes now do the same for the real numbers. Before giving a formal
construction, they first say what the reals are supposed to *be* from a
structural point of view.

That is good mathematical practice. If you do not know the target properties
first, then even a beautiful construction can feel unmotivated.

## The formal target

<Definition title="Model of the real numbers">
A set `R` is a **model of the real numbers** if it is equipped with:

- elements `0,1\in R`,
- binary operations `+` and `\cdot`,
- a total order `\le`,

such that:

- `(R,0,1,+,\cdot,\le)` is an ordered field;
- `(R,\le)` is complete.
</Definition>

This definition compresses the whole chapter into one sentence:

> the reals are an ordered field with no missing least upper bounds or greatest
> lower bounds.

<WorkedExample title="Why Q is not yet a model of the reals">
The rational numbers already satisfy the ordered-field part. What fails is
completeness.

The set

$$
S=\{x\in Q\mid x^2<2\}
$$

is nonempty and bounded above in `Q`, but it has no rational supremum. So `Q`
is close to the target, yet still not the real-number system.
</WorkedExample>

## Why decimal expansions are not the full story

A first instinct is to say:

"A real number is just an infinite decimal expansion."

That instinct is useful, but the notes warn that it is not yet the cleanest
definition.

There are at least two reasons.

- The same real number can have more than one decimal expansion, as in
  `0.9999\ldots = 1`.
- Decimal expansions force a specific base, such as base 10, even though the
  idea of the real numbers should not depend on that arbitrary choice.

So the notes do not reject decimal intuition. Instead, they use it as a guide
toward a more structural construction.

## The approximation idea

Suppose a real number is written informally as

$$
r=10.234890234809234\ldots
$$

Then we can approximate `r` from below and above by rational numbers:

$$
10<r<11,
$$

$$
10.2<r<10.3,
$$

$$
10.23<r<10.24,
$$

and so on.

This is the key insight of section 4.7: even if we do not yet have a final
construction, a real number can already be understood through the rational
approximations that trap it more and more tightly.

<WorkedExample title="Lower and upper rational fences">
For a decimal expansion such as `r=3.1415\ldots`, the first few rational fences
look like

$$
3<r<4,\qquad 3.1<r<3.2,\qquad 3.14<r<3.15.
$$

Each extra digit gives a narrower rational interval containing `r`.
</WorkedExample>

## Dividing the rationals into two camps

From this approximation viewpoint, the real number `r` determines two sets of
rationals:

$$
A=\{q\in Q\mid q<r\},
\qquad
B=\{q\in Q\mid q>r\}.
$$

The important idea is not the notation itself. The important idea is that a
real number can be read as a boundary that separates the rationals into a left
camp and a right camp.

That is exactly the motivation behind the Dedekind-cut construction in the next
part of the lecture notes. Section 4.7 is still motivational rather than fully
formal, but it already tells you what kind of object a real number should be:
something that organizes the rationals by comparison.

## What section 4.7 does and does not do

<TheoremCard title="Section 4.7 is a construction strategy, not yet the finished construction">
The goal of section 4.7 is to explain *why* a real number should be describable
through rational approximations and left/right rational sets. The fully formal
Dedekind-cut definition comes only afterward.
</TheoremCard>

This distinction matters. If you treat section 4.7 as already giving the final
definition, you miss the role of the next step. The notes are building
motivation first, then turning that motivation into a rigorous object.

<CommonMistake title="Infinite decimal strings are not automatically a finished definition">
An infinite decimal expansion gives excellent intuition, but by itself it does
not settle issues such as multiple expansions for the same number or the base
dependence of the notation. That is why the notes move from decimal intuition to
a structural construction.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Section 4.7 is not yet the full Dedekind-cut theory">
At this stage, the notes are telling you how to *think* about a real number: as
something determined by rational approximations and a left/right split of `Q`.
The formal cut definition and the verification of field properties come later.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Which property does Q fail, so that it cannot be a model of the real numbers?">
Compare Q with the formal definition above.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
`Q` fails completeness. It is an ordered field, but not every nonempty bounded
subset of `Q` has its supremum and infimum inside `Q`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What information do the inequalities 10.23 < r < 10.24 give you about r?">
State it in terms of rational approximation.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
They place `r` inside a narrow rational interval. In other words, they give a
lower rational approximation and an upper rational approximation that trap `r`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="For a real number written informally as 3.14159..., write the first four lower/upper rational intervals suggested by its decimal expansion.">
Start with the integer part and then reveal one more digit at a time.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
One possible sequence is

$$
3<r<4,
$$

$$
3.1<r<3.2,
$$

$$
3.14<r<3.15,
$$

$$
3.141<r<3.142.
$$

Each step keeps `r` between two rationals while shrinking the interval.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the identity 0.999... = 1 show that raw decimal strings are not an ideal primary definition of the reals?">
Focus on uniqueness of representation.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Because two different decimal strings can represent the same real number. If raw
strings were the definition, then `0.999...` and `1.000...` would appear to be
different objects even though they describe the same number. So one needs an
additional equivalence rule or a more structural definition.
</RevealSolution>
