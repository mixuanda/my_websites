## Completeness is an existence property

The previous note introduced supremum and infimum. Completeness asks whether
those extremal bounds actually exist whenever they *should* exist.

This is the first point where `Q` and `R` separate decisively. Algebraically,
`Q` already looks very strong: it is a field, and it comes with a familiar total
order. But order-theoretically, `Q` still misses some boundary points.

## The definition

<Definition title="Complete ordered set">
An ordered set `X` is **complete** if:

- every nonempty subset `Y\subseteq X` that is bounded above has a supremum in
  `X`;
- every nonempty subset `Y\subseteq X` that is bounded below has an infimum in
  `X`.
</Definition>

So completeness is not about having many elements. It is about never losing the
least upper bound or greatest lower bound when those bounds ought to be there.

<WorkedExample title="Finite ordered sets are complete">
If `X` is a finite ordered set, every nonempty subset already has a largest and
smallest element. Those elements automatically serve as supremum and infimum.

So finite ordered sets are complete for a simple reason: the relevant extremes
are already attained inside the set.
</WorkedExample>

The interesting question is what happens for infinite ordered sets such as `Q`
and `R`.

## The rational counterexample

The standard counterexample is

$$
S=\{x\in Q\mid x^2<2\}.
$$

This set is not empty, because `1\in S`. It is also bounded above in `Q`; for
instance `2` is an upper bound.

What would happen if `Q` were complete? Then `S` would have a rational
supremum. The whole argument below shows that no rational number can play that
role.

<TheoremCard title="Q is not complete">
The ordered set `(Q,\le)` is not complete.
</TheoremCard>

## Why no rational number can be sup(S)

The proof splits into three cases according to the square of a rational
candidate `s`.

### Case 1: `s^2<2`

Then `s` is still too small. In fact, the lecture notes show that one can move a
little to the right and remain inside `S`.

<CollapsibleProof title="Why s^2<2 cannot be an upper bound">
Assume `s\in Q` and `s^2<2`.

If `s\le 0`, then `1>s` and `1^2<2`, so `s` is not an upper bound.

Now assume `s>0`. Let

$$
M=2-s^2>0.
$$

We want a small rational `\varepsilon>0` such that `r=s+\varepsilon` still
satisfies `r^2<2`. Expanding gives

$$
r^2=s^2+2s\varepsilon+\varepsilon^2.
$$

So it is enough to make

$$
\varepsilon(2s+\varepsilon)<M.
$$

Choose `\varepsilon` so small that `0<\varepsilon<2s` and
`\varepsilon<M/(4s)`. Then `2s+\varepsilon<4s`, hence

$$
\varepsilon(2s+\varepsilon)<4s\varepsilon<M.
$$

Therefore `r^2<2`, so `r\in S` and `r>s`. Thus `s` cannot be an upper bound of
`S`.
</CollapsibleProof>

### Case 2: `s^2>2`

Now `s` really is an upper bound, but it is too large to be the *least* upper
bound. The notes show that we can move slightly left while staying above `S`.

<CollapsibleProof title="Why s^2>2 cannot be the supremum">
Assume `s\in Q` and `s^2>2`. Take a small positive rational `\varepsilon` and
set `r=s-\varepsilon`.

We want `r^2>2` to remain true. Expanding gives

$$
r^2=s^2-2s\varepsilon+\varepsilon^2.
$$

So it is enough to choose `\varepsilon` small enough that

$$
2s\varepsilon-\varepsilon^2<s^2-2.
$$

Because `s^2-2>0`, this can be done with sufficiently small rational
`\varepsilon>0`. Then `r^2>2`, so by the previous bound argument `r` is still
an upper bound of `S`. But now `r<s`, which means `s` was not the least upper
bound after all.
</CollapsibleProof>

### Case 3: `s^2=2`

This is impossible in `Q`, because the previous chapter proved that `sqrt(2)` is
irrational.

Putting the three cases together:

- `s^2<2` means `s` is not even an upper bound;
- `s^2>2` means `s` is an upper bound, but not the least one;
- `s^2=2` cannot happen in `Q`.

So `S` has no supremum in `Q`.

## Density is not the same as completeness

It is tempting to object:

"But there are infinitely many rationals between any two rationals. Surely the
gap can be filled by approximations."

That objection confuses two ideas.

- **Density** says that between two distinct rationals, another rational can be
  found.
- **Completeness** says that every bounded nonempty set has the correct least
  upper bound and greatest lower bound inside the ambient ordered set.

The set `S` shows that you can have endless rational approximations to a missing
boundary and still fail completeness.

<CommonMistake title="Approximation does not equal possession">
`Q` contains rationals arbitrarily close to `sqrt(2)`. That does not mean `Q`
contains the least upper bound of `S`. A sequence of better and better
approximations is weaker than actually having the boundary point.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is 2 an upper bound of S={x in Q : x^2 < 2}?">
If x>2, what happens to x^2?
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
If `x>2`, then `x^2>4>2`, so such an `x` cannot belong to `S`. Therefore every
member of `S` lies at most `2`, so `2` is an upper bound.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the density of Q not imply completeness of Q?">
Answer in one careful sentence.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Density only says there are rationals between nearby rationals, while
completeness requires least upper bounds for all bounded nonempty subsets; the
set `S={x\in Q\mid x^2<2}` has arbitrarily good rational approximations to its
boundary but no rational supremum.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Explain why every finite ordered set is complete.">
Use the fact that nonempty finite subsets always attain their largest and
smallest elements.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
Take any nonempty subset `Y` of a finite ordered set `X`. Because `Y` is finite,
one can compare its elements and find a largest and smallest element. Those are
respectively the maximum and minimum of `Y`, so they also serve as supremum and
infimum. Hence every finite ordered set is complete.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Suppose Q were complete. What would that force for the set S={x in Q : x^2 < 2}, and why is that impossible?">
Connect the definition of completeness directly to the counterexample.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
If `Q` were complete, then every nonempty subset of `Q` with an upper bound
would have a supremum in `Q`. The set `S` is nonempty and bounded above, so it
would have some `s\in Q` with `s=\sup(S)`. But the three-case argument shows no
rational `s` can do that. So completeness of `Q` is impossible.
</RevealSolution>
