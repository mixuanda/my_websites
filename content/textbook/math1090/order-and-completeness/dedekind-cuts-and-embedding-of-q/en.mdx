## Why cuts are the next step

The previous note ended with a picture:

- a real number should determine everything in `Q` that lies to its left;
- it should also determine everything in `Q` that lies to its right;
- the real number itself should behave like the boundary between those two
  camps.

Section 4.8 of the lecture notes turns that picture into a formal definition.
The important move is this: instead of assuming the real number already exists
and then asking what lies below it, we *define* the real number by the left-hand
part of `Q` itself.

That is the basic idea of a **Dedekind cut**.

## Two equivalent definitions

<Definition title="Dedekind cut as a pair">
A **Dedekind cut** is a pair `(A,B)` of nonempty subsets of `Q` such that:

- `Q=A\cup B`;
- for every `a\in A` and `b\in B`, we have `a<b`;
- `A` has no maximum element.
</Definition>

In words, `A` is the entire left side of the boundary, and `B` is the entire
right side. There is no overlap, nothing is missing, and the left side does not
contain its own last point.

<Definition title="Dedekind cut as a single subset">
Equivalently, a subset `A\subset Q` is called a Dedekind cut if:

- `A\ne\varnothing` and `A\ne Q`;
- whenever `x\in A` and `y\in Q` satisfy `y<x`, then `y\in A`;
- for every `x\in A`, there exists `y\in A` with `x<y`.
</Definition>

The second form is often easier to use. Once the left set `A` is known, the
right set is automatically `Q\setminus A`. So the cut is completely determined
by which rationals count as "already to the left of the boundary".

## What each condition is doing

The definition is short, but every clause has a job.

- `A\ne\varnothing` and `A\ne Q` prevent a fake cut with no left side or no
  right side.
- Downward closure says that if a rational is already on the left, then every
  smaller rational must also be on the left.
- The "no maximum" condition says the boundary itself is not stored as the last
  element of `A`; there is always room to move a little farther right while
  staying on the left.

This last point is the subtle one. It is exactly what prevents one rational
number from being represented twice.

<WorkedExample title="The cut for `q=3/2`">
Let

$$
A=\{x\in Q\mid x<3/2\},
\qquad
B=\{x\in Q\mid x\ge 3/2\}.
$$

Then `A` and `B` are both nonempty, every element of `A` is smaller than every
element of `B`, and `A` has no maximum.

To see the last point, start with any `x<3/2` and define

$$
y=\frac{x+3/2}{2}.
$$

Then `x<y<3/2`, so `y\in A` and `x` was not maximal. Thus `(A,B)` is a
Dedekind cut. This cut is the rational number `3/2` viewed inside the real
number system built from cuts.
</WorkedExample>

## Rational cuts and the embedding of `Q`

The lecture notes call a cut `(A,B)` **rational** if `B` has a minimum
element. In that case the boundary is already achieved by a rational number.

If `\min(B)=q`, then necessarily

$$
A=\{x\in Q\mid x<q\}.
$$

That motivates the notation

$$
q_R=\{x\in Q\mid x<q\}.
$$

So each rational number `q` gives a Dedekind cut `q_R`, and the map

$$
i:Q\to R,\qquad i(q)=q_R
$$

embeds `Q` into the cut model of the reals.

<TheoremCard title="The rational numbers sit inside the cut model">
Rational Dedekind cuts are in bijection with rational numbers. After this
embedding is established, it is standard to identify `q` with its cut `q_R`.
In particular, `0_R` and `1_R` play the roles of `0` and `1` inside `R`.
</TheoremCard>

This matters conceptually. The cut construction is not trying to throw away the
rationals and start from scratch. It is enlarging `Q` by adding new boundaries
that were missing before.

<CommonMistake title="Using `x\le q` instead of `x<q`">
The set `\{x\in Q\mid x\le q\}` is nonempty, proper, and downward closed, but
it fails the "no maximum" condition because `q` itself is the largest element.
If we allowed that set as well as `q_R`, then the same rational number would be
represented in two different ways. The strict inequality is not cosmetic; it
removes that ambiguity.
</CommonMistake>

## Order and the first operations on cuts

Defining the set `R` of all cuts is not enough. To match the target from the
previous note, the lecture notes must also define order and arithmetic on `R`.

For order, the natural rule is:

$$
A\le A' \quad\text{if and only if}\quad A\subseteq A'.
$$

This is exactly the right comparison for left sets. If every rational already on
the left of `A` is also on the left of `A'`, then the boundary represented by
`A` cannot lie to the right of the boundary represented by `A'`.

For addition, the notes define

$$
A+A'=\{a+a' \mid a\in A,\ a'\in A'\}.
$$

The idea is that the left side of a sum should consist of rational numbers that
can already be reached by adding something strictly left of the first boundary
to something strictly left of the second boundary.

For multiplication, the notes first handle the case of nonnegative cuts and then
extend the definition by sign rules. When `0_R\le A` and `0_R\le A'`, they use

$$
A\cdot A'
=
\{aa' \mid a\in A,\ a'\in A',\ a\ge 0,\ a'\ge 0\}\cup\{x\in Q\mid x<0\}.
$$

This is more technical than addition, but the guiding idea is the same: build
the left side of the product from rational data that already lies to the left.

<WorkedExample title="Why `1_R+1_R=2_R`">
Write

$$
1_R=\{x\in Q\mid x<1\}.
$$

If `a<1` and `a'<1`, then `a+a'<2`, so every element of `1_R+1_R` lies in
`2_R`.

Conversely, if `x<2`, then `x/2<1`, and

$$
x=\frac{x}{2}+\frac{x}{2}.
$$

So every rational in `2_R` lies in `1_R+1_R`. Hence

$$
1_R+1_R=2_R.
$$

This example shows that the cut definition really extends the ordinary rational
operations rather than inventing new arithmetic.
</WorkedExample>

<TheoremCard title="The cut model reaches the target from 4.4">
The lecture notes define `+`, `\cdot`, and `\le` on Dedekind cuts and then show
that the resulting structure is a complete ordered field containing the embedded
copy of `Q`.
</TheoremCard>

The proofs are not short, but the overall message is clean: Dedekind cuts do
exactly what chapter 4 asked a model of the real numbers to do.

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="The pair version and the subset version are not two different theories">
They describe the same object from two angles. The pair `(A,B)` records both
sides of the boundary explicitly, while the subset form keeps only the left
side and recovers the right side as `Q\setminus A`.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Order on cuts is not a comparison between every element of two sets">
The statement `A\le A'` means `A\subseteq A'`. It does *not* mean that every
element of `A` is less than every element of `A'`. The two left sets usually
overlap heavily, especially when one boundary lies to the left of the other.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="What is the cut `0_R`?">
Write it directly from the definition `q_R=\{x\in Q\mid x<q\}`.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">

$$
0_R=\{x\in Q\mid x<0\},
$$

so it is the set of all negative rational numbers.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="If `A\subseteq A'`, which boundary lies to the left?">
Answer in terms of the cut order.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
If `A\subseteq A'`, then `A\le A'`. The boundary represented by `A` lies at or
to the left of the boundary represented by `A'`.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Show that `q_R=\{x\in Q\mid x<q\}` is a Dedekind cut for every rational number `q`.">
Check the three conditions in the subset definition.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The set `q_R` is nonempty because `q-1<q`, so `q-1\in q_R`. It is not all of
`Q` because `q\notin q_R`.

It is downward closed: if `x<q` and `y<x`, then certainly `y<q`, so `y\in q_R`.

It has no maximum: if `x<q`, then

$$
\frac{x+q}{2}
$$

is rational and satisfies `x<(x+q)/2<q`, so `(x+q)/2\in q_R`. Therefore every
element of `q_R` can be improved by a larger element still in `q_R`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why is `C=\{x\in Q\mid x\le 2\}` not a Dedekind cut, even though it is nonempty, proper, and downward closed?">
Identify the exact condition that fails and explain why it matters.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The set `C` fails the no-maximum condition because `2` is its largest element.
That failure matters because `\{x<2\}` already represents the rational number
`2` as a cut. If `\{x\le 2\}` were also allowed, the same rational boundary
would receive two different names.
</RevealSolution>
