Induction is not a magic trick. It is a proof pattern for statements that are
meant to hold for every natural number.

The same chapter also uses recursive definitions for addition and
multiplication, so it helps to keep the base case and the step visible all the
time.

## Recursive addition

<Definition title="Recursive definition of addition">
For natural numbers `a` and `b`, addition is defined by:

- `a + 0 = a`
- `a + S(b) = S(a + b)`
</Definition>

This is a recursive definition. You know the result when the second input is
`0`, and every later value is built from an earlier one.

<WorkedExample title="Compute `2 + 3` from the definition">
Write `2 = S(S(0))` and `3 = S(S(S(0)))`.

Then:

`2 + 3 = 2 + S(S(S(0)))`

`= S(2 + S(S(0)))`

`= S(S(2 + S(0)))`

`= S(S(S(2 + 0)))`

`= S(S(S(2)))`.

That is the number usually called `5`.
</WorkedExample>

<CommonMistake title="A recursive formula is not yet a proof">
The formulas tell you how the operation is defined. They do not automatically
prove a claim about all natural numbers. For that, you still need induction.
</CommonMistake>

## Recursive multiplication

Multiplication is introduced in the same recursive style. Once addition is
available, multiplication can be read as repeated addition controlled by the
second input.

<Definition title="Recursive definition of multiplication">
For natural numbers `a` and `b`, multiplication is defined by:

- `a · 0 = 0`
- `a · S(b) = (a · b) + a`
</Definition>

The base case says that adding `a` zero times gives `0`. The step says that if
you already know `a · b`, then multiplying by the successor `S(b)` adds one
more copy of `a`.

<WorkedExample title="Compute `3 · 2` from the definition">
Write `2 = S(S(0))`. Then

$$
3\cdot 2
=3\cdot S(S(0))
=(3\cdot S(0))+3
$$

and

$$
3\cdot S(0)=(3\cdot 0)+3=0+3=3.
$$

Therefore

$$
3\cdot 2=3+3=6.
$$

The familiar interpretation “three, taken two times” is recovered from the
recursive rule.
</WorkedExample>

## What induction proves

The induction principle is the reason recursive definitions can support
ordinary algebraic laws. A typical proof has the following shape.

<TheoremCard title="Induction principle">
Let `P(n)` be a statement about a natural number `n`. If:

1. `P(0)` is true, and
2. whenever `P(n)` is true, `P(S(n))` is also true,

then `P(n)` is true for every `n\in N`.
</TheoremCard>

The base case attaches the statement to the starting point `0`. The induction
step proves that truth is preserved when we move one successor forward. The two
parts together rule out the possibility that the statement holds at first but
then fails later.

## A proof sketch

<CollapsibleProof title="Why `S(a) + b = S(a + b)` follows by induction on `b`">
We prove the statement for all `b\in N`.

Base case: when `b = 0`,

`S(a) + 0 = S(a) = S(a + 0)`.

Induction step: assume `S(a) + b = S(a + b)`. Then

`S(a) + S(b) = S(S(a) + b)` by the recursive rule,

`= S(S(a + b))` by the induction hypothesis,

`= S(a + S(b))` by the recursive rule again.

So the statement holds for `S(b)` whenever it holds for `b`.
</CollapsibleProof>

<CollapsibleProof title="Why `a · S(b) = a · b + a` is not a theorem but a definition">
For multiplication, the formula

$$
a\cdot S(b)=(a\cdot b)+a
$$

is not something proved after multiplication is already known. It is the
recursive step that defines multiplication. Later algebraic laws, such as
distributivity, are the statements that require induction.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Algebraic laws come after the definitions

Once addition and multiplication have been defined recursively, the familiar
laws of arithmetic become theorems.

<TheoremCard title="Examples of arithmetic laws proved by induction">
For natural numbers `a`, `b`, and `c`, one proves:

$$
a+b=b+a,
$$

$$
a\cdot(b+c)=a\cdot b+a\cdot c,
$$

and

$$
a\cdot b=b\cdot a.
$$
</TheoremCard>

The important point is the order of logic: we first define the operations, then
prove that they behave like the operations we already know.

<CollapsibleProof title="Proof structure for distributivity">
Fix `a` and `b`, and prove the statement

$$
P(c):\quad a\cdot(b+c)=a\cdot b+a\cdot c
$$

by induction on `c`.

For `c=0`, use `b+0=b`, `a\cdot 0=0`, and the additive identity.

For the step, assume `P(c)`. Then use the recursive rules

$$
b+S(c)=S(b+c)
$$

and

$$
a\cdot S(d)=a\cdot d+a
$$

to rewrite the left side until the induction hypothesis applies. The remaining
terms are rearranged using the associativity of addition, which is itself one
of the earlier induction theorems.
</CollapsibleProof>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="What is the base case in the recursive definition of addition?">
Think about which input is fixed first.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The base case is `a + 0 = a`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What is the base case in the recursive definition of multiplication?">
Look at the second input.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The base case is `a · 0 = 0`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Using the recursive rule, what is `a · S(b)`?">
State the next multiplication value in terms of the previous one.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It is `(a · b) + a`.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="In an induction proof, what is the induction hypothesis?">
Use one short sentence.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It is the assumption that the claim holds for a fixed natural number `n`, before proving it for `S(n)`.
</RevealSolution>
