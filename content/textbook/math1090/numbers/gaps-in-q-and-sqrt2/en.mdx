title: "3.5 Gaps in Q and sqrt(2)"
description: "See why Q still has holes by looking at the irrational number sqrt(2) and the set of rationals below it."
---

The rational numbers are dense, but density is not the same thing as
completeness. The source notes use `sqrt(2)` to show that Q still has holes.

## What the gap means

<Definition title="Irrational number">
An irrational number is a real number that is not rational.
</Definition>

The notes define a classic set:

`S = {x in Q | x^2 < 2}`.

Every rational with square less than `2` belongs to `S`, and every rational
with square bigger than `2` sits above it. The missing point is the square
root of `2`.

## A familiar contradiction

<CollapsibleProof title="Why no rational number squares to 2">
Assume `x in Q` and `x^2 = 2`. Write `x = p/q` in lowest terms with
`p, q in Z` and `q != 0`.

Then `p^2 = 2q^2`, so `p^2` is even. The notes prove that this forces `p` to be
even. Write `p = 2k`.

Substitute again:

`4k^2 = 2q^2`, so `q^2` is even, and then `q` is even too.

That contradicts the choice of `p/q` in lowest terms. So there is no rational
solution to `x^2 = 2`.
</CollapsibleProof>

<CommonMistake title="Dense does not mean complete">
Even though there is always another rational between two rationals, a bounded
set in Q can still fail to have a supremum in Q.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Does the set `S = {x in Q | x^2 < 2}` have a supremum in Q?">
Use the contradiction above and the source notes on completeness.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. The notes show that `S` is bounded above in Q, but no rational supremum
exists there.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Is `sqrt(2)` rational?">
Say yes or no and give the reason in one phrase.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
No. The proof above shows that a rational square root of `2` would lead to a
contradiction.
</RevealSolution>
