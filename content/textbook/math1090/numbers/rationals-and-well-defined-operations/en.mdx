title: "3.4 Rationals and well-defined operations"
description: "Define rational numbers as equivalence classes and check that the usual formulas do not depend on the representative you pick."
---

Integers were built from pairs of natural numbers. Rationals are built in the
same spirit, except now the second entry is not allowed to be `0`.

## The quotient model

<Definition title="Rational numbers">
Let

`Y = Z × (Z \ {0}) = {(a, b) | a in Z, b != 0}`.

Define `(a, b) ~_Q (c, d)` if `a d = b c`.

The rational numbers are the equivalence classes `Y / ~_Q`.
</Definition>

You can think of `[(a, b)]` as the fraction `a/b`. The point of the quotient
construction is that equal fractions become equal by definition.

## Why well-definedness matters

If you write a formula on representatives, you still have to check that the
answer is the same for every representative of the same rational number.
That check is what "well-defined" means here.

<WorkedExample title="Addition still matches the fraction intuition">
The source notes define

`[(a, b)] + [(c, d)] := [(ad + bc, bd)]`.

For example,

`[(1, 2)] + [(1, 3)] = [(5, 6)]`.

If you switch the first representative to `(2, 4)`, the formula gives
`[(10, 12)]`, which is the same rational number as `[(5, 6)]`.
</WorkedExample>

<CollapsibleProof title="Why the addition formula is well-defined">
Suppose `(a, b) ~_Q (a', b')` and `(c, d) ~_Q (c', d')`.
That means `a b' = b a'` and `c d' = d c'`.

The source proof multiplies the right identities together and rearranges the
terms to show

`(ad + bc, bd) ~_Q (a'd' + b'c', b'd')`.

So the sum does not depend on which representatives you started with.
</CollapsibleProof>

<CommonMistake title="Do not compare fractions using a formula that ignores signs">
Worksheet 5 asks about relations such as `p/q R m/n` if `pn - qm > 0`.
That kind of comparison is not safe on arbitrary representatives, because
changing the sign of a representative can change the sign of the test.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Which of these relations from Worksheet 5 is well defined on Q as written: `p/q R m/n` if `p > m`, if `pn - qm > 0`, or if `(pn - mq)nq > 0`?">
Think about what happens if you replace a representative by an equivalent one.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Only the third one is well defined as written. The first two depend on the chosen representatives.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does `Y` exclude pairs with denominator `0`?">
Give the mathematical reason, not the everyday one.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
Because the second coordinate is meant to play the role of a denominator, and the construction must avoid division by zero.
</RevealSolution>
