title: "3.1 Natural numbers and Peano's axioms"
description: "Move from the informal counting picture to a formal description of the natural numbers through zero, successor, and induction."
---

At first sight, the natural numbers seem too familiar to need a definition. We
count with them from childhood, and it is tempting to think that the list

$$
0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots
$$

already says everything important.

A rigorous treatment asks what structure actually
makes the natural numbers behave the way they do. The answer is not the shape of
the symbols, but the presence of a distinguished starting point, a successor
operation, and an induction principle.

## Why a formal definition is needed

If we describe the natural numbers only by writing `0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots`, then we
have not really explained what the dots mean, why the process continues, or why
induction works.

The Peano viewpoint solves that problem by specifying the essential properties
directly. It tells us what must be true in any model of the natural numbers,
without depending on intuition alone.

## The data of a model

<Definition title="A model of the natural numbers">
Suppose `N` is a set equipped with:

- a distinguished element `0 \in N`;
- a function `S : N \to N`, called the **successor map**.

The triple `(N, 0, S)` is called a **model of the natural numbers** if it
satisfies the Peano axioms:

1. `S` is injective: if `S(x) = S(y)`, then `x = y`.
2. No element is its own successor: `S(x) \ne x` for every `x \in N`.
3. Zero is not a successor: there is no `x \in N` with `S(x) = 0`.
4. Induction holds: if a predicate `P` is true of `0`, and whenever `P(x)` is
   true it follows that `P(S(x))` is true, then `P` is true for every `x \in N`.
</Definition>

The central idea is that the natural numbers are characterized by how they are
connected, not by how they are written.

## What each axiom is doing

Each axiom rules out a specific kind of pathology.

- Injectivity says different numbers cannot suddenly merge after one successor
  step.
- `S(x) \ne x` rules out fixed points.
- `S(x) \ne 0` says zero is the starting point, not something reached later.
- Induction rules out disconnected extra pieces and ensures that every element
  lies in the chain generated from `0`.

Taken together, these axioms force the familiar picture of counting forward one
step at a time.

## Reading numbers through successor

<WorkedExample title="How the usual numerals arise from `0` and `S`">
Once `0` and the successor map are fixed, the next numbers are interpreted as

$$
1 = S(0), \qquad
2 = S(S(0)), \qquad
3 = S(S(S(0))),
$$

and so on.

So the notation `2` is shorthand for "the element obtained by applying the
successor map twice to `0`." The notation is convenient, but the structure comes
first.
</WorkedExample>

This is why successor notation remains useful when we want the
definition to remain visible instead of being hidden behind familiar symbols.

## Induction is not an extra trick

Students often meet induction as a proof technique after they already believe
the natural numbers are understood. A construction-first perspective reverses that order.

The induction principle is part of the definition of what the natural numbers
are. In that sense, induction is not merely a useful method for proving
statements about `N`; it is one of the structural facts that makes `N` the
natural numbers in the first place.

<TheoremCard title="What induction gives you">
To prove a statement `P(n)` for all `n \in N`, it is enough to show:

1. `P(0)` is true.
2. For every `x \in N`, if `P(x)` is true, then `P(S(x))` is true.

Once these two facts are established, induction implies that `P(n)` holds for
every natural number `n`.
</TheoremCard>

## A model that fails

<WorkedExample title="Why a finite cycle is not a model of the natural numbers">
Consider the set `\{0,1,2\}` with successor map

$$
S(0)=1, \qquad S(1)=2, \qquad S(2)=0.
$$

This structure does not satisfy the Peano axioms.

First, `0` is a successor because `S(2)=0`, so axiom 3 fails.
This is already enough to reject the structure as a model of the natural
numbers. The example should not be read as an induction-axiom failure: from
`0`, repeated successors still visit the whole finite set. The problem is that
the successor map loops back and makes `0` a successor.

So although the symbols look familiar, this structure is not a model of the
natural numbers.
</WorkedExample>

This example is important because it shows why the Peano axioms are not
ornamental. They exclude structures that resemble counting in superficial ways
but do not behave like `N`.

## Optional model: von Neumann natural numbers

The Peano axioms describe what the natural numbers must do, but they do not
force us to use any particular internal representation. One standard set-theoretic
model is the **von Neumann construction**:

$$
0:=\varnothing,\qquad
1:=\{0\},\qquad
2:=\{0,1\},\qquad
3:=\{0,1,2\}.
$$

In general,

$$
S(n)=n\cup\{n\}.
$$

So each natural number is the set of all earlier natural numbers. In this model,
membership mirrors order: `m\in n` exactly when `m<n`.

<WorkedExample title="Why `2` becomes `{0,1}`">
Starting with `0=\varnothing`, the successor rule gives

$$
1=S(0)=0\cup\{0\}=\{0\},
$$

and then

$$
2=S(1)=1\cup\{1\}=\{0,1\}.
$$

This does not mean the everyday numeral `2` has changed its meaning. It means
we have built a concrete set-theoretic representative that satisfies the same
successor pattern.
</WorkedExample>

## Why the axioms are more informative than the dots

The expression

$$
0,1,2,3,\ldots
$$

is useful notation, but by itself it does not explain why arithmetic and
induction work.

The Peano description does. It tells us:

- where counting begins;
- how one step forward is represented;
- why there are no loops;
- why proofs by induction reach every natural number.

That is why the formal definition is more revealing than the informal list.

## Common mistakes

<CommonMistake title="Do not confuse the symbol with the structural role">
The Peano viewpoint does not say that the written mark `2` has some built-in
meaning. It says that the object denoted by `2` is the second successor of `0`.
</CommonMistake>

<CommonMistake title="Induction is not optional decoration">
Without the induction axiom, a structure can contain a familiar successor chain
starting from `0` and still have extra disconnected elements or loops. The
induction principle rules those out.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Why does the axiom `S(x) \ne 0` matter?">
Explain what would go wrong if `0` were allowed to be a successor.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
If `0` were a successor, then the counting chain could loop back on itself
instead of having a genuine starting point. The structure would no longer match
the one-way progression we expect from the natural numbers.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What does injectivity of the successor map prevent?">
Answer in terms of two different numbers trying to behave like the same next
number.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
It prevents two different elements from having the same successor. Without
injectivity, distinct numbers could collapse into a single next step, which
would destroy the usual linear counting structure.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="After proving a base case and an induction step, what exactly may you conclude?">
State the conclusion carefully.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
You may conclude that the predicate holds for every element of `N`, not merely
for the first few examples that you checked by hand.
</RevealSolution>

## Exercises

<QuickCheck prompt="Show that the structure `\{0,1,2\}` with successor map `S(0)=1`, `S(1)=2`, `S(2)=2` is not a model of the natural numbers. Which axiom fails?">
Check the axioms one by one instead of guessing.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Guided solution">
The key failure is axiom 2, which requires `S(x) \ne x` for every `x \in N`.
Here we have

$$
S(2)=2,
$$

so `2` is its own successor. That already prevents the structure from being a
Peano model.

You can also observe that such a fixed point is incompatible with the intended
"always move forward" interpretation of successor.
</RevealSolution>
