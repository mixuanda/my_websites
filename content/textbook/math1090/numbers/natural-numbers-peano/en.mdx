---
title: "3.1 Natural numbers and Peano's axioms"
description: "Start from counting, then see how Peano's axioms pin down the natural numbers."
---

We usually meet natural numbers as counting numbers. The source notes take a
stricter route: they describe them by a small list of axioms instead of by
examples alone.

## A model of the naturals

<Definition title="A model of the natural numbers">
Suppose `N` is a set with a distinguished element `0` and a function
`S : N -> N`.

The notes call `(N, 0, S)` a model of the natural numbers if:

- `S` is injective.
- `S(x) != x` for every `x in N`.
- There is no `x in N` such that `S(x) = 0`.
- Induction holds: if a predicate is true for `0`, and it passes from `x` to
  `S(x)`, then it is true for all `x in N`.
</Definition>

The point is not that the symbol `0` magically looks like zero. The point is
that the structure tells you how to start and how to move forward.

## Successor comes first

<WorkedExample title="Reading the successor chain">
If `0` is the starting point, then `S(0)` plays the role of `1`,
`S(S(0))` plays the role of `2`, and so on.

That is why the notes sometimes write `2` as `S(S(0))` when they want the
definition to stay visible.
</WorkedExample>

<CommonMistake title="Do not treat the symbol and the role as the same thing">
The Peano setup does not say that the written symbol `2` has some intrinsic
meaning. It says that `2` is whatever object you reach after applying the
successor map twice to `0`.
</CommonMistake>

## Quick checks

<QuickCheck prompt="Which Peano axiom fails for the cycle `0 -> 1 -> 2 -> 0`?">
Think about injectivity, fixed points, and whether `0` is ever a successor.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
The cycle breaks several axioms. `S(2) = 0`, so `0` is a successor, and the
loop also makes induction fail. The source exercise asks you to identify the
failed axioms.
</RevealSolution>

<QuickCheck prompt="What does the induction axiom let you conclude once the base case and successor step are proved?">
Say the answer in one sentence.
</QuickCheck>

<RevealSolution title="Answer">
You can conclude that the predicate holds for every natural number in `N`.
</RevealSolution>
