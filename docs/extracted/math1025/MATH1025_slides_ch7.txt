Well-ordering Principle
Theorem 7.2 (Well-ordering Principle)
LetS be a subset ofN ={0, 1, 2,... }. SupposeS̸=∅. ThenS has a least
element, i.e. there existsm∈S such thatm≤s for alls∈S.
We will show that the Well-ordering Principle is a consequence of the
Principle of Mathematical Induction.
Proof.
Let S be a subset ofN with no least element.
Forn∈ N, denote byP (n) the statement:n̸∈S.
Clearly,P (0) is true since0 would be the least element ofS otherwise.
Suppose P (k) is true for all integersk = 0, 1,...,n .
Now ifn + 1∈S, thenn + 1 would be the least element ofS since
0, 1,...,n ̸∈S.
So n + 1̸∈S and P (n + 1) is true.
By strong induction,P (n) is true for alln∈ N, i.e.S =∅.

Theorem 7.7 (Division Algorithm)
Leta,b∈ Z with b̸= 0. Then, there exist unique integersq and r such that

Let r =a−bq. Thenr≥ 0 and r−b< 0, i.e. 0≤r<b .

Euclid’s Lemma
Proposition 7.14 (Euclid’s Lemma)
Leta,b∈ Z and letp be a prime. Ifp|ab, thenp|a or p|b.
Proof.
If p|a, done.
If p ∤a, then gcd(a,p ) = 1 and am +pn = 1 for somem,n∈ Z.
So abm +bpn =b.
Since p|ab, ab =pq for someq∈ Z.
Thusb =pqm +bpn =p(qm +bn), whereqm +bn∈ Z.
Therefore p|b.
Corollary 7.15 (Corollary to Euclid’s lemma)
Letp be a prime number. Letn∈ Z+\{1} and leta1,a 2,...,a n∈ Z.

Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic
Theorem 7.16 (Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic)
Every positive integer> 1 can be expressed as a product of prime(s) in a
unique way. (Primes are “building blocks” of positive integers> 1.)
Proof of existence part.
Let
S ={n∈ Z+\{1} :n cannot be expressed as a product of prime(s)}⊆ Z+.
Suppose S is nonempty.
Then S has a least elementm by the Well-ordering Principle.
Since m cannot be a prime, we have
m =n1n2 where n1,n 2∈ Z with 1<n 1,n 2 <m .
However, sincem is already the least element ofS, we haven1,n 2̸∈S, so
both can be expressed as a product of prime(s).
Hence m =n1n2 can also be expressed as a product of prime(s).
This is a contradiction and soS =∅.
